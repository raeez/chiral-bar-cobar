\chapter{Geometric dictionary}

This dictionary should be read as a Rosetta Stone between three languages:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Physical}: the language of conformal field theory and operator products
\item \emph{Algebraic}: the language of operads and homological algebra
\item \emph{Geometric}: the language of configuration spaces and residues
\end{itemize}
Each entry represents a precise mathematical correspondence, not merely an analogy.

\begin{remark}[Reading the dictionary]
Each entry represents a precise functor or natural transformation between categories. For instance, the correspondence ``Chiral multiplication $\leftrightarrow$ Residues at collision divisors'' is the content of Theorem \ref{thm:residue-formula}, establishing that the multiplication map factors through the residue homomorphism. \end{remark}


 
\chapter{Sign conventions}
 
We collect our sign conventions for reference:
\begin{itemize}
\item Logarithmic forms: $\eta_{ij} = d\log(z_i - z_j) = \frac{dz_i - dz_j}{z_i - z_j}$
\item Transposition: $\eta_{ji} = \eta_{ij}$ as forms (since $d\log(-1) = 0$; the antisymmetry $e_{ji} = -e_{ij}$ is an Orlik--Solomon algebra convention, not a property of the forms)
\item Residues: $\text{Res}_{z_i=z_j}[\eta_{ij}] = 1$
\item Fermionic permutation: $\psi_i\psi_j = -\psi_j\psi_i$
\item Koszul sign rule: Moving degree $p$ past degree $q$ introduces $(-1)^{pq}$
\item Differential grading: $\deg(d) = 1$, $\deg(\eta_{ij}) = 1$
\item Suspension: $s$ has degree $1$, desuspension $s^{-1}$ has degree $-1$
\end{itemize}

\chapter{\texorpdfstring{Arnold relations for small $n$}{Arnold relations for small n}}
 
Complete list of Arnold relations for logarithmic forms:
 
\emph{$n = 3$.}
\[
\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23} + \eta_{23} \wedge \eta_{31} + \eta_{31} \wedge \eta_{12} = 0
\]

\emph{$n = 4$ (four 3-term Arnold relations, one for each triple $\{i,j,k\} \subset \{1,2,3,4\}$).}
\begin{align*}
\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23} + \eta_{23} \wedge \eta_{31} + \eta_{31} \wedge \eta_{12} &= 0 \\
\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{24} + \eta_{24} \wedge \eta_{41} + \eta_{41} \wedge \eta_{12} &= 0 \\
\eta_{13} \wedge \eta_{34} + \eta_{34} \wedge \eta_{41} + \eta_{41} \wedge \eta_{13} &= 0 \\
\eta_{23} \wedge \eta_{34} + \eta_{34} \wedge \eta_{42} + \eta_{42} \wedge \eta_{23} &= 0
\end{align*}
These four relations (each involving forms that share an index) reduce $\binom{6}{2} = 15$ naive products to $\dim H^2(C_4(\mathbb{C})) = 11$ independent classes.  Products of disjoint pairs such as $\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{34}$ are \emph{not} constrained by Arnold relations and remain independent.
 
\emph{$n = 5$ ($\binom{5}{3} = 10$ independent quadratic relations).}
These are the ten three-term Arnold relations $\eta_{ij} \wedge \eta_{jk} + \eta_{jk} \wedge \eta_{ki} + \eta_{ki} \wedge \eta_{ij} = 0$, one for each triple $\{i,j,k\} \subset \{1,2,3,4,5\}$.  All higher-degree relations (such as the degree-3 relations in $H^3(C_5(\mathbb{C}))$) are consequences of these quadratic relations, by the Orlik--Solomon theorem.
 
\emph{General $n$.} The cohomology ring $H^*(C_n(\mathbb{C}))$ is the quotient of the
exterior algebra $\bigwedge^* \mathbb{C}^{\binom{n}{2}}$ (generated by classes $\eta_{ij}$ in degree~1)
by the ideal generated by $\binom{n}{3}$ quadratic Arnold relations (one for each triple $i < j < k$).
The Poincar\'{e} polynomial is $\prod_{i=0}^{n-1}(1 + it)$, so
$\dim H^k(C_n(\mathbb{C})) = |s(n, n-k)|$ (unsigned Stirling numbers of the first kind)
and the total Betti number is $n!$.

\chapter{\texorpdfstring{Curved $A_\infty$ relations: complete formulas}{Curved A infty relations: complete formulas}}
\label{app:curved-ainfty-formulas}

For reference, we collect the complete curved $A_\infty$ relations. An $A_\infty$ algebra 
$(\mathcal{A}, \{m_k\}_{k \geq 0}, \mu_0)$ satisfies:

\emph{$n = 0$.} (Curvature is a cycle.)
$$m_1(\mu_0) = 0$$

\emph{$n = 1$.} (Failure of strict nilpotence.)
$$m_1^2 = m_2(\mu_0 \otimes \text{id}) - m_2(\text{id} \otimes \mu_0)$$

\emph{$n = 2$.} (Associativity with curvature corrections.)
\begin{multline}
m_1 m_2 - m_2(m_1 \otimes \text{id}) - m_2(\text{id} \otimes m_1) + m_3(\mu_0 \otimes \text{id} 
\otimes \text{id}) \\
- m_3(\text{id} \otimes \mu_0 \otimes \text{id}) + m_3(\text{id} \otimes \text{id} \otimes \mu_0) = 0
\end{multline}

\emph{$n = 3$.} (Higher coherences.)
$$\sum_{\substack{i+j+\ell = 3 \\ j \geq 0}} (-1)^{ij+\ell} m_{i+1+\ell}(\text{id}^{\otimes i}
\otimes m_j \otimes \text{id}^{\otimes \ell}) = 0$$

\emph{General formula.}
$$\sum_{\substack{i+j+\ell=n \\ i,\ell \geq 0, j \geq 0}} (-1)^{ij+\ell} m_{i+1+\ell}
(\text{id}^{\otimes i} \otimes m_j \otimes \text{id}^{\otimes \ell}) = 0$$

\emph{Special case --- Central curvature.}
If $\mu_0 \in Z(\mathcal{A})$ (i.e., $m_2(\mu_0 \otimes a) = m_2(a \otimes \mu_0)$ for all $a$), then the $n=1$ relation gives:
$$m_1^2(a) = m_2(\mu_0, a) - m_2(a, \mu_0) = 0$$
That is, when the curvature element is central, $m_1$ is strictly nilpotent ($m_1^2 = 0$). This is exactly the algebraic condition ensuring $d_{\text{bar}}^2 = 0$ in the bar complex (see the nine-term proof in \S\ref{sec:bar-nilpotency}).
