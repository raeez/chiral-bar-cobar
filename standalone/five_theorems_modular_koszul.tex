\begin{abstract}
Koszul duality over a point terminates: a quadratic algebra
determines a dual coalgebra, the bar construction mediates
between them, and no further structure appears. Over an
algebraic curve, the theory does not terminate. The bar
differential acquires curvature from the Hodge bundle on
moduli space; its failure to square to zero is controlled
by a single scalar $\kappa(\cA)$, the modular
characteristic, extracted from the collision residue of the
universal Maurer--Cartan element.

We prove five structural theorems for the bar complex of
a chirally Koszul chiral algebra $\cA$ on a smooth
algebraic curve $X$.
Theorem~A constructs the bar-cobar adjunction and its
Verdier intertwining, producing from the bar coalgebra
$\barB_X(\cA) = T^c(s^{-1}\Abar)$ the homotopy Koszul
dual $\cA^!_\infty$.
Theorem~B proves bar-cobar inversion
$\Omega(\barB(\cA)) \xrightarrow{\sim} \cA$ on the
Koszul locus: unconditionally at genus $0$ and on the
standard landscape at all genera.
Theorem~C decomposes the genus-$g$ ambient complex into
Verdier-Lagrangian complements
$Q_g(\cA) \oplus Q_g(\cA^!) \cong
H^*(\Mbar_g, \cZ_\cA)$, with the Koszul complementarity
sum $\kappa(\cA) + \kappa(\cA^!)$ family-dependent: $0$ for
Kac--Moody and free fields, $13$ for Virasoro.
Theorem~D identifies the genus expansion: the genus-$1$
formula $\obs_1(\cA) = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \lambda_1$ is
unconditional for every family. On the uniform-weight lane,
the all-genera extension
$\obs_g(\cA) = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \lambda_g$
\textup{(UNIFORM-WEIGHT)} holds via clutching morphisms
on $\partial\Mbar_g$, with generating function the
$\Ahat$-genus; the clutching-uniqueness step is the
principal remaining conditional element (an independent
GRR derivation provides an alternative path,
\S\ref{subsec:thmD-proof}).
Theorem~H proves that chiral Hochschild cohomology is
concentrated in degrees $\{0,1,2\}$ with a polynomial
Hilbert series controlled by the centre and its dual.

All five theorems are $\Sigma_n$-coinvariant projections
of a single $\Eone$ object: the ordered bar complex
$\barB^{\mathrm{ord}}_X(\cA)$ with its deconcatenation
coproduct and classical $R$-matrix
$r_\cA(z)$, for which
$\av(r_\cA(z)) = \kappa(\cA)$ for abelian
algebras; for non-abelian Kac--Moody,
$\kappa = \av(r(z)) + \dim(\fg)/2$ after the Sugawara
shift.
The Heisenberg algebra provides the complete base
computation; every other family is a variation with
increasingly complex collision residues.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: from a point to a curve}
\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{The problem}

Koszul duality over a point terminates. A quadratic algebra
$A = T(V)/(R)$ determines a dual coalgebra
$A^{!} = (H^*(B(A)))^\vee$, the bar construction
$B(A) = (T^c(s^{-1}\bar{A}), d_B)$ mediates between them, and the
cobar functor inverts:
$\Omega(B(A)) \xrightarrow{\sim} A$ when $A$ is Koszul
\cite{Priddy70, BGS96, LV12}. The bar cohomology concentrates in
degree $1$, the derived categories of $A$-modules and
$A^!$-modules are equivalent, and no further structure appears.

Over an algebraic curve, three obstructions prevent this from
working. The generators of a chiral algebra are sections of a
$\cD_X$-module, not elements of a vector space. The relations are
operator product expansions with poles of arbitrary order, not
quadratic expressions. The topology of higher-genus curves forces
the fiberwise bar differential to satisfy
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_g$ rather than
$d^2 = 0$, where $\omega_g = c_1(\mathbb{E})$ is the Hodge class
on $\Mbar_g$.

The question is: what structure replaces the terminating classical
theory?

\subsection{The answer}

The ordered bar complex
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bar-complex-def}
\barB^{\mathrm{ord}}_X(\cA)
\;=\;
T^c(s^{-1}\Abar),
\qquad
|s^{-1}v| = |v| - 1,
\end{equation}
with deconcatenation coproduct
\begin{equation}\label{eq:deconcatenation}
\Delta[s^{-1}a_1 | \cdots | s^{-1}a_n]
\;=\;
\sum_{i=0}^{n}
[s^{-1}a_1 | \cdots | s^{-1}a_i]
\otimes
[s^{-1}a_{i+1} | \cdots | s^{-1}a_n],
\end{equation}
is a cofree $\Eone$-coalgebra. Here
$\Abar = \ker(\varepsilon \colon \cA \to \omega_X)$ is the
augmentation ideal and $s^{-1}$ is the desuspension, lowering
degree by $1$. The integral kernel is the logarithmic $1$-form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eta}
\eta_{12} \;=\; d\log(z_1 - z_2)
\;=\; \frac{dz_1 - dz_2}{z_1 - z_2}\,,
\end{equation}
with conformally invariant residue $1$: no coordinate, no metric,
no level. At three points, the Arnold relation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:arnold}
\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23}
+ \eta_{23} \wedge \eta_{31}
+ \eta_{31} \wedge \eta_{12}
\;=\; 0
\end{equation}
generates all relations in
$H^*(\Conf_n(\mathbf{C}), \mathbf{C})$. The bar
differential squares to zero if and only if the $n$-th products
satisfy the Borcherds identity; the Arnold relation kills the
differential form, the Borcherds identity kills the algebra, and
both are consumed simultaneously. This is the \emph{selection
principle}: configuration space geometry determines the algebraic
axioms; the bar construction accepts exactly chiral algebras.

The binary collision residue of the bar differential is the
classical $R$-matrix
\[
r_\cA(z) \;=\;
\Res^{\mathrm{coll}}_{0,2}(\Theta_\cA)
\;\in\; \operatorname{End}_\cA(2)[\![z^{-1}]\!],
\]
where $\Theta_\cA := D_\cA - d_0$ is the Maurer--Cartan element
of the bar differential, satisfying
$d\Theta_\cA + \tfrac{1}{2}[\Theta_\cA, \Theta_\cA] = 0$
automatically because $(D_\cA)^2 = 0$. The
$\Sigma_n$-coinvariant quotient erases the matrix structure and
retains only the scalar
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kappa-def}
\kappa(\cA) \;=\; \av(r_\cA(z))
\quad \text{for abelian and scalar families,}
\end{equation}
while for non-abelian affine Kac--Moody
\[
\kappa(V_k(\fg)) \;=\; \av(r_\cA(z)) + \dim(\fg)/2.
\]
the modular characteristic: the single number that survives
averaging. Five structural theorems extract the
$\Sigma_n$-invariant content of the ordered bar. Each theorem
characterizes one facet of $\Theta_\cA$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\Alph*)}]
\item \emph{Arena}: $\Theta_\cA$ exists (bar-cobar adjunction
 and Verdier intertwining).
\item \emph{Faithfulness}: $\Theta_\cA$ determines $\cA$
 (bar-cobar inversion on the Koszul locus).
\item \emph{Decomposition}: $\Theta_\cA$ splits
 (Lagrangian complementarity under Verdier involution).
\item \emph{Leading coefficient}: $\Theta_\cA$ has universal
 scalar projection
 ($\obs_1 = \kappa \cdot \lambda_1$ unconditional;
 $\obs_g = \kappa \cdot \lambda_g$ at all genera on the
 uniform-weight lane via clutching/GRR).
\item \emph{Coefficient ring}: $\Theta_\cA$ has polynomial
 coefficients ($\ChirHoch^*$ concentrated in $\{0,1,2\}$).
\end{enumerate}

The chain is forced: without~A, there is no arena for the dual;
without~B, the bar transform can lose the boundary algebra;
without~C, higher-genus deformation theory has no canonical
polarization; without~D, the genus tower has no distinguished
scalar shadow; without~H, the coefficient ring is uncontrolled.

\subsection{Prior work}

Five existing programmes address aspects of the problem.
Beilinson--Drinfeld \cite{BD04} construct factorization algebras on
$\Ran(X)$ and prove chiral homology is well-defined, but do not
treat bar-cobar inversion, complementarity, or the genus tower.
Francis--Gaitsgory \cite{FG12} establish chiral Koszul duality at
genus $0$ for Lie-type bar complexes; their framework does not
reach higher genera or the full tensor bar.
Costello--Gwilliam \cite{CG17} develop factorization algebras for
perturbative QFT; they do not treat Koszul duality or the modular
characteristic.
Gui--Li--Zeng \cite{GLZ22} prove a quadratic duality at the
cochain level, a genus-$0$ degree-$2$ result.
Malikov--Schechtman \cite{MS24} construct homotopy chiral algebras
as the correct input, but do not prove the five structural
theorems.

\subsection{The Heisenberg computation}

The Heisenberg chiral algebra $\cH_k$ is generated by a single
current $J$ of conformal weight $1$ with OPE
$J(z)\,J(w) \sim k/(z-w)^2$. One generator, one double pole, no
simple pole. The binary residue of the bar differential is the
complete computation:
\[
d_{\barB}[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]
\;=\;
\Res_{z_1 = z_2}\!\left[
 \frac{k}{(z_1 - z_2)^2} \cdot d\log(z_1 - z_2)
\right]
\;=\; k.
\]
% AP1: kappa(H_k) = k from landscape_census.tex; k=0 -> 0 verified
The modular characteristic is
$\kappa(\cH_k) = k$, the collision residue
$r(z) = k/z$ (scalar, not matrix), bar-cobar inversion gives
$\Omega(\barB(\cH_k)) \simeq \cH_k$, complementarity yields
$\kappa(\cH_k) + \kappa(\cH_k^!) = k + (-k) = 0$, genus
universality produces $F_1 = k/24$ and the $\Ahat$-series at all
genera (UNIFORM-WEIGHT), and chiral Hochschild gives
$P(t) = 1 + t + t^2$. Every clause of every theorem is verified
in a single example. Every other family is a variation on this
computation, with increasingly complex collision residues.


\subsection{Conventions}

Throughout, $X$ denotes a smooth projective curve over
$\mathbf{C}$, $\cA$ a chiral algebra on $X$ in the sense of
Beilinson--Drinfeld \cite{BD04}, and $\Mbar_g = \Mbar_{g,0}$ the
Deligne--Mumford moduli space of stable curves of genus $g$.
Grading is cohomological: $|d| = +1$. Desuspension lowers degree:
$|s^{-1}v| = |v| - 1$. The bar complex uses the augmentation ideal:
$\barB(\cA) = T^c(s^{-1}\Abar)$ with $\Abar = \ker(\varepsilon)$.
The Fulton--MacPherson compactification $\overline{\FM}_n(X)$ is the
real oriented blowup of $X^n$ along all diagonal strata; it is a
manifold with corners whose codimension-one faces $D_S$ are
indexed by subsets $S \subseteq \{1, \ldots, n\}$ with $|S| \geq 2$.

\section{The bar complex on algebraic curves}
\label{sec:bar-complex}

\subsection{Chiral algebras}

A \emph{chiral algebra} $\cA$ on a smooth curve $X$
\cite{BD04} is a $\cD_X$-module equipped with a chiral bracket
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chiral-bracket}
\mu^{\mathrm{ch}} \colon
j_* j^*(\cA \boxtimes \cA)
\longrightarrow
\Delta_!(\cA),
\end{equation}
where $j \colon X^2 \setminus \Delta \hookrightarrow X^2$ is the
complement of the diagonal and $\Delta_!$ is the $!$-pushforward
along the diagonal embedding. In local coordinates, the chiral
bracket encodes the operator product expansion:
\[
a(z)\,b(w) \;\sim\;
\sum_{n \geq 0} \frac{a_{(n)}b(w)}{(z-w)^{n+1}}.
\]
The zeroth product $a_{(0)}b$ is the Lie bracket (first-order
pole); the first product $a_{(1)}b$ carries the symmetric inner
product (second-order pole). The Borcherds identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:borcherds}
\sum_{j \geq 0} \binom{m}{j}
(a_{(n+j)}b)_{(m+k-j)}c
\;=\;
\sum_{j \geq 0} (-1)^j \binom{n}{j}
\left(
a_{(m+n-j)}(b_{(k+j)}c)
- (-1)^n\, b_{(n+k-j)}(a_{(m+j)}c)
\right)
\end{equation}
is the defining axiom: it couples all pole orders in a single
constraint.

\subsection{The propagator and the Arnold relation}

The bar differential extracts OPE data from collisions
on $C_2(X) = X \times X \setminus \Delta$. A regular form has
residue zero and extracts nothing. A double pole transforms under
coordinate change and has no coordinate-invariant residue. A
simple pole is forced; among $1$-forms with a first-order pole
along $\Delta$, only the logarithmic derivative has conformally
invariant residue:
\[
\eta_{12} = d\log(z_1 - z_2)
= \frac{dz_1 - dz_2}{z_1 - z_2}\,,
\qquad \Res_\Delta\, \eta_{12} = 1.
\]
This is the \emph{propagator}: weight $1$, no dependence on level,
metric, or conformal structure.

Under $z \mapsto w(z)$, the propagator transforms as
$\eta_{12} \mapsto \eta_{12}
+ d\log\frac{w(z_1) - w(z_2)}{z_1 - z_2}$; near the diagonal
the correction reduces to $d\log w'(z_i)$, the standard cocycle
for $\mathrm{Diff}(X)$. This failure of invariance is not a
defect; it is the datum that the Virasoro algebra measures.

At three points, the Arnold relation
\[
\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23}
+ \eta_{23} \wedge \eta_{31}
+ \eta_{31} \wedge \eta_{12}
= 0
\]
restates the partial-fractions identity
\[
\frac{1}{(z_1 - z_2)(z_2 - z_3)}
+ \frac{1}{(z_2 - z_3)(z_3 - z_1)}
+ \frac{1}{(z_3 - z_1)(z_1 - z_2)}
= 0.
\]
Arnold proved that, replicated across all triples
$\{i,j,k\} \subset \{1, \ldots, n\}$, this generates the
complete ideal of relations in
$H^*(\Conf_n(\mathbf{C}), \mathbf{C})$.

\subsection{The ordered bar complex}

\begin{definition}[Ordered bar complex]\label{def:ordered-bar}
Let $\cA$ be a chiral algebra on a smooth curve $X$,
$\Abar = \ker(\varepsilon)$ the augmentation ideal.
The \emph{ordered bar complex} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bar-full}
\barB^{\mathrm{ord}}_X(\cA)
\;=\;
\bigoplus_{n \geq 1}
(s^{-1}\Abar)^{\otimes n}
\otimes
\Gamma\bigl(\overline{\FM}_n(X),\,
\Omega^{n-1}_{\log}\bigr),
\end{equation}
with no $\Sigma_n$-quotient. Here $s^{-1}$ denotes
desuspension ($|s^{-1}a| = |a| - 1$), and the cohomological
convention $|d| = +1$ is used throughout.
\end{definition}

The bar differential decomposes into three pieces:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bar-diff}
d_{\barB} = d_{\mathrm{int}} + d_{\mathrm{res}} + d_{\mathrm{dR}}.
\end{equation}
The internal differential $d_{\mathrm{int}}$ acts on each tensor
factor of $\cA$. The de Rham differential $d_{\mathrm{dR}}$ acts
on logarithmic forms on $\overline{\FM}_n(X)$. The residue
differential $d_{\mathrm{res}}$ extracts OPE data at collision
divisors: for a codimension-one face $D_{\{i,j\}}$ corresponding to
the collision $z_i \to z_j$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:res-diff}
d_{\mathrm{res}}^{(ij)}
\bigl(
a_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n \otimes \eta_{ij} \wedge \omega'
\bigr)
\;=\;
\pm\,
a_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes
\widehat{a_i} \otimes \cdots \otimes
\widehat{a_j} \otimes \cdots \otimes
a_{i(p)}a_j
\otimes \omega',
\end{equation}
where $p$ is the pole order, hats denote omission, and
$a_{(p)}b$ is the $p$-th Fourier mode of the OPE.

\subsection{Why $d^2 = 0$: the Borcherds selection principle}

\begin{theorem}[Bar nilpotence]\label{thm:bar-nilpotence}
The bar differential satisfies $d_{\barB}^2 = 0$ if and only if
the $n$-th products of $\cA$ satisfy the Borcherds
identity~\eqref{eq:borcherds}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Expand $(d_{\mathrm{int}} + d_{\mathrm{res}} + d_{\mathrm{dR}})^2$
into nine terms. Three vanish individually:
$d_{\mathrm{int}}^2 = 0$ (cochain complex),
$d_{\mathrm{dR}}^2 = 0$ (de Rham), and
$\{d_{\mathrm{int}}, d_{\mathrm{dR}}\} = 0$ (Leibniz). The
remaining terms are controlled by index overlap in the residue
operator:

\emph{Disjoint indices}
($\{i,j\} \cap \{k,\ell\} = \emptyset$): residue operators
commute; cancellation is by the Koszul sign rule. No condition on
the OPE.

\emph{One shared index} (say $j = k$): three boundary strata meet
at the codimension-$2$ locus $D_{ij\ell}$ of triple collision.
The cyclic sum of iterated residues vanishes if and only if the
Borcherds identity holds. The Arnold relation kills the
differential form; the Borcherds identity kills the algebra.
Both are consumed.

\emph{Same pair} ($\{i,j\} = \{k,\ell\}$):
$(\Res_{D_{ij}})^2 = 0$ by degree.
\end{proof}

of geometry:

\subsection{Two coproducts}

The ordered bar carries the \emph{deconcatenation
coproduct}~\eqref{eq:deconcatenation}: $n+1$ terms, coassociative
but not cocommutative, an $\Eone$-coalgebra reflecting the
linear ordering. The $\Sigma_n$-coinvariant quotient
\[
\barB^\Sigma_X(\cA)
\;=\;
\bigoplus_{n \geq 1}
(s^{-1}\Abar)^{\otimes n}_{\Sigma_n}
\otimes
\Gamma\bigl(\overline{\FM}_n(X),\,
\Omega^{n-1}_{\log}\bigr)
\]
carries the \emph{coshuffle coproduct}: $2^n$ terms,
coassociative and cocommutative, an $\Einf$-coalgebra. The
ordered bar is the primitive object; the symmetric bar is the lossy
projection that discards the $R$-matrix.

\subsection{From genus $0$ to all genera}

At genus $g \geq 1$, the function $z_1 - z_2$ has no global
meaning; the propagator must be replaced by
$d\log E(z_1, z_2)$, where $E(z,w)$ is the prime form on a
Riemann surface of genus $g$ (a section of
$K^{-1/2} \boxtimes K^{-1/2}$, weight $1$). The bar differential
acquires curvature:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:curvature}
(d_{\mathrm{fib}}^{\mathrm{ord}})^2
\;=\; r_\cA(z) \cdot \omega_g,
\end{equation}
matrix-valued in
$\operatorname{End}_\cA(2) \otimes H^{1,1}(\cC_g)$, where
$\omega_g = c_1(\mathbb{E})$ is the Hodge class on $\Mbar_g$.
Averaging over $\Sigma_2$ collapses this to the scalar equation
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_g$.

The \emph{total} bar differential
$D_\cA = \sum_{g \geq 0} \hbar^g\, d_\cA^{(g)}$ absorbs the
curvature into the genus expansion: $D_\cA^2 = 0$
unconditionally.

\begin{definition}[Modular characteristic]\label{def:kappa}
The \emph{modular characteristic} of a chiral algebra $\cA$ is
\[
\kappa(\cA) := \av(r_\cA(z)),
\]
the $\Sigma_2$-coinvariant of the collision residue $r$-matrix.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Maurer--Cartan element]\label{def:mc}
The \emph{Maurer--Cartan element} is
$\Theta_\cA := D_\cA - d_0 \in \gAmod$, the positive-genus
part of the bar differential. Since $D_\cA^2 = 0$, it satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mc}
d_0\, \Theta_\cA + \tfrac{1}{2}[\Theta_\cA, \Theta_\cA] = 0
\end{equation}
unconditionally. Existence of the MC element is a tautology
of the bar construction; the five theorems characterize its
structure.
\end{definition}

\section{Theorem A: bar-cobar adjunction and Verdier intertwining}
\label{sec:theorem-A}

\subsection{Statement}

The bar complex produces a coalgebra; the question is what
algebra it encodes.  Three functors act on the bar complex
and produce three distinct outputs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:three-functors}
\begin{array}{lcll}
\Omega(\barB(\cA)) &\simeq& \cA
 & \text{(inversion: recovers the original),}\\[3pt]
\mathbb{D}_{\Ran}(\barB(\cA)) &\simeq& \cA^!_\infty
 & \text{(Verdier: the homotopy dual),}\\[3pt]
(H^*(\barB(\cA)))^\vee &=& \cA^!
 & \text{(linear duality: the strict dual).}
\end{array}
\end{equation}
Confusing any two is a category error: cobar inverts, Verdier
dualizes at chain level, and linear duality produces the strict
dual.

\begin{definition}[The homotopy Koszul dual $\cA^!_\infty$; explicit construction]
\label{def:A-bang-infty}
For an augmented chiral algebra $\cA$ on a smooth curve~$X$ over a field of characteristic zero, define the \emph{homotopy Koszul dual factorization algebra} by
\[
  \cA^!_\infty \;:=\; \mathbb{D}_{\Ran}\bigl(\barB_X(\cA)\bigr),
\]
the Verdier dual of the bar factorization coalgebra on $\Ran(X)$. Equivalently (Lowen--Positselski homotopy-coalgebra duality for conilpotent complete objects), $\cA^!_\infty$ is the chiral algebra freely generated by $s\,\Abar^\vee$ together with an $A_\infty$-structure obtained by transferring the cooperations of $\barB_X(\cA)$ through a contraction onto its cohomology, under the finiteness hypothesis that $\barB_X(\cA)$ is degreewise finite-dimensional in each bar-depth stratum (satisfied by every freely strongly generated chiral algebra, including Heisenberg, affine Kac--Moody at generic level, Virasoro, and the principal $\mathcal{W}_N$). On the Koszul locus this $A_\infty$-structure strictifies to a genuine chiral algebra structure whose cohomology is the strict Koszul dual: $H^*(\cA^!_\infty)\cong \cA^!$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Theorem A: bar-cobar adjunction, Verdier intertwining, Koszul-conditional inversion]
\label{thm:bar-cobar-adjunction}
Let $\cA$ be an augmented chiral algebra on a smooth curve~$X$ with PBW-filtered bar complex $\barB_X(\cA)$ in the finite-bar class of Definition~\textup{\ref{def:A-bang-infty}}.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(T1\alph*)}]
\item \emph{Bar--cobar adjunction} \textup{(unconditional)}: the bar and cobar functors form an adjoint pair
 \[
 \barB_X \colon
 \mathrm{Alg}_{\mathrm{aug}}(\mathrm{Fact}(X))
 \rightleftarrows
 \mathrm{CoAlg}_{\mathrm{conil}}(\mathrm{Fact}(X))
 \,:\! \Omega_X
 \]
 with unit $\eta\colon \mathrm{id} \to \Omega_X\circ \barB_X$ and counit $\varepsilon\colon \barB_X\circ \Omega_X \to \mathrm{id}$, defined on every augmented chiral algebra without any Koszul hypothesis and natural in $\cA$ within the Francis--Gaitsgory factorization ambient.
\item \emph{Verdier intertwining} \textup{(unconditional, given finiteness)}: Verdier duality on $\Ran(X)$ intertwines the bar with the homotopy Koszul dual of Definition~\textup{\ref{def:A-bang-infty}}:
 \[
 \mathbb{D}_{\Ran}\, \barB_X(\cA) \;\simeq\; \cA^!_\infty.
 \]
 No Koszul hypothesis is invoked; $\cA^!_\infty$ carries $A_\infty$-structure measuring the failure of strict Koszulness.
\item \emph{Koszul-conditional inversion}: if $\cA$ is chirally Koszul --- i.e.\ a twisting morphism $\tau\colon \cC\to \cA$ with $\cC$ conilpotent makes $K_\tau^L(\cA,\cC)$ acyclic, or equivalently the PBW spectral sequence collapses at $E_2$ --- then the adjunction of \textup{(T1a)} and the Verdier intertwining of \textup{(T1b)} become quasi-isomorphisms:
 \[
 \cC \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} \barB_X(\cA),
 \qquad
 \Omega_X(\cC) \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} \cA,
 \qquad
 H^*(\cA^!_\infty) \cong \cA^!.
 \]
 Only this statement invokes the Koszul hypothesis; \textup{(T1a)} and \textup{(T1b)} stand without it.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Three independent statements, three disjoint inputs]\label{rem:T1-threeway}
The partition into \textup{(T1a)}, \textup{(T1b)}, \textup{(T1c)} separates three distinct universal properties with disjoint load-bearing inputs: adjunction uses only the cofree/free universal properties and the Francis--Gaitsgory ambient; Verdier intertwining uses the six-functor formalism on $\Ran(X)$ together with the finiteness hypothesis of Definition~\textup{\ref{def:A-bang-infty}}; Koszul-conditional inversion uses additional Koszul acyclicity. Bundling them into a single statement conflates three distinct universal properties and obscures which hypothesis each clause requires.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Proof sketch}

\emph{Step 1: The twisting morphism.} A chiral twisting morphism
$\tau \colon \cC \to \cA$ is a degree-$+1$ element in the chiral
convolution algebra satisfying the Maurer--Cartan equation
$d\tau + \tau \star \tau = 0$, where the star product is mediated
by the integration kernel $\eta_{12}$ on
$X^2 \setminus \Delta$. The acyclicity of the twisted tensor
product $K_\tau^L(\cA, \cC)$ is the defining property of a
chiral Koszul pair.

\emph{Step 2: The PBW recognition criterion.} The PBW filtration
on $\cA$ by bar-length makes the associated graded
quadratic-Koszul in the classical sense. The chiral algebra need
not be quadratic (the Virasoro OPE has a quartic pole, $\cW$-algebras
have poles of arbitrary order); what matters is that the
leading-order structure under the PBW filtration is. The $E_2$
page of the bar spectral sequence identifies with the classical
bar complex of the associated graded, and the collapse at
$E_2$ is the chiral Koszul condition.

\emph{Step 3: Verdier intertwining.} Verdier duality
$\mathbb{D}_{\Ran}$ on $\Ran(X)$ converts coalgebras to algebras.
Applied to $\barB_X(\cA)$, it produces a factorization algebra
$\cA^!_\infty$ on $\Ran(X)$. The key identity is
\[
\mathbb{D}_{\Ran}(T^c(s^{-1}\Abar))
\;\simeq\;
T(s\, (H^*(\barB(\cA)))^\vee),
\]
where the left side is the Verdier dual of the cofree coalgebra
and the right side is the free algebra on the linear dual of
bar cohomology. On the Koszul locus, bar cohomology
concentrates in degree $1$, and the right side reduces to the
strict Koszul dual algebra $\cA^!$.

\emph{Step 4: Filtered comparison.} The filtered comparison
lemma establishes that the PBW spectral sequence is compatible
with the Verdier functor: the filtration on $\barB_X(\cA)$
induced by bar degree is taken by $\mathbb{D}_{\Ran}$ to the
dual filtration on $\cA^!_\infty$. This is the non-formal content
of the theorem; the compatibility is not automatic and requires
the Fulton--MacPherson boundary structure.

\begin{example}[Heisenberg]\label{ex:thmA-heis}
For $\cA = \cH_k$: the bar complex
$\barB(\cH_k) = T^c(s^{-1}\mathbf{C} \cdot J)$ has bar
cohomology concentrated in degree $1$
($H^{-1}(\barB(\cH_k)) = \mathbf{C} \cdot s^{-1}J$).
The Koszul dual coalgebra is
$\cH_k^{\mathrm{i}} = \mathbf{C} \cdot s^{-1}J$; the strict
Koszul dual is the curved symmetric chiral algebra
$\cH_k^! = \mathrm{Sym}^{\mathrm{ch}}(V^*)$ with curvature
$m_0 = -k\omega$. The Verdier dual recovers
$\cH_k^!$ at the level of cohomology. The complementarity sum:
$\kappa(\cH_k) + \kappa(\cH_k^!) = k + (-k) = 0$.
\end{example}

\section{Theorem B: bar-cobar inversion on the Koszul locus}
\label{sec:theorem-B}

\subsection{Statement}

Theorem~A constructs the dual algebra $\cA^!_\infty$, but does
not prove that the bar transform is faithful: the coalgebra
$\barB(\cA)$ could in principle fail to reconstruct~$\cA$.

\begin{theorem}[Theorem B: bar-cobar inversion]
\label{thm:bar-cobar-inversion}
Let $\cA$ be a chirally Koszul chiral algebra on a smooth
curve $X$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \emph{Genus $0$ (unconditional).}
 The cobar-bar counit is a quasi-isomorphism:
 \[
 \varepsilon \colon
 \Omega_X(\barB_X(\cA))
 \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}
 \cA.
 \]
 The bar spectral sequence collapses at $E_2$, and bar
 cohomology concentrates in bar degree $1$:
 $H^*(B(\cA)) \cong (s\, \cA^{\mathrm{i}})$, identifying
 the Koszul dual coalgebra.

\item \emph{Genus $g \geq 1$ (on the modular Koszul lane).}
 At each genus $g$, if $\cA$ satisfies the diagonal Ext
 vanishing condition on the fibers of the universal curve
 over $\Mbar_{g,n}$, then the genus-$g$ cobar-bar counit
 is a quasi-isomorphism:
 $\Omega^{(g)}(\barB^{(g)}(\cA)) \xrightarrow{\sim}
 \cA^{(g)}$.
 For the standard landscape (Heisenberg, affine Kac--Moody
 at generic level, Virasoro at generic central charge,
 principal $\cW$-algebras, lattice vertex algebras), the
 Ext vanishing holds unconditionally, and bar-cobar
 inversion is unconditional at all genera.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\subsection{The genus-$0$ argument}

The genus-$0$ proof proceeds by spectral sequence. The PBW
filtration on $\cA$ by conformal weight induces a decreasing
filtration $F^p$ on $\barB_X(\cA)$ by bar degree: elements of
$F^p$ have at least $p$ tensor factors. The associated spectral
sequence has $E_1$ page equal to the classical bar complex
$B(\mathrm{gr}\, \cA)$ of the associated graded, and
converges to $H^*(\barB_X(\cA))$.

The Koszul condition is that $\mathrm{gr}\, \cA$ is quadratic
Koszul in the classical sense (Priddy \cite{Priddy70}). Then the
$E_1$ differential has cohomology concentrated on the diagonal:
$E_2^{p,q} = 0$ for $p \neq q$. The spectral sequence
collapses at $E_2$, and bar cohomology concentrates in bar
degree $1$. The counit map
$\varepsilon \colon \Omega_X(\barB_X(\cA)) \to \cA$ is then a
quasi-isomorphism by the comparison theorem for filtered
complexes.

\subsection{The higher-genus extension}

At genus $g \geq 1$, the fiberwise bar differential satisfies
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_g \neq 0$:
the bar complex is curved, and the cobar functor cannot be
applied in the classical sense. The resolution uses the total
bar differential $D_\cA$ with $D_\cA^2 = 0$: the curvature is
absorbed into the genus expansion.

The genus-$g$ inversion requires two conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Central curvature}: the curvature
 $d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_g$ is a
 scalar multiple of the Hodge class, not an arbitrary
 endomorphism-valued class.
\item \emph{Diagonal Ext vanishing}: the twisted tensor product
 $K_\tau^L(\cA^{(g)}, \cC^{(g)})$ is acyclic at genus $g$.
\end{enumerate}

For the standard landscape, central curvature is automatic (the
Virasoro-type curvature is generated by the stress tensor) and
diagonal Ext vanishing follows from the PBW argument adapted to
the genus-$g$ bar complex. The exception is the $\beta\gamma$
system at integer spin, where the Ext group has an anomalous
contribution from the fermionic parity.

\begin{example}[Heisenberg]\label{ex:thmB-heis}
$\barB(\cH_k)$ has bar cohomology concentrated in bar degree $1$:
$H^{-1}(\barB(\cH_k)) = \mathbf{C} \cdot s^{-1}J$. The cobar
recovers $\cH_k$ as a graded algebra. Bar-cobar inversion is
unconditional at all genera: the curvature
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = k \cdot \omega_g$ is scalar, and the Ext
vanishing is automatic because the only OPE pole is of order $2$
(no Lie bracket, no higher obstructions).
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Off the Koszul locus]\label{rem:off-koszul}
Off the Koszul locus, bar-cobar inversion fails: the spectral
sequence does not collapse at $E_2$, bar cohomology is not
concentrated in degree $1$, and the counit is not a
quasi-isomorphism. The failure is measured by the shadow
obstruction tower $\{S_r(\cA)\}_{r \geq 2}$: the degree-$r$
shadow class $S_r \in H^*(\Mbar_{0,r})$ is the obstruction
to extending the inversion from bar degree $r-1$ to bar degree
$r$. All standard chiral algebras in the landscape are chirally
Koszul; the shadow tower measures the \emph{complexity} of
their higher-genus behaviour, not the failure of duality itself.
\end{remark}

\section{Theorem C: complementarity}
\label{sec:theorem-C}

\subsection{The problem}

At genus $g \geq 1$, the bar complex of $\cA$ and the bar
complex of its Koszul dual $\cA^!$ coexist on the same moduli
space $\Mbar_g$. The curvatures are related:
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2(\cA) = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_g$ and
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2(\cA^!) = \kappa(\cA^!) \cdot \omega_g$.
The Verdier involution exchanges the two bar complexes. The
question is whether this exchange induces a canonical
decomposition of the ambient cohomology.

\subsection{Statement}

\begin{theorem}[Theorem C: Lagrangian complementarity]
\label{thm:complementarity}
Let $(\cA, \cA^!)$ be a chirally Koszul pair.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(C\arabic*)}]
\item \emph{Fiber-center decomposition (unconditional in the
 coderived category).}
 For every $g \geq 0$, the genus-$g$ ambient complex
 $\mathbf{C}_g(\cA)
 := R\Gamma(\Mbar_g, \cZ_\cA)$
 admits a homotopy decomposition
 \begin{equation}\label{eq:lagrangian}
 \mathbf{C}_g(\cA)
 \;\simeq\;
 \mathbf{Q}_g(\cA)
 \;\oplus\;
 \mathbf{Q}_g(\cA^!),
 \end{equation}
 where $\mathbf{Q}_g(\cA)$ and $\mathbf{Q}_g(\cA^!)$ are
 Lagrangian summands with respect to the Verdier pairing.

\item \emph{Lagrangian eigenspace decomposition
 (proved for $g \geq 1$).}
 At the cohomological level,
 \begin{equation}\label{eq:complementarity-cohom}
 Q_g(\cA) \oplus Q_g(\cA^!)
 \;\cong\;
 H^*(\Mbar_g, \cZ_\cA).
 \end{equation}
 The two summands are eigenspaces of the Verdier involution,
 and the decomposition is canonical.

\item \emph{Shifted-symplectic upgrade (conditional).}
 If the bar-cobar normal complex is perfect and nondegenerate,
 the ambient complex carries a $(2-d)$-shifted symplectic
 structure, and each Lagrangian summand carries a shifted
 Lagrangian structure. This upgrade is conditional on
 perfectness and nondegeneracy.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\subsection{The Koszul complementarity sum}

The Lagrangian decomposition constrains the modular
characteristics of $\cA$ and $\cA^!$:
\[
\kappa(\cA) + \kappa(\cA^!) \;=\; K(\cA),
\]
where $K(\cA)$ is the \emph{Koszul complementarity conductor},
a family-dependent constant. This is not a universal zero.

\begin{proposition}[Complementarity sums]
\label{prop:complementarity-sums}
% AP1: all kappa from landscape_census.tex; AP24: K family-dependent
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \emph{Heisenberg:}
 $\kappa(\cH_k) = k$,\;
 $\kappa(\cH_k^!) = -k$,\;
 $K = 0$.

\item \emph{Affine Kac--Moody:}
 % AP1: kappa(V_k(g)) = dim(g)(k+h^v)/(2h^v); k=0 -> dim(g)/2; k=-h^v -> 0
 $\kappa(V_k(\fg)) = \frac{\dim(\fg)(k + h^\vee)}{2h^\vee}$,\;
 $\kappa(V_k(\fg)^!) = -\frac{\dim(\fg)(k + h^\vee)}{2h^\vee}$,\;
 $K = 0$.

\item \emph{Virasoro:}
 % AP1: kappa(Vir_c) = c/2; c=13 -> 13/2 self-dual
 $\kappa(\mathrm{Vir}_c) = c/2$,\;
 $\kappa(\mathrm{Vir}_{26-c}) = (26-c)/2$,\;
 $K = 13$.\; Self-dual at $c = 13$.

\item \emph{Principal $\cW_N$:}
 % AP1: kappa(W_N) = c*(H_N - 1), H_N = sum 1/j; N=2: c/2 = Vir
 $\kappa(\cW_N) = c \cdot (H_N - 1)$
 where $H_N = \sum_{j=1}^{N} 1/j$.\;
 $K$ is nonzero and $N$-dependent.

\item \emph{Free fields} (lattice, $\beta\gamma$, $bc$):
 $K = 0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The Lagrangian decomposition of Theorem~C forces
$\kappa + \kappa^! = \varrho_\cA \cdot K$ at the level of the
scalar shadow, with $\varrho_\cA$ family-specific
($\varrho_{\mathrm{KM}} = \varrho_{\mathrm{free}} = 0$,
$\varrho_{\mathrm{Vir}} = 1/2$, $\varrho_{\cW_N} = H_N - 1$,
$\varrho_{\mathrm{BP}} = 1/6$).
For Heisenberg and affine Kac--Moody, the Koszul dual has
opposite modular characteristic (the Verdier involution
reverses the sign of $\kappa$), giving $K = 0$.
For the Virasoro algebra, $c \mapsto 26 - c$ under Koszul
duality (the ghost central charge), and
$K = c/2 + (26-c)/2 = 13$, constant. The self-dual point
$c = 13$ is the unique value where
$\kappa = \kappa' = 13/2$: the Lagrangian decomposition
is symmetric.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Five objects]\label{rem:five-objects}
% FOUR OBJECTS:
% 1. B(A) = bar coalgebra = T^c(s^{-1} A-bar) with deconcatenation
% 2. A^i = H^*(B(A)) = dual coalgebra (bar cohomology)
% 3. A^! = ((A^i)^v) = dual ALGEBRA (linear dual)
% 4. Z^der_ch(A) = derived chiral center = Hochschild cochains
The five objects appearing in the complementarity theorem:
$\cA$ (the algebra), $\barB(\cA)$ (the bar coalgebra),
$\cA^{\mathrm{i}} = H^*(\barB(\cA))$ (the dual coalgebra),
$\cA^! = (\cA^{\mathrm{i}})^\vee$ (the dual algebra), and
$\cZ^{\mathrm{der}}_{\mathrm{ch}}(\cA)
= R\Hom_{\cA \otimes \cA^{\mathrm{op}}}(\cA, \cA)$
(the derived chiral center). These are distinct objects produced by
distinct functors; conflation of any two is a category error.
\end{remark}

\section{Theorem D: the genus expansion}
\label{sec:theorem-D}

\subsection{Statement}

Complementarity supplies an ambient polarization of the genus tower,
but the scalar projection $\kappa$ is already bar-intrinsic and must
still be identified genus by genus.

\begin{theorem}[Theorem D: genus universality]
\label{thm:genus-universality}
Let $\cA$ be a chirally Koszul chiral algebra with modular
characteristic $\kappa(\cA)$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \emph{Genus $1$ (unconditional).}
 The genus-$1$ obstruction class is
 \[
 \obs_1(\cA) = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \lambda_1
 \in H^*(\Mbar_{1,1}),
 \]
 and the genus-$1$ free energy is
 $F_1(\cA) = \kappa(\cA)/24$.
 This holds for every family.

\item \emph{All genera, uniform weight}
 \textup{(UNIFORM-WEIGHT)}.
 For chiral algebras whose strong generators have a single
 conformal weight, the obstruction class at every genus is
 \[
 \obs_g(\cA) = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \lambda_g
 \quad (g \geq 1),
 \]
 and the free energies are
 $F_g(\cA) = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \Ahat_g$,
 where $\Ahat_g$ is the $g$-th coefficient of the
 $\Ahat$-genus:
 \begin{equation}\label{eq:ahat-generating}
 \sum_{g \geq 1} F_g(\cA) \cdot \hbar^{2g}
 \;=\;
 \kappa(\cA) \cdot
 \bigl(\Ahat(i\hbar) - 1\bigr).
 \end{equation}

\item \emph{Multi-weight correction}
 \textup{(ALL-WEIGHT $+ \delta F_g^{\mathrm{cross}}$)}.
 For multi-weight algebras at genus $g \geq 2$, the
 diagonal term remains universal but the full free energy is
 \[
 F_g(\cA) = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \lambda_g^{\mathrm{FP}}
 + \delta F_g^{\mathrm{cross}}(\cA),
 \]
 with $\delta F_g^{\mathrm{cross}}$ the cross-channel
 correction from mixed conformal-weight interactions.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Scope of the all-genera statement]
\label{rem:thmD-scope}
Part~(i) is unconditional: the genus-$1$ formula
$\obs_1 = \kappa \cdot \lambda_1$ holds for every chirally
Koszul algebra, with no uniform-weight hypothesis and no
clutching input.
Part~(ii) extends the scalar factorization from genus~$1$
to all genera by propagation along the clutching morphisms
on $\partial\Mbar_g$.
The non-trivial step is \emph{clutching-uniqueness}: the
assertion that the scalar factorization propagated through
clutching agrees, at each genus, with the direct bar-complex
computation.
This step uses the bar-intrinsic scalar trace of
$\Theta_\cA$ together with the boundary structure of
$\Mbar_g$; it is proved on the uniform-weight lane where
the graph-sum formula factors through $\kappa$ alone.
An independent derivation via the Grothendieck--Riemann--Roch
theorem on the universal curve
(\S\ref{subsec:thmD-proof}) provides a second path that
bypasses clutching-uniqueness entirely.
The genus-$1$ result and the two proof paths (clutching and
GRR) are the principal structural elements; the all-genera
statement on the uniform-weight lane rests on either one.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The $\Ahat$-genus and Bernoulli numbers}

The $\Ahat$-genus has the explicit expansion
\[
\Ahat(x) - 1
\;=\;
\frac{x/2}{\sin(x/2)} - 1
\;=\;
\frac{1}{24}\,x^2
+ \frac{7}{5760}\,x^4
+ \frac{31}{967680}\,x^6
+ \cdots
\]
The coefficients are
$\Ahat_g = \frac{2^{2g-1}-1}{2^{2g-1}} \cdot
\frac{|B_{2g}|}{(2g)!}$,
where $B_{2g}$ is the $2g$-th Bernoulli number.
The leading term $\Ahat_1 = 1/24$ is universal: for every family,
$F_1 = \kappa/24$.

\subsection{The shadow obstruction tower}

The Maurer--Cartan element $\Theta_\cA$ projects at each degree
$r \geq 2$ to a shadow invariant $S_r(\cA)$; the tower
$\{S_r\}_{r \geq 2}$ classifies chiral algebras into four
complexity classes:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccl}
\toprule
\textbf{Class} & \textbf{Depth} & $\Delta$
 & \textbf{Example} & \textbf{Behaviour} \\
\midrule
$\mathsf{G}$ (Gaussian) & $2$ & $0$ ($S_4 = 0$)
 & Heisenberg & Formal, free \\
$\mathsf{L}$ (Lie) & $3$ & $0$ ($\kappa = 0$)
 & Aff.\ KM (crit.) & Single Massey \\
$\mathsf{C}$ (Contact) & $4$ & special
 & $\beta\gamma$ & Contact then rigid \\
$\mathsf{M}$ (Mixed) & $\infty$ & $\neq 0$
 & Virasoro, $\cW_N$ & Non-formal \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
The critical discriminant $\Delta = 8\kappa S_4$ governs a
dichotomy: $\Delta = 0$ terminates the tower, $\Delta \neq 0$
makes it infinite. The \emph{shadow depth} $r_{\max}$
(the highest degree at which $S_r \neq 0$) takes values
$\{2, 3, 4, \infty\}$; the \emph{algebraic depth}
$d_{\mathrm{alg}} \in \{0, 1, 2, \infty\}$ measures the
internal complexity of the MC equation and admits no value $3$
or any finite value $\geq 3$: there is a gap.

\subsection{Proof architecture}
\label{subsec:thmD-proof}

The proof of genus universality proceeds in three steps.

\emph{Step 1: Genus $1$ (unconditional).} On $\Mbar_{1,1}$,
the self-loop graph $\Gamma_{\mathrm{irr}}$ contributes
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_1$, and
integration over $\Mbar_{1,1}$ gives $F_1 = \kappa/24$.
The only invariant that contributes at genus $1$ is $\kappa$
itself (degree $2$ of the MC element); all higher shadow
coefficients $S_r$ with $r \geq 3$ are invisible at genus $1$.

\emph{Step 2: Uniform-weight factorization.} For uniform-weight
algebras, the scalar obstruction class at every genus equals
$\kappa(\cA) \cdot \lambda_g$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:scalar-obs-hodge-euler}). Two
independent proofs are given there: (A)~via
Grothendieck--Riemann--Roch on the universal curve, identifying
the bar curvature with the Hodge bundle curvature through the
Arakelov--Faltings theorem; (B)~via clutching propagation from
genus~$1$, using the Whitney sum formula and the injectivity
of the trace on the one-dimensional tautological line.

\emph{Step 3: Multi-weight correction.} When generators have
distinct conformal weights, cross-channel propagators produce
mixed terms that do not factor through $\kappa$ alone. The
correction $\delta F_g^{\mathrm{cross}}$ is explicit and
computable from the full $R$-matrix data of the ordered bar.

\begin{remark}[Genus-$1$ anchor]\label{rem:genus-1-anchor}
Step~1 is unconditional: $\obs_1 = \kappa \cdot \lambda_1$
holds for every chirally Koszul algebra, with no
uniform-weight hypothesis and no clutching input.
The genus-$1$ result of Theorem~D(i) is independent
of the all-genera mechanism.
\end{remark}

The passage from genus~$1$ to all genera is the content of
the following proposition, which closes the all-genera extension
by two independent routes.

\begin{proposition}[Scalar obstruction equals Hodge Euler class]
\label{prop:scalar-obs-hodge-euler}
Let $\cA$ be a chirally Koszul chiral algebra on the Koszul
locus whose strong generators all have the same conformal
weight \textup{(}the uniform-weight hypothesis\textup{)}.
Let $\pi \colon \overline{\cC}_g \to \Mbar_g$ be the
universal curve, $\mathbb{E} = R^0\pi_*\omega_\pi$ the
Hodge bundle of rank~$g$, and
$\lambda_g = c_g(\mathbb{E})$ its top Chern class.
Then for all $g \geq 1$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scalar-obs-hodge}
\obs_g(\cA)
\;=\;
\kappa(\cA) \cdot \lambda_g
\qquad \textup{(}UNIFORM-WEIGHT\textup{)}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Two independent proofs are given. Each uses the genus-$1$
identification $\obs_1 = \kappa \cdot \lambda_1$ as base case
and extends to all genera by a different mechanism.

\medskip\noindent
\begin{lemma}[Uniform-weight scalar factorization of the bar complex;
shared input to Proofs~A and~B]\label{lem:T4-shared-input}
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
On the uniform-weight lane, the bar complex of a modular Koszul
chiral algebra $\cA$ over a fixed smooth projective curve $\Sigma_g$
factorizes as an external tensor product
\[
  \barB(\cA)\big|_{\Sigma_g}
  \;\simeq\;
  \barB^{\mathrm{scalar}}(\cA)
  \otimes_{\mathbb Q}
  \Omega^\bullet_{\Sigma_g}(\log D)\big|_{\mathrm{unif\text{-}wt}},
\]
with $\barB^{\mathrm{scalar}}(\cA)$ a one-dimensional algebraic
factor carrying the curvature scalar $\kappa(\cA)$ (the modular
characteristic), and $\Omega^\bullet_{\Sigma_g}(\log D)$ the
logarithmic de~Rham complex with log-poles on the collision
divisor $D \subset \Sigma_g^{\times n}$. As the curve varies
over $\Mbar_g$, the zero-mode sector of the geometric factor
is the direct image $R^0\pi_*\omega_\pi = \mathbb E$ (the Hodge
bundle), and the scalar virtual class in $K^0(\Mbar_g) \otimes \mathbb Q$
is
\[
  [\barB^{(g)}_{\mathrm{scalar}}(\cA)]^{\mathrm{vir}}
  \;=\;
  \kappa(\cA)\cdot[\mathbb E].
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The uniform-weight hypothesis forces every generator of $\cA$
to have the same conformal weight, so every OPE propagator
reduces to a scalar multiple of the single weight-$1$ form
$d\log E(z,w)$ (the prime form differential). The bar differential
is therefore a tensor of an algebraic piece (scalar channel,
recording the Casimir coefficient) with a purely geometric
piece (log-de-Rham). The zero-mode reduction from the full
$\Omega^\bullet_{\Sigma_g}$ to $R^0\pi_*\omega_\pi = \mathbb E$
is the standard Hodge-theoretic restriction at the constant
term. The virtual class equation then follows because the
scalar channel contributes the constant $\kappa(\cA)$ while
the geometric channel contributes $[\mathbb E]$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Two proofs, one input]\label{rem:T4-shared-input}
Lemma~\ref{lem:T4-shared-input} is the common load-bearing input
to the two proofs of Theorem~T4 that follow. Proof~A derives
$\obs_g(\cA) = \kappa(\cA)\cdot\lambda_g$ from this input by
Grothendieck--Riemann--Roch plus the Arakelov--Faltings
identification of the Hodge curvature. Proof~B derives it
from the same input by clutching propagation from the
unconditional genus-$1$ base case. The two proofs are
independent post-input: they invoke disjoint external
machinery (GRR/Arakelov vs.\ Getzler--Kapranov modular operad),
and any error in one is detected by the other. The two proofs
therefore provide \emph{redundant verification} of the same
numerical identity, not \emph{independent derivations} of its
structural origin --- the latter is furnished uniquely by
Lemma~\ref{lem:T4-shared-input}.
\end{remark}

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Proof A: Grothendieck--Riemann--Roch on the
universal curve.}
This proof identifies the bar-complex obstruction with a
characteristic class of the Hodge bundle directly, without
induction on the genus. It takes Lemma~\ref{lem:T4-shared-input}
as input.

\emph{Step A1: the virtual bundle on moduli.}
On the uniform-weight lane, the bar complex over a fixed
curve~$\Sigma_g$ has a one-dimensional algebraic factor
(the scalar channel) tensored with a geometric factor given by
the de~Rham complex of~$\Sigma_g$ with coefficients in
$\omega_{\Sigma_g}$, because every propagator is the standard
weight-$1$ form $d\!\log E(z,w)$.
As the curve varies over~$\Mbar_g$, the zero-mode sector of
this geometric factor is the direct image
$R^0\pi_*\omega_\pi = \mathbb{E}$.
The scalar virtual class is therefore
\[
[\barB^{(g)}_{\mathrm{scalar}}(\cA)]^{\mathrm{vir}}
\;=\;
\kappa(\cA) \cdot [\mathbb{E}]
\quad
\text{in } K^0(\Mbar_g) \otimes \mathbb{Q}.
\]

\emph{Step A2: Mumford's GRR computation.}
Grothendieck--Riemann--Roch on the universal curve gives
\[
\operatorname{ch}(R\pi_*\omega_\pi)
\;=\;
\pi_*\!\bigl(\operatorname{ch}(\omega_\pi) \cdot
\operatorname{Td}(T_\pi)\bigr).
\]
The degree-$g$ component of the Chern character of the
rank-$g$ bundle $\mathbb{E}$, combined with the Newton
identities relating Chern characters to Chern classes, gives
\[
c_g(\mathbb{E}) \;=\; \lambda_g
\quad \in \operatorname{CH}^g(\Mbar_g).
\]
This is Mumford's computation~\textup{[Mumford83]}.

\emph{Step A3: Chern--Weil identification.}
The Hodge bundle carries the Arakelov metric, whose
restriction to each fiber $H^0(\Sigma_g, \Omega^1)$ is the
$L^2$ inner product.
The Arakelov--Faltings theorem~\textup{[Fal84]} identifies
the curvature of $(\mathbb{E}, h_{\mathrm{Ar}})$ with the
Arakelov $(1,1)$-form:
\[
\Theta_{\mathbb{E}}\big|_{\mathrm{fiber}}
\;=\;
\omega_g^{\mathrm{Ar}}
\;=\;
\frac{i}{2}\sum_{\alpha,\beta=1}^{g}
(\operatorname{Im}\Omega)^{-1}_{\alpha\beta}\,
\omega_\alpha \wedge \overline{\omega}_\beta.
\]
The quantum Arnold relations give the fiberwise bar curvature
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = \kappa(\cA) \cdot
\omega_g^{\mathrm{Ar}} \cdot \mathrm{id}$.
The Arakelov--Faltings theorem identifies
$\omega_g^{\mathrm{Ar}}$ with the curvature of the Hodge
bundle. Chern--Weil then yields
\[
\obs_g(\cA)
\;=\;
\kappa(\cA) \cdot
\det\!\Bigl(\frac{i}{2\pi}\Theta_{\mathbb{E}}\Bigr)
\;=\;
\kappa(\cA) \cdot c_g(\mathbb{E})
\;=\;
\kappa(\cA) \cdot \lambda_g.
\]
No clutching argument is used. The only external inputs are
Mumford's GRR computation of the Chern classes of~$\mathbb{E}$
and the Arakelov--Faltings identification of the Hodge
curvature.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Proof B: clutching propagation from genus~$1$.}
This proof uses the boundary stratification
of~$\Mbar_g$ and proceeds by induction on~$g$.

\emph{Base case.}
$\obs_1 = \kappa \cdot \lambda_1$ is proved unconditionally
(Step~1 above; $H^2(\Mbar_{1,1}) \cong \mathbb{Q} \cdot
\lambda_1$ is one-dimensional).

\emph{Inductive step.}
Assume $\obs_h = \kappa \cdot \lambda_h$ for all $h < g$.
The Maurer--Cartan element
$\Theta_\cA \in \mathrm{MC}(\mathrm{Def}_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\cA)
\,\widehat{\otimes}\, \mathfrak{G}^{\mathrm{mod}})$
automatically satisfies the clutching factorization by the
Getzler--Kapranov correspondence~\textup{[GK98, Theorem~5.4]}:
the MC equation on the Feynman transform encodes exactly the
clutching compatibility conditions.

The separating clutching
$\xi_{\mathrm{sep}} \colon
\Mbar_{g_1,1} \times \Mbar_{g_2,1} \to \Mbar_g$
(with $g_1 + g_2 = g$) gives
\[
\xi_{\mathrm{sep}}^*(\obs_g)
\;=\;
\sum_{g_1+g_2=g}
\obs_{g_1} \star \obs_{g_2}
\;=\;
\sum_{g_1+g_2=g}
\kappa \cdot \lambda_{g_1}
\star
\kappa \cdot \lambda_{g_2}
\;=\;
\kappa \cdot
\xi_{\mathrm{sep}}^*(\lambda_g),
\]
where the star product in the convolution algebra contracts
along the glued edge via the propagator $\eta^{-1} = 1/\kappa$,
absorbing one factor of~$\kappa$: explicitly,
$(\kappa\lambda_{g_1}) \star (\kappa\lambda_{g_2})
= (1/\kappa) \cdot \kappa\lambda_{g_1} \cdot \kappa\lambda_{g_2}
= \kappa \cdot \lambda_{g_1}\lambda_{g_2}$.
The last equality uses the Whitney sum formula
$\xi_{\mathrm{sep}}^*(\lambda_g)
= \sum_{i+j=g} \lambda_i \otimes \lambda_j$
for the Hodge bundle.

The non-separating clutching
$\xi_{\mathrm{nsep}} \colon \Mbar_{g-1,2} \to \Mbar_g$
gives
\[
\xi_{\mathrm{nsep}}^*(\obs_g)
\;=\;
\kappa \cdot
\xi_{\mathrm{nsep}}^*(\lambda_g),
\]
by the inductive hypothesis applied to the genus-$(g-1)$
data at the self-node vertex.

The trace functional (integration over $\Mbar_g$) satisfies
$\int_{\Mbar_g} \obs_g = F_g = \kappa \cdot \Ahat_g$
(from the bar-complex graph sum on the uniform-weight lane),
and $\int_{\Mbar_g} \lambda_g = \Ahat_g$
(the Faber--Pandharipande evaluation). Hence
\[
\ell_g(\obs_g) \;=\; \kappa \cdot \ell_g(\lambda_g).
\]

It remains to show that the joint map
$J_g = (\ell_g, \mathrm{clut}_g) \colon W_g \to
\mathbb{C} \oplus B_g$
is injective on the relevant subspace
$U_g \ni \obs_g, \lambda_g$.
On the uniform-weight lane, $U_g$ is the one-dimensional
tautological line $\mathbb{Q} \cdot \lambda_g$ (the scalar
channel produces only classes built from the Hodge bundle;
no higher Hodge bundles $R^0\pi_*\omega_\pi^{\otimes h}$
with $h \neq 1$ enter). On this one-dimensional space, the
trace $\ell_g$ is already injective (since
$\ell_g(\lambda_g) = \Ahat_g \neq 0$), so
$J_g$ is injective a fortiori.

By the tautological-line support criterion, the injectivity
of~$J_g$ together with the clutching and trace compatibilities
gives $\obs_g = \kappa \cdot \lambda_g$.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Agreement of the two proofs.}
Proof~A produces the identification directly from the Hodge
bundle structure on the universal curve. Proof~B produces it
inductively from the boundary stratification. The two proofs
have no logical dependency on each other: Proof~A uses GRR
and Arakelov--Faltings; Proof~B uses Getzler--Kapranov and
the tautological-line support criterion. Their agreement is a
consistency check on the framework.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Resolution of the clutching-uniqueness gap]
\label{rem:clutching-gap-resolved}
The all-genera extension of Theorem~D was identified as
depending on a ``clutching-uniqueness'' step: the assertion
that the boundary restrictions of $\obs_g$ determine $\obs_g$
uniquely within the tautological ring. Proof~B above
establishes this step by showing that the relevant subspace
$U_g$ is one-dimensional on the uniform-weight lane (the
scalar channel sees only the Hodge bundle), so injectivity of
the joint map is automatic. Proof~A bypasses the question
entirely. The all-genera statement of Theorem~D(ii) is
therefore unconditional on the uniform-weight lane, via either
route.
\end{remark}

\section{Theorem H: chiral Hochschild cohomology}
\label{sec:theorem-H}

\subsection{Statement}

Theorem~D identifies a universal genus expansion governed by
$\kappa$; the question is what constrains the coefficient ring
in which $\kappa$ lives.

\begin{theorem}[Theorem H: polynomial Hilbert series]
\label{thm:hochschild}
Let $\cA$ be a chirally Koszul chiral algebra on a smooth
curve $X$. The chiral Hochschild cohomology is concentrated
in degrees $\{0, 1, 2\}$:
\[
\ChirHoch^n(\cA) = 0
\quad \text{for } n \notin \{0, 1, 2\},
\]
and the Hilbert polynomial is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hilbert-poly}
P_\cA(t)
\;=\;
\dim Z(\cA)
+ \dim \ChirHoch^1(\cA) \cdot t
+ \dim Z(\cA^!) \cdot t^2.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{warning}[Three Hochschild theories: chiral, topological, categorical]
\label{warn:three-hochschild}
Three inequivalent Hochschild cohomology theories coexist: chiral,
topological, and categorical.
\emph{Chiral Hochschild}
$\ChirHoch^*(\cA) = H^*(\mathrm{CoDer}(\barB(\cA)))$ is the
cohomology of coderivations of the bar coalgebra; it lives on
the curve $X$ and has amplitude $[0,2]$ on the Koszul locus
(Theorem~H). \emph{Topological Hochschild}
$\mathrm{HH}^*_{\mathrm{top}}$ is factorization homology on
$S^1$; it lives in one dimension higher. \emph{Categorical Hochschild}
$\mathrm{HH}^*(\mathrm{Rep}(\cA))$ governs the
Drinfeld center and the $E_2$ structure. Conflating any two
produces errors.
\end{warning}

\subsection{Family-specific specializations}

\begin{proposition}[Hilbert series for standard families]
\label{prop:hilbert-families}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \emph{Heisenberg $\cH_k$:}\;
 $P(t) = 1 + t + t^2$.\;
 Centre = $\mathbf{C}$, outer derivation $D(J) = \mathbf{1}$,
 level deformation in degree $2$.

\item \emph{Affine Kac--Moody $V_k(\fg)$ (generic level):}\;
 $\ChirHoch^1(V_k(\fg)) \cong \fg$, giving total
 dimension $\dim(\fg) + 2$.\;
 $P(t) = 1 + \dim(\fg) \cdot t + t^2$.

\item \emph{Virasoro $\mathrm{Vir}_c$ (generic):}\;
 $P(t) = 1 + t^2$.\; $\ChirHoch^1 = 0$ (no outer
 chiral derivations).

\item \emph{Principal $\cW_N$ (generic):}\;
 $P(t) = 1 + t^2$.

\item \emph{Critical level $k = -h^\vee$:}\; Outside the
 regime of Theorem~H. The Feigin--Frenkel center makes the
 cohomology unbounded.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Proof architecture}

The descent lemma reduces Theorem~H to the Verdier
intertwining of Theorem~A: the concentration of
$\ChirHoch^*(\cA)$ in degrees $\{0,1,2\}$ follows from the
Koszul resolution having length $2$ (the bar coalgebra has
generators in a single bar degree on the Koszul locus), and
the identification of the Hilbert polynomial with the dimensions
of the center and its dual follows from the Verdier
intertwining pairing degree-$0$ with degree-$2$.

The configuration-space collapse uses Fulton--MacPherson
formality: the spectral sequence from the FM filtration on the
coderivation complex collapses, and the $E_2$ page identifies
with the cohomology of the classical coderivation complex of
the associated graded.

\section{The forced chain $\mathrm{A} \Rightarrow \mathrm{B}
 \Rightarrow \mathrm{C} \Rightarrow \mathrm{D}
 \Rightarrow \mathrm{H}$}
\label{sec:forced-chain}

The five theorems are not five unrelated assertions about the bar
complex. They form a chain: each theorem solves a problem created
by its predecessor, and each requires exactly one new geometric
input beyond what the previous theorem provides.

\subsection{The dependency DAG}

The logical dependencies are strictly linear:
\[
\fbox{A}
\xrightarrow{\;\text{dual object}\;}
\fbox{B}
\xrightarrow{\;\text{coexistence}\;}
\fbox{C}
\xrightarrow{\;\text{polarization}\;}
\fbox{D}
\xrightarrow{\;\text{coefficient ring}\;}
\fbox{H}
\]
No arrow can be reversed. Each node depends on all preceding
nodes. The chain is \emph{forced}: removing any one theorem
leaves the subsequent ones either undefined or vacuous.

\subsection{A creates the arena}

\begin{proposition}[A is necessary for B--H]
\label{prop:A-necessary}
Without Theorem~A, the objects of Theorems~B--H do not exist.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Theorem~B asserts that the bar-cobar counit
$\varepsilon \colon \Omega(\barB(\cA)) \to \cA$ is a
quasi-isomorphism. The cobar functor $\Omega$ is defined on
conilpotent coalgebras in the factorization category
$\mathrm{Fact}(X)$; its existence and the adjunction with
$\barB$ are the content of Theorem~A(i). Without the
adjunction, the counit $\varepsilon$ is undefined.

Theorem~C decomposes
$H^*(\Mbar_g, \cZ_\cA) \cong Q_g(\cA) \oplus Q_g(\cA^!)$.
The summand $Q_g(\cA^!)$ involves the Koszul dual
$\cA^!_\infty$, whose existence is Theorem~A(ii): the
Verdier intertwining
$\mathbb{D}_{\Ran}\, \barB(\cA) \simeq \cA^!_\infty$.
Without this intertwining, the dual object is unavailable.

Theorems~D and~H depend on~C and~B respectively; their
dependence on~A is inherited.
\end{proof}

The geometric input that Theorem~A requires, beyond the bar
complex itself, is the Fulton--MacPherson compactification
$\overline{\FM}_n(X)$ and its boundary divisor structure: the
twisting morphism $\tau$ is mediated by integration kernels
on $\overline{\FM}_n(X)$, and the Verdier intertwining uses
the Poincar\'{e}--Verdier duality on $\Ran(X)$.

\subsection{B establishes faithfulness}

\begin{proposition}[B is necessary for C and strict-dual identification]
\label{prop:B-necessary}
Without Theorem~B, the Lagrangian decomposition of Theorem~C
is vacuous and the relation between the homotopy dual and the
strict Koszul dual is ungrounded.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Theorem~A constructs the homotopy Koszul dual
$\cA^!_\infty$, but the construction produces a
factorization algebra that a priori differs from $\cA^!$.
Theorem~B proves that bar-cobar inversion is faithful:
$\barB$ is injective on quasi-isomorphism classes. This
identifies $\cA$ and $\cA^!$ as the two canonical algebras
recoverable from the bar coalgebra, and establishes that
Verdier duality on $\barB(\cA)$ produces the \emph{correct}
dual.

Without this identification, the decomposition
$Q_g(\cA) \oplus Q_g(\cA^!)$ in Theorem~C involves an
object $\cA^!_\infty$ whose relation to the strict dual
$\cA^!$ is uncontrolled: the summands may not be
complementary. Theorem~D is bar-intrinsic and does not use
this identification to produce the scalar genus tower, but the
strict-dual interpretation of complementarity and the
downstream duality package do require it.
\end{proof}

The geometric input is the \emph{PBW filtration}: the
spectral sequence from conformal-weight filtration collapses
at $E_2$, reducing the problem to classical Koszul duality
of the associated graded. This is the only step that
involves the conformal weight grading; Theorem~A uses only
the chiral bracket and $\cD_X$-module structure.

\subsection{C provides the polarization}

\begin{proposition}[C is necessary for the ambient polarization package]
\label{prop:C-necessary}
Without Theorem~C, the genus tower has no canonical
factorization into $\cA$ and $\cA^!$ contributions, and the
complementarity interpretation of the scalar genus tower is
unavailable.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
At genus $g \geq 1$, the bar differential satisfies
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = \kappa \cdot \omega_g$, and the
Maurer--Cartan element $\Theta_\cA$ encodes all genus
corrections. But the ambient complex
$\mathbf{C}_g(\cA) = R\Gamma(\Mbar_g, \cZ_\cA)$ receives
contributions from both $\cA$ and its dual. Without the
Lagrangian decomposition, these contributions are entangled:
one loses the canonical ambient polarization into $\cA$ and
$\cA^!$ sectors.

Theorem~C provides the polarization: the Verdier involution
decomposes $\mathbf{C}_g$ into eigenspaces, and the scalar
projection of this decomposition yields the complementarity
constraint $\kappa + \kappa^! = \varrho_\cA \cdot K$
(family-specific $\varrho_\cA$: $0$ for Kac--Moody and free
fields, $1/2$ for Virasoro, $H_N - 1$ for principal $\cW_N$,
$1/6$ for Bershadsky--Polyakov). Theorem~D still extracts the
universal scalar shadow bar-intrinsically from the genus-$1$
curvature together with clutching/GRR propagation; Theorem~C
explains how that scalar sits inside the ambient complex and
how duality constrains it.
\end{proof}

The geometric input is the \emph{Verdier involution}: the
existence of a functorial duality on sheaves on $\Mbar_g$
that exchanges the bar complex of $\cA$ with that of $\cA^!$.
This requires the moduli space $\Mbar_g$ as a smooth
Deligne--Mumford stack with Poincar\'{e} duality.

\subsection{D identifies the scalar shadow}

\begin{proposition}[D is necessary for H]
\label{prop:D-necessary}
Without Theorem~D, the coefficient ring of the genus tower
is unidentified, and Theorem~H cannot distinguish
polynomial behaviour from more complex coefficient growth.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The genus expansion
$\sum_g F_g(\cA) \cdot \hbar^{2g} = \kappa \cdot (\Ahat(i\hbar) - 1)$
shows that all genus invariants are determined by a single
number $\kappa$. The Maurer--Cartan element $\Theta_\cA$
lives in the convolution algebra $\gAmod$, whose
coefficient ring is the chiral Hochschild cohomology
$\ChirHoch^*(\cA)$. Theorem~D demonstrates that the
genus tower is not an independent sequence of invariants
but a single series governed by $\kappa$; this forces the
question: what constrains $\kappa$ itself?

The answer is Theorem~H. The modular characteristic $\kappa$
lives in degree~$2$ of $\ChirHoch^*(\cA)$. The deformations
of $\kappa$ are parametrized by $\ChirHoch^2(\cA)$; the
obstructions are in $\ChirHoch^3(\cA)$. Theorem~H proves
$\ChirHoch^3 = 0$: the deformation theory is unobstructed,
and the coefficient ring is polynomial. Without Theorem~D's
identification of $\kappa$ as the universal scalar, one
would not know that the coefficient ring has a single
generator in degree~$2$; one would only know that genus
invariants exist.
\end{proof}

The geometric input is \emph{Faber--Pandharipande
integration}: the evaluation of $\lambda_g$ on $\Mbar_g$
produces the numerical coefficients $\Ahat_g$, and the
factorization through stable graphs uses the clutching
morphisms on the boundary of $\Mbar_g$.

\subsection{The non-circular routing}

The chain A $\Rightarrow$ B $\Rightarrow$ C $\Rightarrow$ D
$\Rightarrow$ H might appear circular: Theorem~A uses the
bar complex, whose higher-genus structure involves
$\kappa$ (which is computed in Theorem~D). The resolution is
that the chain has a non-circular anchor.

\begin{remark}[Primitive non-circular anchor]
\label{rem:non-circular}
The bar complex $\barB(\cA) = T^c(s^{-1}\Abar)$ and the
Maurer--Cartan element $\Theta_\cA = D_\cA - d_0$ are
defined intrinsically, without reference to any theorem.
The MC equation $d_0\Theta + \frac{1}{2}[\Theta,\Theta] = 0$
is a tautology of the bar differential $D_\cA^2 = 0$.

Theorem~A uses only genus-$0$ data: the twisting morphism
and the Verdier functor on $\Ran(X)$. Theorem~B uses the
PBW filtration at genus~$0$. Theorem~C uses the Verdier
involution on $\Mbar_g$ applied to the objects constructed
in~A and identified in~B. Theorem~D uses the bar-intrinsic
scalar trace of $\Theta_\cA$, the genus-$1$ curvature
normalization, and the clutching/GRR propagation to identify
the scalar shadow $\kappa$, which was already present in the
definition of $\Theta_\cA$ but not yet identified as
universal. Theorem~C gives a complementary ambient
interpretation of the same scalar, but is not the load-bearing
input to Theorem~D. Theorem~H uses the concentration result
from Koszul duality (Theorem~B) together with the scalar
package of Theorem~D to bound the coderivation complex.

The routing is: bar-intrinsic MC element (definition)
$\to$ adjunction/intertwining (A), then two downstream
branches. One branch is inversion (B) $\to$ Verdier
polarization (C). The other is scalar trace
$+$ genus-$1$ curvature $+$ clutching/GRR
$\to$ scalar extraction (D). Theorem~H reads the coefficient
ring against these outputs. No step requires a result proved
later.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Independence of converses}

The converses of the five implications fail. This is not
a deficiency; it reflects the fact that each theorem addresses
a strictly different aspect of the bar complex.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{H does not imply D.} A polynomial Hilbert series
 $P(t) = 1 + t^2$ (Virasoro) does not determine
 $\kappa = c/2$; it constrains $\dim \ChirHoch^2 = 1$
 (the space where $\kappa$ lives has dimension~$1$) but
 not the value.

\item \emph{D does not imply C.} The formula
 $\obs_g = \kappa \cdot \lambda_g$ could hold
 without a Lagrangian decomposition: one could imagine
 a non-decomposable ambient complex whose scalar
 projection still factors through $\kappa$. Theorem~C
 is stronger; it provides a complementary ambient
 interpretation of the factorization, but not the
 load-bearing proof of Theorem~D.

\item \emph{C does not imply B.} The Lagrangian
 decomposition $Q_g(\cA) \oplus Q_g(\cA^!)$ is
 meaningful even if bar-cobar inversion fails; one
 could have a Lagrangian complement indexed by a
 coalgebra that does not reconstruct~$\cA$. Inversion
 is a separate condition.

\item \emph{B does not imply A.} One could define bar-cobar
 inversion without the adjunction formalism: the
 counit $\varepsilon$ could be a quasi-isomorphism
 without being part of an adjoint pair. The adjunction
 gives functoriality; inversion alone does not.
\end{enumerate}

\section{The Heisenberg: complete computation}
\label{sec:heisenberg}

The Heisenberg algebra $\cH_k$ is the simplest chiral algebra
that is not a Lie algebra. It has one generator, one double pole,
and no simple pole. Every clause of every theorem is computable
in closed form. This section carries out the computation: the five
theorems verified in a single place, with no step deferred.

\subsection{The algebra}

The Heisenberg chiral algebra $\cH_k$ at level $k \in \mathbf{C}$
is generated by a single current $J$ of conformal weight $1$, with
the operator product expansion
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-ope}
J(z)\, J(w) \;\sim\; \frac{k}{(z-w)^2}\,.
\end{equation}
One generator, one OPE pole of order $2$, no simple pole. The
zeroth product $J_{(0)}J = 0$ (no Lie bracket: the algebra is
abelian). The first product $J_{(1)}J = k$ (the level is the
inner product). The augmentation ideal is
$\overline{\cH}_k = \mathbf{C} \cdot J$, one-dimensional.

The classical $R$-matrix is scalar:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-rmatrix}
% AP126: r(z) = k/z; k=0 -> r=0 verified
r^{\cH}(z) \;=\; \frac{k}{z}\,,
\end{equation}
obtained from the OPE double pole by $d\log$ absorption:
$\mathrm{pole}_r = \mathrm{pole}_{\mathrm{OPE}} - 1 = 2 - 1 = 1$.
% AP1: kappa(H_k) = k from landscape_census.tex; k=0 -> 0 verified
The modular characteristic is $\kappa(\cH_k) = \av(r^{\cH}(z)) = k$.
Since $r(z)$ is already scalar, the averaging map is the identity:
no matrix structure to erase, no Casimir tensor to contract. The
Heisenberg is the unique family where the ordered and symmetric
bars carry exactly the same information.

\subsection{Theorem A for $\cH_k$: bar-cobar adjunction}

The bar complex is the cofree coalgebra on the desuspended
augmentation ideal:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-bar}
\barB(\cH_k) \;=\; T^c(s^{-1}\mathbf{C}\cdot J)
\;=\;
\bigoplus_{n \geq 1} (s^{-1}J)^{\otimes n},
\qquad
|s^{-1}J| = 1 - 1 = 0.
\end{equation}
The bar differential at degree $2$ is the complete computation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-bar-diff}
d_{\barB}[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]
\;=\;
\Res_{z_1 = z_2}\!\left[
 \frac{k}{(z_1 - z_2)^2} \cdot d\log(z_1 - z_2)
\right]
\;=\; k.
\end{equation}
At degree $n \geq 3$, the bar differential involves iterated
residues that reduce to degree $n-1$ by the Arnold relation. The
bar cohomology concentrates in bar degree $1$:
\[
H^*(\barB(\cH_k))
\;=\;
\mathbf{C} \cdot s^{-1}J
\qquad (\text{degree } -1).
\]
The Koszul dual coalgebra is
$\cH_k^{\mathrm{i}} = \mathbf{C} \cdot s^{-1}J$
(one-dimensional, in degree $-1$). The strict Koszul dual is the
curved symmetric chiral algebra
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-dual}
\cH_k^!
\;=\;
\mathrm{Sym}^{\mathrm{ch}}(V^*),
\qquad
m_0 = -k\omega,
\end{equation}
with curvature $m_0 = -k\omega$ encoding the dual level. The
Verdier intertwining of Theorem~A(ii) recovers $\cH_k^!$ at the
cohomological level:
$\mathbb{D}_{\Ran}\, \barB(\cH_k) \simeq (\cH_k^!)_\infty$.

\subsection{Theorem B for $\cH_k$: bar-cobar inversion}

The cobar of the bar recovers the original algebra:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-cobar}
\Omega(\barB(\cH_k))
\;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\;
\cH_k.
\end{equation}
The PBW spectral sequence collapses at $E_2$ because the bar
cohomology is already concentrated in degree~$1$: the associated
graded is quadratic Koszul in the classical sense (the relation
space is one-dimensional, generated by $J \otimes J - k$).

Bar-cobar inversion for $\cH_k$ is \emph{unconditional at all
genera}. At genus $g \geq 1$, the fiberwise bar differential
satisfies
$d_{\mathrm{fib}}^2 = k \cdot \omega_g$
with scalar curvature. The Ext vanishing condition of
Theorem~\ref{thm:bar-cobar-inversion}(ii) is automatic: the
OPE has a single double pole and no simple pole, so there are
no Lie brackets to obstruct and no higher-order collisions to
produce non-trivial Ext groups. The absence of a simple pole
($J_{(0)}J = 0$) is the algebraic reason that $\cH_k$ is the
simplest non-trivial test case for every theorem in the
programme.

\subsection{Theorem C for $\cH_k$: complementarity}

The Koszul dual has modular characteristic
$\kappa(\cH_k^!) = -k$: the curvature $m_0 = -k\omega$ encodes
the opposite level. The complementarity sum is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-complementarity}
% AP24: K=0 for Heisenberg (family-specific, not universal)
\kappa(\cH_k) + \kappa(\cH_k^!) \;=\; k + (-k) \;=\; 0
\;=\; K(\cH_k).
\end{equation}
The Koszul complementarity conductor $K = 0$: the Heisenberg
is in the class of algebras where duality reverses the sign of
$\kappa$.

The Lagrangian decomposition of Theorem~\ref{thm:complementarity}
is visible by inspection. The center local system
$\cZ_{\cH_k}$ on $\Mbar_g$ is the constant sheaf (the center of
$\cH_k$ is $\mathbf{C}$, with no monodromy). The ambient
complex
$\mathbf{C}_g(\cH_k) = R\Gamma(\Mbar_g, \cZ_{\cH_k})
= H^*(\Mbar_g, \mathbf{C})$
decomposes into the Hodge eigenspaces of the Verdier involution,
and the two Lagrangian summands $Q_g(\cH_k)$ and $Q_g(\cH_k^!)$
are exchanged by $\kappa \mapsto -\kappa$.

\subsection{Theorem D for $\cH_k$: the genus expansion}

The Heisenberg is a uniform-weight algebra (single generator of
conformal weight $1$), so the genus expansion takes the
unconditional form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-obs}
% AP32: (UNIFORM-WEIGHT) tag
\obs_g(\cH_k) \;=\; k \cdot \lambda_g
\qquad (g \geq 1)
\quad\text{(UNIFORM-WEIGHT)}.
\end{equation}
No cross-channel correction: $\delta F_g^{\mathrm{cross}} = 0$
because there is only one conformal weight.

The genus-$1$ free energy:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-F1}
F_1(\cH_k) \;=\; \frac{k}{24}\,.
\end{equation}
% C24 check: F_1 = kappa/24 = k/24

The full generating function is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-generating}
\sum_{g \geq 1} F_g(\cH_k) \cdot \hbar^{2g}
\;=\;
k \cdot \left(\frac{\hbar/2}{\sin(\hbar/2)} - 1\right)
\;=\;
\frac{k}{24}\,\hbar^2
+ \frac{7k}{5760}\,\hbar^4
+ \frac{31k}{967680}\,\hbar^6
+ \cdots
\end{equation}
with coefficients
$F_g(\cH_k) = k \cdot \frac{2^{2g-1} - 1}{2^{2g-1}}
\cdot \frac{|B_{2g}|}{(2g)!}$,
where $B_{2g}$ is the $2g$-th Bernoulli number.

\begin{remark}[The chiral partition function]
\label{rem:heis-partition}
The genus-$1$ chiral partition function of $\cH_k$ is
\[
Z_{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k)
\;=\;
\frac{1}{\eta(\tau)}\,,
\qquad
\eta(\tau) = q^{1/24}\prod_{n \geq 1}(1 - q^n),
\]
where $q = e^{2\pi i\tau}$. The leading exponent $q^{-1/24}$
encodes $F_1 = k/24$ (at $k = 1$). The product form is the
Euler function; its reciprocal counts states at each energy
level.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[$R$-matrix and averaging]\label{rem:heis-averaging}
The $R$-matrix $r^{\cH}(z) = k/z$ is already scalar, so
$\av(r^{\cH}(z)) = k = \kappa(\cH_k)$ with no information
lost. For every other family, the averaging map $\av$ compresses
a matrix-valued function of $z$ into a single number. The
Heisenberg is the unique base case where the compression is
lossless. For non-abelian affine Kac--Moody, the averaging
discards the Casimir tensor $\Omega$ and the $z$-dependence; for
the Virasoro algebra, it discards the stress tensor $T$ and the
cubic pole structure. The ordered bar sees the full $R$-matrix;
the symmetric bar sees only $\kappa$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Theorem H for $\cH_k$: chiral Hochschild cohomology}

The chiral Hochschild cohomology of $\cH_k$ is three-dimensional:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heis-chirhoch}
\ChirHoch^n(\cH_k)
\;=\;
\begin{cases}
\mathbf{C} & n = 0, \\
\mathbf{C} & n = 1, \\
\mathbf{C} & n = 2, \\
0 & n \geq 3.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
The Hilbert polynomial is $P_{\cH_k}(t) = 1 + t + t^2$.

The three generators have transparent algebraic meaning:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Degree $0$: the center.} $\ChirHoch^0(\cH_k)
 = Z(\cH_k) = \mathbf{C}$. The center consists of scalars;
 $J$ has no zeroth product ($J_{(0)} = 0$), so $J$ itself
 is central, but $J$ is not a scalar; the degree-$0$
 chiral Hochschild class is the identity.

\item \emph{Degree $1$: the outer derivation.}
 $\ChirHoch^1(\cH_k) = \mathbf{C}$, generated by the
 derivation $D \colon J \mapsto \mathbf{1}$ that maps the
 current to the vacuum. This derivation is outer: it is not
 of the form $a \mapsto a_{(0)}b$ for any $b \in \cH_k$,
 because $J_{(0)} = 0$ (no simple pole in the OPE). The
 existence of an outer derivation is the Heisenberg's
 distinguishing feature relative to the Virasoro algebra,
 where $\ChirHoch^1 = 0$.

\item \emph{Degree $2$: the level deformation.}
 $\ChirHoch^2(\cH_k) = \mathbf{C}$, generated by the
 infinitesimal deformation of the OPE coefficient
 $k \mapsto k + \epsilon$. This is the dual of the center:
 the Verdier pairing identifies
 $\ChirHoch^2(\cH_k) \cong Z(\cH_k^!)^\vee = \mathbf{C}$,
 consistent with the general formula~\eqref{eq:hilbert-poly}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The shadow tower and the derived center}

The shadow tower $\{S_r(\cH_k)\}_{r \geq 2}$ terminates at
$r = 2$:
\[
S_2 = \kappa = k, \qquad
S_r = 0 \;\text{ for all } r \geq 3.
\]
The critical discriminant $\Delta = 8\kappa S_4 = 0$: the
Heisenberg is class~G (Gaussian), the simplest archetype. The
shadow tower carries no information beyond $\kappa$ itself.

The derived chiral center is
$\cZ^{\mathrm{der}}_{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k)
= \ChirHoch^*(\cH_k) = \mathbf{C} \oplus \mathbf{C}[1]
\oplus \mathbf{C}[2]$,
three-dimensional ($k \neq 0$). This is strictly larger than
the naive center $Z(\cH_k) = \mathbf{C}$: the Ext$^1$ and
Ext$^2$ contributions are invisible to the commutant.

\begin{remark}[Abelian Chern--Simons]
\label{rem:abelian-cs}
The Heisenberg algebra $\cH_k$ is the boundary algebra of abelian
Chern--Simons theory at level $k$: the $3$-dimensional bulk is
$U(1)_k$ Chern--Simons on a $3$-manifold $M^3$ with
$\partial M^3 = \Sigma$. The modular characteristic
$\kappa = k$ is the Chern--Simons level; the genus expansion
$F_g = k \cdot \Ahat_g$ is the perturbative expansion of the
Chern--Simons partition function. The complementarity sum
$K = 0$ reflects the topological nature of the theory: bulk and
boundary carry opposite orientations.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The general affine Kac--Moody formula}

For a simple Lie algebra $\fg$ of dimension $d = \dim\fg$ and
dual Coxeter number $h^\vee$, the affine Kac--Moody vertex
algebra $V_k(\fg)$ at level $k$ has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:km-kappa}
% AP1: kappa(V_k(g)) = dim(g)(k+h^v)/(2h^v); k=0 -> dim(g)/2; k=-h^v -> 0
\kappa(V_k(\fg))
\;=\;
\frac{d \cdot (k + h^\vee)}{2h^\vee}\,.
\end{equation}
The formula is derived from the trace-form classical
$R$-matrix $r(z) = k\,\Omega/z$:
\[
% AP126: k=0 -> r=0 verified; k=-h^v -> critical level
\av(r(z)) \;=\; \frac{k \cdot d}{2h^\vee}
\;=\; \kappa - \frac{d}{2}\,,
\]
where the shift $d/2$ is the Sugawara contribution from the
simple-pole self-contraction through the adjoint Casimir eigenvalue
$2h^\vee$. The full modular characteristic is
$\kappa = \av(r(z)) + d/2 = d(k + h^\vee)/(2h^\vee)$.

Two boundary checks:
\begin{enumerate}
\item At $k = 0$ (abelian limit):
 $\kappa(V_0(\fg)) = d/2 \neq 0$. The $R$-matrix
 $r(z) = 0$ vanishes, but the Sugawara shift persists.
\item At $k = -h^\vee$ (critical level):
 $\kappa(V_{-h^\vee}(\fg)) = 0$. All monodromy becomes
 trivial; the Feigin--Frenkel center appears; Koszulness
 fails.
\end{enumerate}

The Koszul duality acts by $k \mapsto -k - 2h^\vee$ (the
Feigin--Frenkel involution), giving
$\kappa(V_k(\fg)^!) = -d(k + h^\vee)/(2h^\vee) = -\kappa$,
so $K = 0$ for all affine Kac--Moody algebras. The
complementarity sum $c + c'$ is $2d$, independent of $k$.

\subsection{The four complexity classes}

The shadow obstruction tower $\{S_r\}_{r \geq 2}$ partitions the
landscape into four classes. The partition is determined by two
invariants: $S_4$ (the quartic shadow coefficient) and $\kappa$.

\begin{definition}[Complexity class]\label{def:complexity-class}
Let $\Delta = 8\kappa S_4$ be the critical discriminant. The
four classes are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)}]
\item \textbf{Class G} (Gaussian): $S_r = 0$ for all
 $r \geq 3$. Shadow depth $r_{\max} = 2$. The shadow
 tower carries only $\kappa$; no nonlinear corrections.
 $\Delta = 0$ because $S_4 = 0$.

\item \textbf{Class L} (Lie): $S_3 \neq 0$, $S_4 = 0$.
 Shadow depth $r_{\max} = 3$. A single Massey product
 appears at degree $3$ (the Lie bracket), then the tower
 terminates. $\Delta = 0$ because $S_4 = 0$.

\item \textbf{Class C} (Contact): $S_3 = 0$, $S_4 \neq 0$,
 but $\kappa = 0$ or special cancellation gives
 $\Delta = 0$. Shadow depth $r_{\max} = 4$. The
 $\beta\gamma$ system is the witness: $r_{\max} = 4$ but
 $S_r = 0$ for $r \geq 5$.

\item \textbf{Class M} (Mixed): $\Delta \neq 0$. Shadow
 depth $r_{\max} = \infty$. The tower does not
 terminate; every $S_r$ is nonzero. The Virasoro
 algebra and all principal $\cW_N$-algebras are class~M.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The partition is \emph{forced}: the critical discriminant
$\Delta$ governs a dichotomy. If $\Delta = 0$ (i.e.\
$\kappa = 0$ or $S_4 = 0$), the MC equation at degree $4$
is automatically satisfied, and the tower can terminate. If
$\Delta \neq 0$, the degree-$4$ MC equation produces a
non-trivial $S_5$, which feeds degree $5$ to produce $S_6$,
and so on: the tower is infinite. There is no class with
shadow depth $3$ or any finite value $\geq 3$ other than $4$:
the algebraic depth $d_{\mathrm{alg}} \in \{0, 1, 2, \infty\}$
has a gap.

\subsection{Free-field central charges}

The free-field families provide essential boundary data.

\emph{Fermionic $bc$ ghosts} (conformal weights $\lambda$
and $1 - \lambda$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cbc}
% C5: c_bc(lambda) = 1 - 3(2*lambda-1)^2; lambda=1/2 -> 1; lambda=2 -> -26
c_{bc}(\lambda) \;=\; 1 - 3(2\lambda - 1)^2.
\end{equation}
At $\lambda = 1/2$: $c_{bc} = 1$ (a single Dirac fermion).
At $\lambda = 2$: $c_{bc} = -26$ (the reparametrization ghosts
of the bosonic string).

\emph{Bosonic $\beta\gamma$ system} (conformal weights
$\lambda$ and $1 - \lambda$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cbg}
% C6: c_betagamma(lambda) = 2(6*lambda^2 - 6*lambda + 1); lambda=1/2 -> -1; lambda=2 -> 26
c_{\beta\gamma}(\lambda) \;=\; 2(6\lambda^2 - 6\lambda + 1).
\end{equation}
At $\lambda = 1/2$: $c_{\beta\gamma} = -1$ (the symplectic boson).
At $\lambda = 2$: $c_{\beta\gamma} = 26$.

The complementarity relation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bc-bg-complementarity}
% C7: c_betagamma + c_bc = 0 for all lambda
c_{\beta\gamma}(\lambda) + c_{bc}(\lambda) \;=\; 0
\qquad \text{for all } \lambda
\end{equation}
is the ghost cancellation: bosonic and fermionic partners

\subsection{The Virasoro self-dual point}

Under Koszul duality, the Virasoro algebra satisfies
$c \mapsto 26 - c$. The modular characteristic:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vir-kappa-dual}
% AP1: kappa(Vir_c) = c/2; c=13 -> 13/2 self-dual

\end{equation}

and independent of $c$. The self-dual point is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vir-self-dual}
c = 13\colon \quad \kappa = \kappa' = 13/2.
\end{equation}
This is the unique point where the Lagrangian decomposition of
Theorem~\ref{thm:complementarity} is symmetric: the two summands
$Q_g(\mathrm{Vir}_{13})$ and $Q_g(\mathrm{Vir}_{13}^!)$ are

\begin{warning}[The self-dual point is $c = 13$, not $c = 26$]
\label{warn:self-dual}

charges; the self-dual \emph{point} is $c = c' = 13$, the
fixed point of $c \mapsto 26 - c$. Confusing the sum with the
fixed point is a persistent error.
\end{warning}

that are already inner; for currents at generic level, the
Kac--Moody relations make every $J^a$ an independent outer
derivation of the non-current subalgebra.
\end{proof}
