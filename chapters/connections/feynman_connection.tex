\section{Bridge to Feynman diagrams: Heisenberg as free boson QFT}

The genus stratification of the Heisenberg bar-cobar complex reproduces the perturbative loop expansion. The Heisenberg algebra $\mathcal{H}_\kappa$ describes a free massless boson, and its genus-$g$ bar complex corresponds to the $g$-loop Feynman diagrams.

\subsection{The free boson field theory}

The Heisenberg vertex algebra $\mathcal{H}_\kappa$ is the algebraic incarnation of the free boson with action:
\begin{equation}
S[\phi] = \frac{1}{4\pi\kappa} \int d^2z \, \partial\phi \bar{\partial}\phi
\end{equation}

The field $\phi(z, \bar{z})$ is a real scalar, and we decompose into holomorphic/antiholomorphic parts:
\begin{equation}
\phi = \phi_L(z) + \phi_R(\bar{z})
\end{equation}

The holomorphic current is:
\begin{equation}
a(z) = \partial_z \phi_L(z)
\end{equation}

This generates $\mathcal{H}_\kappa$ with:
\begin{equation}
[a(z), a(w)] = \kappa \cdot \partial_z \delta(z - w)
\end{equation}

The \emph{propagator} (two-point function) is:
\begin{equation}
\langle \phi(z) \phi(w) \rangle = -\kappa \log|z - w|^2 + \text{const}
\end{equation}

In complex coordinates:
\begin{equation}
\langle a(z) a(w) \rangle = \partial_z \partial_w \langle \phi(z) \phi(w) \rangle = \frac{\kappa}{(z-w)^2}
\end{equation}

This is the OPE used in the bar-cobar construction.

\subsection{Feynman rules for free boson}

The Feynman rules are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Propagator.} Draw a line between points $z$ and $w$, contributes
\begin{equation}
G(z,w) = \frac{\kappa}{(z-w)^2}
\end{equation}

\item \emph{Vertex.} For the free theory, there are NO vertices (no interaction)

\item \emph{External legs.} Each insertion of $a(z_i)$ is an external leg
\end{enumerate}

\emph{Example.} The 2-point function $\langle a(z_1) a(z_2) \rangle$ corresponds to a single propagator:

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}
\node at (0,0) {$z_1$};
\node at (3,0) {$z_2$};
\draw[thick] (0.3,0) -- (2.7,0);
\node at (1.5, 0.3) {$\frac{\kappa}{(z_1-z_2)^2}$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\subsection{Genus 0 (tree level) diagrams}

At genus 0, we have only tree diagrams. For the free boson, this means:
\begin{itemize}
\item No loops (all diagrams are trees)
\item Each diagram connects $n$ external points via propagators
\item The amplitude is computed by products of propagators $G(z_i, z_j)$
\end{itemize}

\noindent\emph{Bar complex interpretation.}
\begin{equation}
\bar{B}^{(0)}_n = \int_{C_n(\mathbb{P}^1)} \omega \otimes a(z_1) \otimes \cdots \otimes a(z_n)
\end{equation}

The differential $d^{(0)}$ computes tree-level factorization:
\begin{equation}
d^{(0)}[a \otimes a] = \text{Res}_{z_1 \to z_2} \frac{a(z_1) a(z_2)}{z_1 - z_2}
\end{equation}

The propagator $\kappa(z_1-z_2)^{-2}$ combined with the logarithmic form $\eta_{12} \sim (z_1-z_2)^{-1}dz_1$ yields a triple pole, whose residue vanishes:
\begin{equation}
\text{Res}_{z_1 \to z_2} \frac{1}{(z_1-z_2)^3} = 0
\end{equation}

This means tree-level diagrams \emph{do not see the level $\kappa$}---it appears as an overall normalization but not as a quantum correction.

\subsection{Genus 1 (one-loop) diagrams}

At genus $1$, each connected diagram has a single loop. For the free boson, the simplest one-loop diagram is the tadpole:

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.2]
\node at (0,0) {$\bullet$};
\draw[thick] (0,0) arc (180:540:0.5);
\node at (0.5, -1.2) {tadpole};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

The amplitude requires point-splitting regularization of the coincident propagator:
\begin{equation}
A_{\text{tadpole}} = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \langle \partial\phi(z) \partial\phi(z + \epsilon) \rangle = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \frac{\kappa}{\epsilon^2}
\end{equation}

This divergence is the UV divergence of the one-loop vacuum energy.

\emph{Regularized version.} On an elliptic curve $E_\tau = \mathbb{C}/(\mathbb{Z} + \tau\mathbb{Z})$:
\begin{equation}
A_{\text{tadpole}}^{(E_\tau)} = \kappa \sideset{}{'}\sum_{(n,m) \in \mathbb{Z}^2} \frac{1}{(n + m\tau)^2}
\end{equation}

This is (up to constants) the Eisenstein series $E_2(\tau)$, which is \emph{not quite modular} but transforms with an anomaly:
\begin{equation}
E_2\left(-\frac{1}{\tau}\right) = \tau^2 E_2(\tau) + \frac{6\tau}{\pi i}
\end{equation}

The anomaly $\frac{6\tau}{\pi i}$ is a universal property of $E_2$ (independent of $\kappa$).  Since the tadpole amplitude is $\kappa \cdot E_2(\tau)$, the anomaly of the \emph{amplitude} is $\kappa \cdot \frac{6\tau}{\pi i}$, proportional to $\kappa$---this is the one-loop quantum correction to modularity.

\noindent\emph{Bar complex interpretation.}

The tadpole diagram corresponds to the genus-1 element:
\begin{equation}
[\text{Tr}(a \otimes a)]^{(1)} \in \bar{B}^{(1)}_1
\end{equation}

The differential:
\begin{equation}
d^{(0)}[\text{Tr}(a \otimes a)]^{(1)} = \kappa \cdot [1]^{(1)}
\end{equation}

extracts the level as the coefficient of the one-loop divergence.

\subsection{Loop number and genus}

The relationship between loop number and genus is as follows.

\begin{theorem}[Loop-genus correspondence; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere{} \cite{costello-renormalization}]
For any connected Feynman diagram $\Gamma$ with:
\begin{itemize}
\item $V$ vertices
\item $E$ edges (propagators)
\item $F$ faces
\end{itemize}
The number of loops is:
\begin{equation}
L = E - V + 1
\end{equation}
The genus $g$ of the ribbon graph (the closed surface obtained by thickening
and capping boundary components) satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\chi = V - E + F = 2 - 2g, \quad\text{hence}\quad g = \frac{L + 1 - F}{2}
\end{equation}
where $F$ is the number of boundary components (faces). Since $F \geq 1$, we have $g \leq L/2$,
with equality $g = L/2$ when $F = 1$ (single boundary component).
\end{theorem}

\emph{For the free boson:}
\begin{itemize}
\item A tadpole has one vertex ($V = 1$) and one self-loop edge ($E = 1$)
\item Loop count: $L = E - V + 1 = 1 - 1 + 1 = 1$
\end{itemize}

For an interacting theory with $\phi^4$ coupling, a one-loop box diagram has $4$ internal edges forming the loop and $4$ vertices (each 4-valent), giving $L = 4 - 4 + 1 = 1$.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Amplitude expansion in $\kappa$}{Amplitude expansion in}}

The full free boson correlation function expands as:
\begin{align}
\langle a(z_1) \cdots a(z_n) \rangle_{\text{all genera}} &= \sum_{g=0}^\infty \kappa^{n/2+g-1} \langle a(z_1) \cdots a(z_n) \rangle^{(g)}_{\text{conn.}}\\
&= \kappa^{n/2-1}\underbrace{\langle \cdots \rangle^{(0)}}_{\text{tree}} + \kappa^{n/2} \cdot \underbrace{\langle \cdots \rangle^{(1)}}_{\text{1-loop}} + \kappa^{n/2+1} \cdot \underbrace{\langle \cdots \rangle^{(2)}}_{\text{2-loop}} + \cdots
\end{align}

The bar complex homology mirrors this:
\begin{equation}
H_*(\bar{B}(\mathcal{H}_\kappa)) = \bigoplus_{g=0}^\infty H_*(\bar{B}^{(g)}) \cdot \kappa^g
\end{equation}

The genus grading has the following physical interpretation:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\kappa^0$ (genus 0): Classical physics, tree amplitudes
\item $\kappa^1$ (genus 1): First quantum correction, one-loop
\item $\kappa^g$ (genus $g$): $g$-loop quantum corrections
\end{itemize}

\begin{remark}[Convention]
The weighting $\kappa^g$ is the natural grading on the bar filtration: $\bar{B}_g$ is the genus-$g$
piece and $\kappa$ enters as the level parameter.  In closed string theory, the genus expansion instead uses $g_s^{2g-2}$ (the Euler characteristic weight).  For an $n$-point function of the chiral half, the string-theoretic convention would give $g_s^{2g-2+n}$.  The two are related by $\kappa \sim g_s^{-2}$ when comparing the chiral bar complex filtration with string amplitudes (large $\kappa$ corresponds to weak coupling).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Configuration space integrals = Feynman integrals}

The bar complex elements are configuration space integrals. We now make the Feynman diagram connection explicit:

\emph{Genus~0 (tree):}
\begin{equation}
\int_{C_n(\mathbb{P}^1)} \omega(z_1, \ldots, z_n) \prod_{i=1}^n a(z_i)
\end{equation}

This computes tree-level amplitudes. The form $\omega$ encodes the kinematic invariants (like $(z_i - z_j)^{-1}$ propagators).

\emph{Genus~1 (one-loop):}
\begin{equation}
\int_{E_\tau} dz \int_{C_n(E_\tau)} a(z) \otimes a(z_1) \otimes \cdots \otimes a(z_n)
\end{equation}

The first integral $\int_{E_\tau} dz$ is the \emph{loop momentum integral}. On the torus, this becomes:
\begin{equation}
\int_0^1 \int_0^1 d\text{Re}(z) \, d\text{Im}(z)
\end{equation}

summed over lattice points $z \in \mathbb{Z} + \tau\mathbb{Z}$.

\emph{The divergence.} When $z \to z_i$ (loop touches external leg), we get:
\begin{equation}
\sim \int \frac{\kappa}{|z - z_i|^2} dz \sim \text{divergent}
\end{equation}

This is the UV divergence in QFT.

\subsection{Renormalization via compactification}

The bar complex naturally regulates divergences through:

\emph{Fulton--MacPherson compactification.}

\begin{equation}
\overline{C}_n(X)
\end{equation}

The boundary divisors $D_{ij}$ where points collide are replaced by exceptional divisors. Logarithmic forms such as $d\log(z_i - z_j)$ (with simple poles) extend to the compactification and give well-defined integrals, while higher-order poles require additional regularization.  The compactification provides a geometric framework for organizing the divergences:
\begin{itemize}
\item Exceptional divisor = geometric regulator
\item Residue on divisor = renormalization condition
\item Log forms extend; double-pole forms require subtraction
\end{itemize}

The level $\kappa$ enters as the coefficient needing renormalization at one-loop.

\subsection{Higher genus: multi-loop structure}

At genus $g \geq 2$, we have Riemann surfaces $\Sigma_g$ with $g$ handles. Each handle contributes an independent loop integral.

\emph{Two-loop example} ($g=2$). The surface has two handles, giving four independent homology cycles $a_1, b_1, a_2, b_2 \in H_1(\Sigma_2, \mathbb{Z})$ (an $a$-cycle and $b$-cycle for each handle).

The amplitude involves:
\begin{equation}
\int_{\gamma_1} \int_{\gamma_2} \langle a(z_1) a(z_2) \rangle \, dz_1 \, dz_2
\end{equation}

This gives a two-loop Feynman integral with:
\begin{itemize}
\item Two independent loop momenta
\item Nested divergences as loops approach each other
\item Coefficient $\kappa^2$ from two loop momentum integrals
\end{itemize}

\emph{Bar complex:}
\begin{equation}
\bar{B}^{(2)}_n = \int_{\Sigma_2} \int_{C_n(\Sigma_2)} \omega \otimes a(z_1) \otimes \cdots
\end{equation}

The homology (schematic; the actual computation involves cohomology of configuration spaces on genus-$2$ curves, not a simple tensor product):
\begin{equation}
H_*(\bar{B}^{(2)}) \approx \kappa^2 \cdot H^*(\overline{\mathcal{M}}_2) \otimes H^*(\mathcal{A})
\end{equation}

\subsection{Explicit one-loop calculation: partition function}

We now compute the one-loop partition function on the torus.

\emph{Setup.} Free boson on $E_\tau$ with action:
\begin{equation}
S[\phi] = \frac{1}{4\pi\kappa} \int_{E_\tau} d^2z \, |\partial \phi|^2
\end{equation}

\emph{Partition function:}
\begin{equation}
Z_{E_\tau} = \int \mathcal{D}\phi \, e^{-S[\phi]}
\end{equation}

For the Gaussian integral (with zero mode excluded):
\begin{equation}
Z_{E_\tau} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\det'(-\Delta)}}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta$ is the Laplacian on $E_\tau$ and $\det'$ denotes the zeta-regularized determinant with zero eigenvalue excluded.  The level $\kappa$ enters only through the zero-mode integral normalization.

\paragraph{Zeta function regularization.}
\begin{equation}
\det'(-\Delta) = \exp\left( -\frac{d}{ds} \zeta_{\Delta}(s) \Big|_{s=0} \right)
\end{equation}

For the torus:
\begin{equation}
\zeta_\Delta(s) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{2s}} \sum_{(n,m) \neq (0,0)} \frac{\text{Im}(\tau)^s}{|n + m\tau|^{2s}}
\end{equation}

Evaluating:
\begin{equation}
Z_{E_\tau} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\text{Im}(\tau)}} \frac{1}{|\eta(\tau)|^2}
\end{equation}

where $\eta(\tau)$ is the Dedekind eta function:
\begin{equation}
\eta(\tau) = q^{1/24} \prod_{n=1}^\infty (1 - q^n), \quad q = e^{2\pi i \tau}
\end{equation}

\emph{Modular transformation:}
\begin{equation}
\eta\left(-\frac{1}{\tau}\right) = \sqrt{-i\tau} \, \eta(\tau)
\end{equation}

Therefore, using $\mathrm{Im}(-1/\tau) = \mathrm{Im}(\tau)/|\tau|^2$:
\begin{equation}
Z_{E_{-1/\tau}} = Z_{E_\tau}
\end{equation}

The partition function $Z_{E_\tau} = (\mathrm{Im}\,\tau)^{-1/2} |\eta(\tau)|^{-2}$ is modular invariant as a density on the moduli space $\mathcal{M}_{1,0}$: the $|\tau|$ factor from $|\eta(-1/\tau)|^{-2}$ exactly cancels the $|\tau|$ from $(\mathrm{Im}(-1/\tau))^{-1/2}$.

\emph{Bar complex interpretation.}

The partition function $Z_{E_\tau}$ is computed by:
\begin{equation}
Z_{E_\tau} = \langle [\mathbf{1}]^{(1)} \rangle = H_0(\bar{B}^{(1)})
\end{equation}

Since $Z$ is modular invariant as a density, the conformal anomaly is invisible at $c = 1$.  Note that the Heisenberg algebra $\mathcal{H}_\kappa$ has central charge $c = 1$ for any value of the level $\kappa$ (the level scales the OPE but does not change the central charge).  For a theory of general central charge $c$ (e.g., $c$ free bosons), the partition function $Z \propto (\mathrm{Im}\,\tau)^{-c/2}|\eta(\tau)|^{-2c}$ and the anomaly appears in the non-invariant measure factor:
\begin{equation}
\log |\eta(-1/\tau)|^{-2c} - \log |\eta(\tau)|^{-2c} = -c \log|\tau|
\end{equation}

This anomalous transformation is controlled by the central charge class in $H^2(\bar{B}^{(1)})$.

\subsection{String theory perspective}

In string theory, the worldsheet is a Riemann surface $\Sigma_g$. The string amplitude for genus-$g$ worldsheet is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}^{(g)} = \int_{\mathcal{M}_g} d\mu_g \, \langle \mathcal{V}_1 \cdots \mathcal{V}_n \rangle_{\Sigma_g}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathcal{M}_g$ = moduli space of genus-$g$ curves
\item $d\mu_g$ = natural measure on moduli space
\item $\langle \cdots \rangle_{\Sigma_g}$ = worldsheet correlation function
\end{itemize}

\emph{Genus expansion:}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}_{\text{total}} = \sum_{g=0}^\infty g_s^{2g-2} \mathcal{A}^{(g)}
\end{equation}

where $g_s$ is the string coupling.

\emph{Bar-cobar connection.} The bar complex computes exactly these amplitudes:
\begin{equation}
H_*(\bar{B}^{(g)}(\mathcal{H}_\kappa)) \leftrightarrow \mathcal{A}^{(g)}
\end{equation}

The level $\kappa \sim \frac{1}{g_s^2}$ sets the string coupling scale.

\subsection{Path integral interpretation}

\begin{theorem}[Bar complex = path integral for the free boson; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]\label{thm:bar-cobar-path-integral-heisenberg}
For the Heisenberg vertex algebra $\mathcal{H}_\kappa$ \textup{(}free boson at level~$\kappa \neq 0$\textup{)} on a compact Riemann surface $\Sigma_g$ of genus~$g \geq 0$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bar-partition-heisenberg}
Z_g^{\mathcal{H}_\kappa}(\Sigma_g) = H^0(\bar{B}^{(g)}(\mathcal{H}_\kappa)) = \mathbb{C}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bar-npoint-heisenberg}
\langle \alpha(z_1) \cdots \alpha(z_n) \rangle_{\Sigma_g}
= H^n(\bar{B}^{(g)}(\mathcal{H}_\kappa)) / H^0(\bar{B}^{(g)}(\mathcal{H}_\kappa))
\end{equation}
That is, the bar complex cohomology of $\mathcal{H}_\kappa$ at genus~$g$ computes
the same correlation functions as the free boson path integral on~$\Sigma_g$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof matches the bar complex cohomology
\textup{(}Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:heisenberg-higher-genus})} with the
Gaussian path integral, degree by degree.

\emph{Step~1: Bar complex cohomology.}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:heisenberg-higher-genus}, the bar complex
cohomology of $\mathcal{H}_\kappa$ on~$\Sigma_g$ is:
\[
H^n(\bar{B}^{(g)}(\mathcal{H}_\kappa)) = \begin{cases}
\mathbb{C} & n = 0, \\
\mathbb{C}^{2g} & n = 1, \\
\mathbb{C} \oplus \mathbb{C} \cdot c_\kappa^{(g)} & n = 2, \\
0 & n > 2.
\end{cases}
\]
Here $H^1 \cong H^1(\Sigma_g, \mathbb{C})$ and $H^2$ contains
the fundamental class and the central extension class.

\emph{Step~2: Free boson path integral.}
The free boson action on $\Sigma_g$ is $S[\phi] = \frac{\kappa}{2}
\int_{\Sigma_g} \partial\phi \wedge \bar{\partial}\phi$, and the
path integral is Gaussian.  The partition function (normalized) is
$Z_g = 1$ after removing the zero-mode volume, matching
$H^0 = \mathbb{C}$.

The $n$-point functions of the current $\alpha(z) = \partial\phi(z)$
are determined by Wick's theorem:
\begin{itemize}
\item The $1$-point function vanishes: $\langle \alpha(z) \rangle = 0$,
  but the \emph{zero modes} of~$\alpha$ (holomorphic and antiholomorphic
  $1$-forms) give a $2g$-dimensional space, matching $H^1 \cong
  H^1(\Sigma_g, \mathbb{C})$.
\item The $2$-point function $\langle \alpha(z_1)\alpha(z_2) \rangle
  = \kappa \cdot \partial_{z_1}\partial_{z_2} G(z_1, z_2)$, where
  $G(z_1, z_2)$ is the Green's function on $\Sigma_g$, has a
  double pole at $z_1 = z_2$ with residue $\kappa$, matching the bar
  differential residue $d(\alpha \otimes \alpha \cdot \eta_{12})
  = \kappa \cdot 1$ from
  Theorem~\ref{thm:heisenberg-higher-genus}.  The additional class
  $c_\kappa^{(g)} = \kappa \cdot \lambda$ (Hodge class) corresponds to
  the one-loop determinant $(\det'\Delta_{\Sigma_g})^{-1/2}$.
\item For $n > 2$, all connected $n$-point functions vanish by
  Wick's theorem (the theory is free), matching $H^n = 0$.
\end{itemize}

\emph{Step~3: Factorization homology comparison.}
The Heisenberg vertex algebra $\mathcal{H}_\kappa$ defines a
factorization algebra on $\Sigma_g$ in the sense of
Costello--Gwilliam~\cite{CG17}.  By
Theorem~\ref{thm:verdier-AF-compat}, the bar complex cohomology
$H^*(\bar{B}^{(g)}(\mathcal{H}_\kappa))$ computes the factorization
homology $\int_{\Sigma_g} \mathcal{H}_\kappa$.  For the free
boson, the Costello--Gwilliam framework identifies factorization
homology with the BV cohomology of the free scalar field, which is
computed by the Gaussian path integral.  The matching of
Steps~1--2 is therefore not a coincidence but a consequence of the
factorization algebra structure.
\end{proof}

\begin{conjecture}[Bar-cobar as worldsheet path integral: general case; \ClaimStatusConjectured]\label{conj:bar-cobar-path-integral}
For a general chiral algebra $\cA$ on $\Sigma_g$, the bar complex computes:
\begin{equation}
\exp\left( \sum_{g,n} \frac{1}{n!} \int_{C_n(\Sigma_g)} \langle a(z_1) \cdots a(z_n) \rangle_g \right) = \det(\mathbf{1} + \bar{B}(\cA))
\end{equation}

where the right side is the ``determinant'' of the bar complex \textup{(}Fredholm determinant in infinite dimensions\textup{)}.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{remark}[Scope]
\label{rem:bar-path-integral-scope}
For the Heisenberg algebra \textup{(}free boson\textup{)}, the bar-cobar
path integral identification is proved at all genera
\textup{(}Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:bar-cobar-path-integral-heisenberg})},
using the explicit bar complex cohomology
\textup{(}Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:heisenberg-higher-genus})} and the
Gaussian nature of the path integral.

For a general interacting chiral algebra~$\cA$, the conjecture
remains open.  The obstructions are:
\textup{(i)}~the bar complex cohomology at genus $g \geq 2$ involves
non-trivial interactions encoded in the higher $A_\infty$ operations
$m_k$ ($k \geq 3$), for which no closed-form computation is available;
\textup{(ii)}~the ``determinant'' interpretation requires a rigorous
Fredholm determinant for the bar complex as an infinite-dimensional
chain complex;
\textup{(iii)}~for non-free theories, the path integral is not Gaussian
and requires the Costello renormalization framework~\cite{costello-renormalization}
to define rigorously.
\end{remark}
