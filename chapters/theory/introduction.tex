\section{Poincaré Duality and Quantum Field Theory}
\label{sec:nonabelian-poincare-intro}

\subsection{Beyond Classical Poincaré Duality}

Classical Poincaré duality establishes an isomorphism between homology and cohomology:
\begin{equation}
H_k(M) \cong H^{n-k}(M)^\vee
\end{equation}
for an $n$-dimensional closed oriented manifold $M$. This is fundamentally \emph{abelian}—both sides are vector spaces related by a linear duality.

\begin{remark}[Non-Abelian Poincaré Duality]
\label{prin:nonabelian-poincare}
Non-abelian Poincaré duality, in the sense of Ayala-Francis, extends this to a duality between \emph{algebraic structures}:
\begin{equation}
\int_M \mathcal{A} \simeq \left(\int_{-M} \mathcal{A}^!\right)^\vee
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathcal{A}$ is a factorization algebra (encoding local-to-global algebraic data)
\item $\mathcal{A}^!$ is its Koszul dual factorization algebra
\item $-M$ is $M$ with the reversed orientation
\item $\int_M$ denotes factorization homology
\item The duality preserves non-abelian (non-commutative) structure
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Chiral Algebras as Factorization Algebras}

Following Beilinson-Drinfeld and Francis-Gwilliam, a chiral algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on a curve $X$ is equivalently:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{BD Perspective}: A $\mathcal{D}_X$-module with chiral operations defined via residues
\item \textbf{Factorization Perspective}: A factorization algebra on $X$ satisfying:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}(U \sqcup V) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathcal{A}(U) \otimes \mathcal{A}(V)
\end{equation}
for disjoint open sets $U, V \subset X$
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}[Why This Matters]
The factorization property encodes \textbf{locality} of quantum field theory: observations at separated points are independent (factorize). This is the physical content underlying the mathematical structure.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Three-Way Correspondence}

Our central insight is that chiral Koszul duality sits at the nexus of three perspectives:

\begin{center}

\text{Chiral Koszul Duality} \arrow[r, "\text{specializes}"] \arrow[d, "\text{realizes}"'] & \text{Non-Abelian Poincaré Duality} \arrow[d, "\text{via}"] \\
\text{Configuration Space Geometry} \arrow[r, "\text{computes}"'] & \text{Factorization Homology}
\end{tikzcd}

\begin{theorem}[Unification via Configuration Spaces; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{thm:three-way-correspondence}
For a chiral Koszul pair $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A}^!)$ on a curve $X$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Algebraic}: $\Omega(\bar{B}(\mathcal{A})) \simeq \mathcal{A}$ (bar-cobar adjunction)
\item \textbf{Geometric}: Both $\bar{B}(\mathcal{A})$ and $\mathcal{A}^!$ realized via integrals on $\overline{C}_n(X)$
\item \textbf{Homological}: 
\begin{equation}
\int_X \mathcal{A} \simeq \left(\int_{-X} \mathcal{A}^!\right)^\vee
\end{equation}
computed by factorization homology
\end{enumerate}
The three perspectives are equivalent and mutually enriching.
\end{theorem}

\section{The Central Mystery}

In two-dimensional conformal field theory, the most fundamental observables are correlation functions of local operators. When two chiral operators $\phi_1(z_1)$ and $\phi_2(z_2)$ approach each other on a Riemann surface, their correlation functions develop singularities controlled by the operator product expansion (OPE):

$$\phi_1(z_1) \phi_2(z_2) \sim \sum_k \frac{C^k_{12}}{(z_1 - z_2)^{h_1 + h_2 - h_k}} \phi_k(z_2) + \text{regular terms}$$

The structure constants $C^k_{12}$ encode the complete algebraic structure of the chiral algebra. This local singularity data—purely algebraic in nature—turns out to have a natural geometric interpretation that forms the foundation of our work.

\section{The Key Observation}

The key observation is elementary yet profound: the logarithmic differential form $d\log(z_1 - z_2) = \frac{dz_1 - dz_2}{z_1 - z_2}$ has a simple pole precisely when $z_1 = z_2$. When we compute the residue
$$\text{Res}_{z_1=z_2} d\log(z_1 - z_2) \cdot \phi_1(z_1)\phi_2(z_2) = C^k_{12} \phi_k(z_2)$$
we extract exactly the structure constant from the OPE. This simple fact—that algebraic structure constants become geometric residues—motivates our entire construction.

\section{Why Configuration Spaces?}

But why should we expect such a geometric interpretation to exist? The answer lies in a fundamental principle of quantum field theory: locality. The requirement that operators commute at spacelike separation forces the algebraic structure to be encoded in the singularities as operators approach each other. These singularities naturally live on configuration spaces—the spaces parametrizing positions of operators on the curve. The compactification of these spaces, which adds boundary divisors corresponding to collision patterns, provides the geometric arena where quantum algebra becomes algebraic geometry.

\section{Dictionary: Chiral Algebra Types and Three Koszul Dualities}
\label{sec:dictionary}

This monograph works with three levels of chiral algebraic structure, distinguished by
the amount of commutativity imposed on the chiral operations.  We fix terminology and
relate it to the standard operadic language at the outset.

\begin{definition}[$\Einf$-Chiral Algebra = Vertex Algebra]\label{def:einf-chiral}
Throughout this monograph, we work over $k = \mathbb{C}$ and let $X$
denote a smooth algebraic curve over~$\mathbb{C}$.  The ambient
$\infty$-category is $\mathcal{V} = \mathcal{D}\text{-mod}(X)$, the
stable $\infty$-category of (right) $\mathcal{D}_X$-modules, equipped
with the \emph{chiral tensor product}
$\otimes^{\mathrm{ch}}$ \cite[\S3.4]{BD04}.  Duality in this category
means \emph{Verdier duality} $\mathbb{D}$ of holonomic
$\mathcal{D}$-modules (not the na\"{\i}ve linear dual
$\mathrm{Hom}_k(-, k)$); see
Remark~\ref{rem:verdier-vs-linear} below.

An \emph{$\Einf$-chiral algebra} on $X$ is a commutative algebra
object in the chiral tensor category
$(\mathcal{D}\text{-mod}(X), \otimes^{\mathrm{ch}})$.
This notion admits the following equivalent characterizations, each
requiring specific additional structure:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \textbf{BD chiral Lie algebra perspective}
   \cite[\S3.3]{BD04}: a $\mathcal{D}_X$-module $\mathcal{A}$
   equipped with a chiral bracket
   $\mu: j_*j^*(\mathcal{A} \boxtimes \mathcal{A})
   \to \Delta_!\mathcal{A}$ satisfying skew-symmetry and the Jacobi
   identity.  This is a \emph{Lie} algebra object in the chiral
   tensor category; the passage to the commutative viewpoint is via
   the \emph{chiral envelope} functor
   $\mathrm{Ind}: \chirLie\text{-alg} \to \chirCom\text{-alg}$,
   which is an equivalence by
   \cite[Theorem~3.4.9]{BD04}.
\item \textbf{Vertex algebra perspective}: a vertex algebra
   $(V, Y, \mathbf{1}, T)$ satisfying the \emph{locality axiom}:
   for all $a, b \in V$, there exists $N \geq 0$ such that
   $(z-w)^N [Y(a,z), Y(b,w)] = 0$.  The equivalence with~(a)
   requires $X = \mathbb{A}^1$ (or formal disk) and
   \emph{translation-equivariance} of the $\mathcal{D}$-module
   structure; see \cite[\S3.7]{FBZ04}.
\item \textbf{Factorization algebra perspective}: a factorization
   algebra of $\mathcal{D}$-modules on $\operatorname{Ran}(X)$ with
   the symmetric monoidal factorization structure
   (Definition~\ref{def:factorization-algebra-AF}).  The equivalence
   with~(a) is \cite[Theorem~3.4.9]{BD04}; it holds for any
   smooth curve~$X$ and does not require
   translation-equivariance.
\end{enumerate}
The term ``$\Einf$'' reflects the maximal level of factorization
structure imposed on the chiral operations; see
Remark~\ref{rem:En-terminology} below for the precise relationship
to, and distinction from, the standard homotopy-theoretic usage
of $\Einf$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Verdier duality versus linear duality]
\label{rem:verdier-vs-linear}
Throughout this monograph, for a holonomic $\mathcal{D}_X$-module
$\mathcal{M}$, we write $\mathbb{D}(\mathcal{M})$ for the
\emph{Verdier dual} $\mathcal{D}$-module (the derived dual in the
category of $\mathcal{D}$-modules, with the appropriate shift by
$\dim X$).  This is \emph{not} the na\"{\i}ve linear dual
$\mathcal{M}^\vee := \mathrm{Hom}_k(\mathcal{M}, k)$, which is
ill-behaved for infinite-dimensional modules.  When we write
$\mathcal{A}^!$ for the Koszul dual, the underlying duality used
in its construction is always Verdier duality on the configuration
spaces $\overline{C}_n(X)$ (see
Theorem~\ref{thm:bar-cobar-verdier}).
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[$\Eone$-Chiral Algebra = Nonlocal Vertex Algebra]\label{def:e1-chiral}
An \emph{$\Eone$-chiral algebra} on $X$ is an associative (but not necessarily commutative)
algebra object in the chiral compound tensor $\infty$-category of $\mathcal{D}_X$-modules.
Concretely, it is an algebra over the \emph{associative chiral operad} $\chirAss$:
a $\mathcal{D}_X$-module $\mathcal{A}$ with a binary chiral operation
\[
\mu: \mathcal{A} \chirtensor \mathcal{A} \to \Delta_!\mathcal{A}
\]
satisfying $\mu \circ (\mu \otimes \operatorname{id}) = \mu \circ (\operatorname{id} \otimes \mu)$
but \textbf{not} necessarily skew-symmetry.

In vertex algebra language, this is a quadruple $(V, Y, \mathbf{1}, T)$
satisfying vacuum, translation, and weak associativity (the Borcherds identity),
but without the locality axiom.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[The Associative Chiral Operad]\label{def:chiral-ass-operad}
The \textbf{associative chiral operad} $\chirAss$ on a smooth curve $X$ is the
non-$\Sigma$ chiral operad defined as follows.  For each $n \geq 1$,
the space of $n$-ary operations is indexed by \emph{ordered} configurations
in $C_n(X)$:
\[
\chirAss(n) \;=\; \Delta^{(n)}_!\, \omega_X,
\]
where $\Delta^{(n)}: X \hookrightarrow X^n$ is the small diagonal and
the right $\Sigma_n$-action is \emph{not} imposed (the operations are
sensitive to the ordering of inputs).  The operadic composition maps
\[
\circ_i: \chirAss(m) \otimes^{\mathrm{ch}} \chirAss(n) \;\to\;
\chirAss(m+n-1)
\]
are induced by the collision maps on configuration spaces
$C_m(X) \times_{X} C_n(X) \to C_{m+n-1}(X)$ (inserting the $n$-point
configuration at the $i$-th slot), with the chiral tensor product
encoding the singularity structure at coinciding points.

The quadratic presentation is:
\[
\chirAss \;=\; \operatorname{Free}^{\mathrm{ch}}(\mu) \,\big/\,
(\mu \circ_1 \mu \,=\, \mu \circ_2 \mu),
\]
where $\mu \in \chirAss(2)$ is the generating binary operation and
the single relation encodes associativity.  This is the chiral
lift of the classical associative operad $\operatorname{Ass}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[$\chirAss$ Self-Duality; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{prop:chirAss-self-dual}
The associative chiral operad is Koszul self-dual:
\[
(\chirAss)^! \;\cong\; \chirAss \otimes \operatorname{sgn}.
\]
Consequently, $\Eone$--$\Eone$ Koszul duality exchanges $\Eone$-chiral
algebras with $\Eone$-chiral coalgebras, staying within the same
operadic type.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The argument has two steps.

\textbf{Step 1} (Classical Koszul duality of $\operatorname{Ass}$).
The associative operad $\operatorname{Ass}$ is Koszul, with Koszul dual
$\operatorname{Ass}^! \cong \operatorname{Ass} \otimes \operatorname{sgn}$
\cite[Theorem~7.4.1]{LV12}.  This follows from the quadratic presentation
$\operatorname{Ass} = \operatorname{Free}(\mu)/
(\mu \circ_1 \mu - \mu \circ_2 \mu)$:
the relation is symmetric under the Koszul dual involution (which
maps $R \subset E \otimes E$ to $R^\perp$ under the pairing), so the
dual operad has the same generators and relations up to the
sign twist.

\textbf{Step 2} (Chiral lift).
The chiral tensor structure on $\mathcal{D}\text{-mod}(X)$ is compatible
with operadic Koszul duality: taking the Koszul dual of a chiral operad
$\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{ch}}$ defined by generators and quadratic relations
in the chiral tensor category yields
$(\mathcal{P}^{\mathrm{ch}})^! \cong (\mathcal{P}^!)^{\mathrm{ch}}$,
since the orthogonal complement construction commutes with the chiral
tensor product (both are computed fiber-by-fiber on $X^2$, away from
the diagonal, then extended by $j_*$).  Applying this to
$\mathcal{P} = \operatorname{Ass}$ gives
$(\chirAss)^! \cong (\operatorname{Ass}^!)^{\mathrm{ch}}
\cong \chirAss \otimes \operatorname{sgn}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Pro-nilpotence and completeness]\label{rem:pronilpotent-e1}
The bar-cobar adjunction
$\bar{B}: \chirAss\text{-Alg} \rightleftarrows \chirAss\text{-CoAlg}
:\Omega$
is an equivalence of $\infty$-categories when restricted to
\emph{augmented, pro-nilpotent} $\Eone$-chiral algebras and
\emph{conilpotent} $\Eone$-chiral coalgebras.
The pro-nilpotence condition ensures convergence of the cobar
differential (which involves an infinite sum of compositions),
while conilpotence ensures the bar complex is well-defined
degree-wise; see Remark~\ref{rem:three-koszul-mechanisms}(ii)
and Appendix~\ref{app:nilpotent-completion}.
The formal statement and proof are given in
Theorem~\ref{thm:e1-chiral-koszul-duality}.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[$\Pinf$-Chiral Algebra = Chiral Poisson]\label{def:pinf-chiral}
A \emph{$\Pinf$-chiral algebra} is an $\Einf$-chiral algebra equipped with a compatible
$L_\infty$ (homotopy Lie) structure.  This is the chiral analog of a Poisson algebra:
the commutative chiral product and the Lie bracket coexist via a Leibniz rule.
\end{definition}

These form a hierarchy of inclusions:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chiral-hierarchy}
\Einf\text{-chiral} \;\subset\; \Pinf\text{-chiral} \;\subset\; \Eone\text{-chiral}
\end{equation}
Every $\Einf$-chiral algebra is canonically $\Eone$-chiral (by forgetting commutativity).
The converse is false: an $\Eone$-chiral algebra may have $[Y(a,z), Y(b,w)] \neq 0$
for all powers of $(z-w)$.  Deformation quantization passes from $\Pinf$-chiral
(semiclassical) to $\Eone$-chiral (fully quantum, noncommutative).

\begin{theorem}[$\Eone$-Chiral Algebras $=$ Nonlocal Vertex Algebras;
\ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]\label{thm:e1-chiral-nonlocal-va}
Let $X = \mathbb{A}^1$ (or a formal disk).  Then translation-equivariant
$\Eone$-chiral algebras on $X$
(Definition~\ref{def:e1-chiral}) are equivalent to nonlocal vertex algebras:
quadruples $(V, Y, \mathbf{1}, T)$ satisfying the vacuum axiom, translation
axiom, and weak associativity (the Borcherds identity), but
\emph{not} the locality axiom.

More precisely, there is an equivalence of categories:
\[
\left\{
\begin{array}{c}
\text{Translation-equivariant}\\
\chirAss\text{-algebras on } \mathbb{A}^1
\end{array}
\right\}
\;\simeq\;
\left\{
\begin{array}{c}
\text{Nonlocal vertex}\\
\text{algebras over } \mathbb{C}
\end{array}
\right\}
\]
given by $\mathcal{A} \mapsto (\Gamma(\mathbb{A}^1, \mathcal{A}), Y, \mathbf{1}, T)$
where $Y$ is the state-field map extracted from the chiral bracket and
$T = \partial_z$ is the translation operator.

The equivalence specializes, upon imposing skew-symmetry
($\chirAss \to \chirCom$), to the Beilinson--Drinfeld equivalence
between $\Einf$-chiral algebras and vertex algebras
\cite[Theorem~3.4.9]{BD04}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Terminology: $E_n$-chiral algebras versus $E_n$-algebras]\label{rem:En-terminology}
Our use of the notation ``$E_n$-chiral algebra'' follows a filtration by the level
of \emph{factorization structure} imposed on the chiral operations.
An $\Eone$-chiral algebra satisfies weak associativity (algebra over the
associative chiral operad $\chirAss$), while an $\Einf$-chiral algebra satisfies
the full locality axiom of Beilinson--Drinfeld \cite{BD04}, recovering the notion
of vertex algebra.

This terminology must \emph{not} be confused with the standard homotopy-theoretic
usage, where ``$E_\infty$-algebra'' denotes an algebra over the $E_\infty$ operad
(homotopy-commutative up to all higher coherences).  In our setting, the relevant
operad is the \emph{chiral operad} of Beilinson--Drinfeld, and the $E_n$ label
refers to the depth of the factorization constraint---from $\Eone$ (binary
associativity only) through $\Einf$ (full locality/commutativity of the
factorization structure).  The subscript indexes the factorization level, not
the little $n$-discs operad of May--Boardman--Vogt.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Three Koszul duality mechanisms]\label{rem:three-koszul-mechanisms}
Throughout this monograph, ``Koszul duality'' refers to three distinct but interrelated
mechanisms.  To prevent confusion, we distinguish them explicitly:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Francis--Gaitsgory chiral Koszul duality} \cite{FG12}:
an equivalence $\chirLie\text{-alg} \simeq \chirCom\text{-coalg}$ between chiral Lie algebras
and commutative chiral coalgebras, via the Chevalley/Primitives adjunction,
under pro-nilpotence hypotheses.  This operates at the level of operads
($\operatorname{Lie}^! = \operatorname{Com}$) in the chiral tensor category.

\item \textbf{Bar--cobar resolution}: for a dg (chiral) algebra $\mathcal{A}$,
the natural map $\Omega(\bar{B}(\mathcal{A})) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathcal{A}$ is a
quasi-isomorphism under completeness hypotheses.  This is a \emph{universal resolution},
not a ``Koszulness'' property in the Priddy sense.
Every augmented algebra admits a bar construction; the quasi-isomorphism
$\Omega\bar{B} \simeq \operatorname{id}$ holds without quadraticity.

\item \textbf{Quadratic/Priddy duality} $\mathcal{A} \mapsto \mathcal{A}^!$:
for a quadratic (or more generally, augmented) chiral algebra, one constructs a
genuine \emph{Koszul dual algebra} $\mathcal{A}^!$.
When $\mathcal{A}$ is Koszul in the classical sense (the bar spectral sequence collapses
at $E_2$), the bar complex $\bar{B}(\mathcal{A})$ is quasi-isomorphic to $\mathcal{A}^!$.
For non-quadratic algebras, this requires nilpotent completion
(Appendix~\ref{app:nilpotent-completion}).
\end{enumerate}
A referee should be able to determine, for every theorem in this book labeled
``Koszul duality,'' which of (i)--(iii) is being invoked.
The geometric content of this monograph is that all three mechanisms admit
configuration space realizations via the Fulton--MacPherson compactification.
\end{remark}


\section{Main Results and Organization}

Our first result establishes the geometric bar construction for chiral algebras through configuration space integrals. This construction is elementary at its core: we take tensor products of the chiral algebra and integrate logarithmic forms over configuration spaces. The residues at collision divisors extract the algebraic operations:

\begin{theorem}[Geometric Bar Construction, Theorem 3.2; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
For a chiral algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on a smooth curve $X$, we construct a geometric bar complex at the chain level:
$$\bar{B}^{\text{geom}}(\mathcal{A})_n = \Gamma\left(\overline{C}_n(X), \mathcal{A}^{\boxtimes n} \otimes \Omega^*_{\text{log}}\right)$$

\end{theorem}

\section{The Arnold Relations: Foundation of Consistency}

\subsection{Discovery and Significance}

This principle, discovered by V.I. Arnold in studying braid groups, is the cornerstone ensuring $d^2 = 0$ for the bar differential. We provide complete proofs in multiple ways—combinatorial, topological, and operadic—establishing this fundamental identity from different points of view. Each approach illuminates different aspects of the underlying geometry.

The Arnold relations state that certain combinations of logarithmic forms vanish identically:

\begin{theorem}[Arnold-Orlik-Solomon Relations - Fundamental; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
For logarithmic forms $\eta_{ij} = d\log(z_i - z_j)$ on configuration space, and any subset $S \subset \{1, \ldots, n\}$ with distinct $i, j \notin S$:
$$\sum_{k \in S} (-1)^{|k|} \eta_{ik} \wedge \eta_{kj} \wedge \bigwedge_{l \in S\setminus\{k\}} \eta_{kl} = 0$$
where $|k|$ denotes the position of $k$ in the ordering of $S$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Why These Relations Matter}

The Arnold relations are not merely a technical tool—they encode the fundamental consistency of local operator algebras in quantum field theory:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Algebraic Consistency}: They ensure the Jacobi identity for the chiral algebra
\item \textbf{Geometric Consistency}: They guarantee that residue extraction is well-defined independent of the order of operations
\item \textbf{Homological Consistency}: They are precisely the condition for $d^2 = 0$ in the bar complex
\item \textbf{Physical Consistency}: They encode the associativity of the operator product expansion
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Three Perspectives on the Proof}

We establish these relations through three independent proofs, each revealing different aspects:

\textbf{1. Combinatorial Proof (Following Arnold)}:
The relations follow from the elementary identity
$$z_i - z_j = (z_i - z_k) + (z_k - z_j)$$
by taking logarithmic derivatives and carefully tracking the resulting terms. This proof is constructive and yields explicit formulas.

\textbf{2. Topological Proof (Via Stokes' Theorem)}:
Consider the map $S^1 \times C_{|S|}(X) \to C_{|S|+2}(X)$ given by placing points $i$ and $j$ on a small circle. Applying Stokes' theorem to appropriate forms on this space yields the Arnold relations as boundary contributions.

\textbf{3. Operadic Proof (Higher Structure)}:
The configuration space naturally forms an operad with composition given by inserting configurations. The condition that this operad is a complex (has differential squaring to zero) is precisely the Arnold relations.

Complete detailed proofs are provided in Appendix A, with computational examples for small values of $|S|$.

For a chiral algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on a smooth curve $X$, the chiral Hochschild complex is:
$$CH^*(\mathcal{A}) = \text{RHom}_{\mathcal{D}_X}(\bar{B}^{\text{geom}}(\mathcal{A}), \mathcal{A})$$
with differential combining chiral operations and the de Rham differential.
\end{definition}

The geometric realization through our bar construction gives:
$$CH^n(\mathcal{A}) \cong H^n\left(\bar{B}^{\text{geom}}(\mathcal{A}) \otimes_{\mathcal{A}} \mathcal{A}\right)$$

\begin{theorem}[Deformation-Obstruction Theory; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
The chiral Hochschild cohomology controls:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $CH^0(\mathcal{A})$ = center of $\mathcal{A}$ (conserved charges in physics)
\item $CH^1(\mathcal{A})$ = outer derivations $\mathrm{Der}(\mathcal{A})/\mathrm{Inn}(\mathcal{A})$ (infinitesimal symmetries)
\item $CH^2(\mathcal{A})$ = infinitesimal deformations (marginal operators in CFT)
\item $CH^3(\mathcal{A})$ = obstructions to extending deformations
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Periodicity Phenomena}

A remarkable feature of chiral algebras is the appearance of periodicity:

\begin{theorem}[Periodicity in Cohomology; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]\label{thm:periodicity-cohomology}
For the following chiral algebras, the Hochschild cohomology exhibits periodicity:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Virasoro}: $CH^{p+2}(\mathrm{Vir}_c) \cong CH^p(\mathrm{Vir}_c)$ (period~$2$, where $p$ denotes the chiral Hochschild degree)
\item \textbf{Affine Kac-Moody}: $CH^{p+2h}(\widehat{\mathfrak{g}}_k) \cong CH^p(\widehat{\mathfrak{g}}_k)$ at critical level $k = -h^\vee$ (period $2h$, twice the Coxeter number $h$; see proof below)
\item \textbf{W-algebras}: Period determined by the principal grading
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{(1) Virasoro.}
The stress tensor $T$ has conformal weight $h = 2$, so the bar complex is graded by total conformal weight.  The chiral Hochschild cohomology $CH^n(\mathrm{Vir}_c) = H^n(\bar{B}^{\mathrm{ch}}(\mathrm{Vir}_c))$ is computed by integration over $\overline{C}_{n+1}(X)$.  The periodicity operator is multiplication by the class $[T] \in CH^0(\mathrm{Vir}_c)$: since $T$ is the unique (up to scalar) weight-2 primary, the map $\alpha \mapsto \alpha \cdot [T]$ shifts bar degree by 2 and factors through
\[
CH^p(\mathrm{Vir}_c) \xrightarrow{\;[T]\;} CH^{p+2}(\mathrm{Vir}_c).
\]
By Goncharova--Feigin--Fuchs \cite{FF84}, the Lie algebra cohomology $H^*(\mathrm{Witt}, \mathbb{C})$ is an exterior algebra on generators in all odd degrees $\geq 3$: $\Lambda(c_1, c_2, c_3, \ldots)$ with $|c_i| = 2i+1$.  The Godbillon--Vey class in degree~3 is the first generator.  In the chiral setting, the bar complex enriches this by configuration space data; conjecturally, the $p$-th chiral Hochschild group acquires additional structure from $H^*(\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g,n})$ via the moduli space integration.  The period-2 isomorphism $CH^{p+2} \cong CH^p$ is proved in Theorem~\ref{thm:virasoro-periodicity}: at generic~$c$ the Kac determinant is non-vanishing, so the Virasoro representation has no null vectors, which forces the relevant $\mathrm{Ext}$ groups to vanish and the spectral sequence to degenerate at~$E_2$.  The periodicity then follows from the Gel'fand--Fuchs theorem $H^*_{\mathrm{cont}}(L_1; \mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}[\Theta]$.

\textbf{(2) Affine Kac-Moody at critical level.}
At $k = -h^\vee$, the Sugawara construction degenerates (the denominator $k + h^\vee$ vanishes), and the center of $\widehat{\mathfrak{g}}_{-h^\vee}$ becomes large: $Z(\widehat{\mathfrak{g}}_{-h^\vee}) \cong \mathrm{Fun}(\mathrm{Op}_{\check{\mathfrak{g}}}(D^\times))$ (Feigin-Frenkel \cite{FF92}), where $\mathrm{Op}_{\check{\mathfrak{g}}}$ denotes opers for the Langlands dual.  The chiral Hochschild cohomology at critical level is:
\[
CH^n(\widehat{\mathfrak{g}}_{-h^\vee}) \cong H^n(\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}; \mathrm{Fun}(\mathrm{Op}_{\check{\mathfrak{g}}}))
\]
The periodicity with period $2h$ arises from the Coxeter element $w_{\mathrm{Cox}} \in W$ of order $h$ (the Coxeter number): the action of $w_{\mathrm{Cox}}$ on $\mathfrak{h}^*$ has eigenvalues $e^{2\pi i m_j/h}$ where $m_1, \ldots, m_r$ are the exponents.  This induces a $\mathbb{Z}/h$-grading on the relative Lie algebra cohomology, and since $w_{\mathrm{Cox}}^{h} = \mathrm{id}$, the double periodicity $CH^{n+2h} \cong CH^n$ follows.  (For simply-laced $\mathfrak{g}$, $h = h^\vee$ and this coincides with the critical level period; for non-simply-laced $\mathfrak{g}$, $h \neq h^\vee$ in general.)

\textbf{(3) $W$-algebras.}
For $\mathcal{W}^k(\mathfrak{g})$ obtained by Drinfeld-Sokolov reduction, the principal grading on $\mathfrak{g}$ determines the conformal weights of the $W$-algebra generators: $h_1 = 2, h_2 = 3, \ldots, h_r = m_r + 1$ where $m_1, \ldots, m_r$ are the exponents.  The periodicity period is $\mathrm{lcm}(h_1, \ldots, h_r)$, arising from the joint action of the $r$ periodicity operators (one per generator).  For $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}_2$ this gives period $2$ (recovering the Virasoro case), and for $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}_3$ ($W_3$) the period is $\mathrm{lcm}(2,3) = 6$.
\end{proof}

This periodicity reflects hidden structure from the point of view of the genus $0$ theory---the cohomology classes correspond to modular forms of specific weights, with periodicity arising from representation theory of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$.

   for some dimension $d$
\item \textbf{Convergence}: The bar spectral sequence
   $$E_1^{p,q} = H^q(C_{p+1}(X), \mathcal{A}^{\boxtimes(p+1)}) \Rightarrow H^{p+q}(\bar{B}(\mathcal{A}))$$
   converges
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Necessity} (Koszul dual exists $\Rightarrow$ (1)--(4)):

Suppose $\mathcal{A}^! = \widehat{\bar{B}}(\mathcal{A})$ is a well-defined Koszul dual coalgebra with $\Omega(\mathcal{A}^!) \simeq \mathcal{A}$.

\emph{(1) Finite generation:} The cobar construction $\Omega(\mathcal{A}^!)$ is generated as a chiral algebra by $s^{-1}\overline{\mathcal{A}^!}$.  For $\Omega(\mathcal{A}^!) \simeq \mathcal{A}$ to hold, $\mathcal{A}$ must be generated by the cogenerators of $\mathcal{A}^!$.  Since $\mathcal{A}^!$ is a completed coalgebra with finitely many cogenerators in each conformal weight (the completion is over $\mathcal{D}_X$-modules), $\mathcal{A}$ is finitely generated over $\mathcal{D}_X$.

\emph{(2) Formal smoothness:} The chiral Hochschild cohomology $CH^n(\mathcal{A}) \cong \operatorname{Ext}^n_{\mathcal{A}\text{-bimod}}(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A})$ controls the deformation theory of $\mathcal{A}$.  By bar-cobar duality, $CH^n(\mathcal{A}) \cong H^n(\operatorname{Hom}_{\text{coalg}}(\mathcal{A}^!, \mathcal{A}^!))$.  Since $\mathcal{A}^!$ has finite-dimensional graded pieces (from the conilpotent filtration), each $\operatorname{Hom}$ space is finite-dimensional, hence $\dim CH^n(\mathcal{A}) < \infty$.

\emph{(3) Poincaré duality:} Verdier duality on $\overline{C}_n(X)$ provides the non-degenerate pairing.  Specifically, the bar complex $\bar{B}(\mathcal{A})$ carries logarithmic forms, and the cobar complex $\Omega(\mathcal{A}^!)$ carries distributional sections.  The residue pairing $\langle \omega, K \rangle = \int_{\overline{C}_n} \omega \wedge K$ between these is non-degenerate (by Verdier duality on the smooth variety $\overline{C}_n(X)$), giving $CH^i(\mathcal{A}) \times CH^{d-i}(\mathcal{A}) \to \omega_X$ with $d = \dim_\mathbb{C} C_n(X) = n$.

\emph{(4) Convergence:} The bar spectral sequence converges because $\mathcal{A}^!$ exists as a completed coalgebra.  The completion ensures that the inverse system $\{\bar{B}(\mathcal{A})/F_n\}$ satisfies the Mittag-Leffler condition (Theorem~\ref{thm:completion-convergence} in Appendix~\ref{app:nilpotent-completion}), which implies the spectral sequence associated to the conilpotent filtration converges to $H^*(\widehat{\bar{B}}(\mathcal{A}))$.

\medskip
\textbf{Sufficiency} ((1)--(4) $\Rightarrow$ Koszul dual exists):

Given (1)--(4), we construct $\mathcal{A}^!$ and verify bar-cobar inversion.

Condition (1) ensures the bar complex $\bar{B}_n(\mathcal{A})$ is a finitely generated $\mathcal{D}_X$-module in each bar degree (since $\mathcal{D}_X$ is Noetherian on the smooth curve $X$).  Condition (2) provides the polynomial growth bound: $\dim CH^n < \infty$ controls the obstruction theory for extending the bar differential level-by-level.  Together, (1) and (2) establish the convergence criteria of Theorem~\ref{thm:completion-convergence}, so $\widehat{\bar{B}}(\mathcal{A})$ is a well-defined completed dg coalgebra.

Define $\mathcal{A}^! := \widehat{\bar{B}}(\mathcal{A})$.  Condition (3) (Poincaré duality) ensures the Verdier duality identification $\mathbb{D}(\bar{B}(\mathcal{A})) \cong \Omega(\mathcal{A}^!)$ is an isomorphism of dg modules, not merely a quasi-isomorphism.  Condition (4) (spectral sequence convergence) ensures the bar-cobar inversion map $\Omega(\mathcal{A}^!) \to \mathcal{A}$ is a quasi-isomorphism: the spectral sequence computing $H^*(\Omega(\widehat{\bar{B}}(\mathcal{A})))$ converges to $H^*(\mathcal{A})$ by hypothesis, and the bar-cobar adjunction unit provides the comparison map (Theorem~\ref{thm:completed-bar-cobar}).
\end{proof}


\subsection{The Fundamental Bar-Cobar Relationship}

The central result of this monograph is making precise the relationship between chiral algebras in a Koszul pair. We establish not merely that they are "dual" in some abstract sense, but rather that their bar and cobar constructions provide explicit, mutually inverse transformations.

\begin{theorem}[Extended Koszul Duality, Theorem 4.3; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{thm:extended-koszul-intro}
For a chiral Koszul pair $(\mathcal{A}_1, \mathcal{A}_2)$ of chiral algebras, we establish:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{I. The Bar-Cobar Isomorphism:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Bar transforms algebra to dual coalgebra:}
   $$\bar{B}^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}_1) \simeq \mathcal{A}_2^! \quad \text{and} \quad \bar{B}^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}_2) \simeq \mathcal{A}_1^!$$
   as quasi-isomorphisms of chiral coalgebras.

\item \textbf{Cobar reconstructs the dual algebra:}
   $$\Omega^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}_2^!) \simeq \mathcal{A}_1 \quad \text{and} \quad \Omega^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}_1^!) \simeq \mathcal{A}_2$$
   as quasi-isomorphisms of chiral algebras.

\item \textbf{Composition gives quasi-isomorphisms to identity:}
   $$\Omega^{\text{ch}}(\bar{B}^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}_i)) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathcal{A}_i, \quad \bar{B}^{\text{ch}}(\Omega^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}_i^!)) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathcal{A}_i^!$$
   for $i = 1, 2$, establishing that bar and cobar are quasi-inverse equivalences.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{II. How Structures Correspond:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Generators and relations interchange:}
   \begin{itemize}
   \item Generating fields of $\mathcal{A}_1$ correspond to relations of $\mathcal{A}_2$
   \item Relations of $\mathcal{A}_1$ correspond to generating fields of $\mathcal{A}_2$
   \item This explains the slogan: "strong coupling $\leftrightarrow$ weak coupling"
   \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Algebraic operations correspond to coalgebraic operations:}
   \begin{itemize}
   \item Chiral product $\mu: \mathcal{A}_1 \otimes \mathcal{A}_1 \to \mathcal{A}_1$ corresponds to coproduct $\Delta: \mathcal{A}_2^! \to \mathcal{A}_2^! \otimes \mathcal{A}_2^!$
   \item Higher multiplications $m_n$ correspond to higher comultiplications $\Delta_n$
   \item Associativity of products becomes coassociativity of coproducts
   \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{OPE pole orders encode coproduct terms:}
   \begin{itemize}
   \item An OPE singularity $\phi_1(z)\phi_2(w) \sim \frac{a}{(z-w)^k}$ in $\mathcal{A}_1$ becomes a coproduct term in $\mathcal{A}_2^!$
   \item The residue map $\text{Res}_{z=w}$ extracts coproduct coefficients from OPE data
   \item Distribution-valued correlators in $\mathcal{A}_2$ reconstruct OPE structure of $\mathcal{A}_1$
   \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{III. Geometric Realization:}

The abstract isomorphisms are realized geometrically through configuration space integration:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Perfect pairing via integration:}
   $$\langle \omega_{\text{bar}}, K_{\text{cobar}} \rangle = \int_{\overline{C}_n(X)} \omega_{\text{bar}} \wedge \iota^* K_{\text{cobar}}$$
   where $\omega_{\text{bar}} \in \bar{B}^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}_1)$ is a logarithmic form, $K_{\text{cobar}} \in \Omega^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}_2^!)$ is a distribution-valued kernel, and $\iota: C_n(X) \hookrightarrow \overline{C}_n(X)$ is the inclusion of open into compactified configuration space.

\item \textbf{Residues extract coalgebra structure:}
   The differential on the bar side:
   $$d_{\text{bar}} = \sum_{D \in \text{Bdry}} (-1)^{|D|} \text{Res}_D$$
   computes coproduct operations by extracting residues at collision divisors.

\item \textbf{Distributions reconstruct algebra structure:}
   The differential on the cobar side:
   $$d_{\text{cobar}} = \sum_{i<j} \Delta_{ij} \cdot \delta(z_i - z_j)$$
   reconstructs products by inserting distributional singularities.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{IV. Extensions:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Curved algebras:} The duality extends to curved $A_\infty$ structures with curvature element $m_0 \in \mathcal{A}^2$ (a degree-2 element satisfying the curved $A_\infty$ relations $m_1(m_0) = 0$, $m_1^2 = [m_0, -]_{m_2}$)

\item \textbf{Filtered structures:} Koszul pairs of filtered chiral algebras satisfy graded duality at each filtration level

\item \textbf{Higher genus corrections:} At genus $g \geq 1$, quantum corrections enter through period integrals, with complementary deformation-obstruction spaces
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof references]
Parts~I--III are proved in the body of this monograph:
\begin{itemize}
\item Part~I follows from the $\Eone$-chiral Koszul duality theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:e1-chiral-koszul-duality}), the bar-cobar inversion quasi-isomorphism (Theorem~\ref{thm:bar-cobar-inversion-qi}), and the symmetric Koszul duality (Theorem~\ref{thm:symmetric-koszul}).
\item Part~II is a consequence of the bar construction computing the Verdier dual (Theorem~\ref{thm:bar-computes-dual}) and the structure of the coalgebra (Theorem~\ref{thm:coalgebra-via-NAP}).
\item Part~III follows from the geometric bar-cobar construction (Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric-equals-operadic-bar}) and the Verdier duality pairing (Theorem~\ref{thm:verdier-config}).
\item Part~IV: the curved extension uses the completed bar-cobar correspondence (Theorem~\ref{thm:completion-koszul}); the higher genus statement is the Koszul complementarity theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:koszul-complementarity-higher-genus}), proved via the Kodaira--Spencer map (Theorem~\ref{thm:kodaira-spencer-chiral-complete}).
\end{itemize}
\end{proof}



We further establish a fundamental relationship between Koszul duality and quantum corrections:

\begin{theorem}[Koszul Complementarity, Theorem 6.5.1; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
For a Koszul dual pair $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A}^!)$ of chiral algebras on a genus $g$ surface, the spaces of quantum corrections to the Arnold relations satisfy:
$$\mathcal{Q}_g(\mathcal{A}) \oplus \mathcal{Q}_g(\mathcal{A}^!) \cong H^*(\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g, Z(\mathcal{A}))$$
where $Z(\mathcal{A})$ denotes the center of the chiral algebra, viewed as a local system on $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g$.
\end{theorem}

This reveals that Koszul dual chiral algebras have complementary quantum corrections—what one algebra sees as a deformation, its dual sees as an obstruction, and vice versa. This provides a complete classification of quantum corrections through Koszul duality and explains the deep relationship between bosonic and fermionic theories in physics.



\section{Concrete Computational Power}

Throughout the paper we utilize the principle that chiral algebraic structures naturally live on configuration spaces, with the bar-cobar construction providing the dictionary between algebraic and geometric perspectives. This geometric realization transforms abstract algebraic computations into concrete integrations that can be explicitly performed. 

We compute concrete examples that demonstrate the full power of our approach:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The Heisenberg vertex algebra}: We show how the central extension appears geometrically from the failure of logarithmic forms to satisfy exact Arnold relations at genus one
\item \textbf{Free fermions and boson-fermion correspondence}: The bar complex of free fermions is quasi-isomorphic to the Koszul dual coalgebra of symplectic bosons, $\bar{B}^{\text{ch}}(\text{fermions}) \simeq (\text{bosons})^!$, while the cobar construction establishes the inverse relationship $\Omega^{\text{ch}}((\text{fermions})^!) \simeq \text{bosons}$, realizing boson-fermion duality geometrically through the bar-cobar duality adjunction
\item \textbf{$\beta\gamma$ systems}: Complete computation through degree 5, with explicit Koszul dual identification
\item \textbf{W-algebras at critical level}: The bar complex simplifies dramatically, with differential given entirely by screening charges
\item \textbf{Affine Kac-Moody algebras}: We compute their bar complexes and show how quantum deformations arise from higher genus contributions
\end{itemize}


\section{Standing Assumptions and Conventions}
\label{sec:standing-assumptions}

To avoid ambiguity and forward references, we establish the foundational
decisions that govern every construction in this monograph.

\begin{convention}[Standing Assumptions]\label{conv:standing-assumptions}
Unless explicitly stated otherwise, the following assumptions are in force throughout this work.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(SA\arabic*)}]
\item\label{SA:ground-field} The ground field is $\mathbb{C}$.  All vector spaces, algebras, and categories are over $\mathbb{C}$.

\item\label{SA:grading} We work in the cohomological grading convention: differentials have degree $+1$.  The bar construction uses the induced grading with suspension shifting degrees by $-1$.  See Appendix~\ref{app:sign-conventions} for detailed sign rules.

\item\label{SA:convergence} All infinite sums, products, and formal power series are understood in the appropriate completed tensor product or topological vector space.  When we write $\sum_{n \geq 0}$, convergence is in the formal (adic) topology unless stated otherwise.  Configuration space integrals converge absolutely on Fulton--MacPherson compactifications by Theorem~\ref{thm:FM-convergence}.

\item\label{SA:curves} Algebraic curves are smooth, projective, and geometrically connected over $\mathbb{C}$, unless stated otherwise.  ``Genus $g$'' always refers to the arithmetic genus.  Points on curves are closed points.

\item\label{SA:completion} Chiral algebras and vertex algebras are understood in their completed form when genus $g \geq 1$ constructions are involved.  The completion is with respect to the filtration induced by the order of poles at the diagonal; see Appendix~\ref{app:nilpotent-completion} for details.
\end{enumerate}
\end{convention}

\noindent
The subsections below expand on each of these assumptions and establish further
conventions that are used without comment in the body of the text.

\subsection{Ambient Category}
\label{subsec:ambient-category}

We work throughout with \emph{holonomic $\mathcal{D}$-modules} on smooth
algebraic curves over $\mathbb{C}$.  More precisely:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item A \emph{chiral algebra on $X$} is a $\mathcal{D}_X$-module equipped
  with a chiral bracket, in the sense of Beilinson--Drinfeld
  \cite{BD}.\footnote{When we pass to factorization algebras
  (Definition~\ref{def:factorization-algebra-AF}), we use the
  Costello--Gwilliam formulation on the Ran space; the equivalence with the
  BD definition is Theorem~\ref{thm:chiral-ran}.}

\item All $\mathcal{D}$-modules arising from chiral algebras are assumed
  \emph{holonomic} unless stated otherwise.  This ensures that Verdier
  duality $\mathbb{D}$ is well-defined and that the functors $j_!$, $j_*$,
  and the pushforward $\pi_*$ along configuration-space projections all
  exist with the expected exactness properties.

\item The \emph{logarithmic forms} $\omega^{\log}$ on
  Fulton--MacPherson compactifications
  $\overline{C}_n(X)$ are interpreted as sections of the log de Rham
  complex, which is holonomic when regarded as a $\mathcal{D}$-module on the
  compactification.

\item The \emph{cobar construction} is defined intrinsically as the
  Verdier dual of the bar complex in the holonomic $\mathcal{D}$-module
  category (Definition~\ref{def:geom-cobar-intrinsic}).  The distributional
  model (Definition~\ref{def:geom-cobar}) is then a \emph{consequence}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:cobar-distributional-model}): $\delta$-functions
  arise as $\mathcal{D}$-module morphisms under Verdier duality, not as
  naive products of distributions.  In particular, $d_{\mathrm{cobar}}^2 = 0$
  follows from $d_{\mathrm{bar}}^2 = 0$ by exactness of Verdier duality
  (Corollary~\ref{cor:cobar-nilpotence-verdier}), without a separate
  analytic verification.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Grading Convention}
\label{subsec:grading-convention}

We use \textbf{cohomological grading} throughout: the differential has
degree $|d| = +1$, so that a cochain complex is $C^0 \xrightarrow{d}
C^1 \xrightarrow{d} C^2 \xrightarrow{d} \cdots$.

\begin{itemize}
\item The \emph{suspension} is $sV = V[-1]$, so $(sV)^k = V^{k-1}$.
\item The \emph{desuspension} is $s^{-1}V = V[1]$, so $(s^{-1}V)^k = V^{k+1}$.
\item The Koszul sign rule applies: when a map of degree $p$ passes an
  element of degree $q$, the sign $(-1)^{pq}$ is introduced.
\end{itemize}

\begin{remark}
The standard reference Loday--Vallette \cite{LV} uses homological grading
($|d| = -1$).  Our sign conventions are related by the standard dictionary;
see Appendix~\ref{app:signs} for an explicit comparison table.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Augmentation and Completion}
\label{subsec:augmentation-completion}

All chiral algebras and coalgebras in this monograph are \emph{augmented}
unless stated otherwise:

\begin{definition}[Augmented chiral algebra]\label{def:augmented-standing}
An augmented chiral algebra is a pair $(\mathcal{A}, \varepsilon)$ where
$\varepsilon: \mathcal{A} \to \omega_X$ is a chiral algebra morphism
(the augmentation) such that the composition
$\omega_X \xrightarrow{\eta} \mathcal{A} \xrightarrow{\varepsilon} \omega_X$
is the identity.  The \emph{augmentation ideal} is
$\bar{\mathcal{A}} = \ker(\varepsilon)$.
\end{definition}

The bar complex is always the \emph{reduced} bar complex
$\bar{B}^{\mathrm{ch}}(\mathcal{A})$, formed from tensor powers of the
augmentation ideal $\bar{\mathcal{A}}$.

\medskip

\textbf{Completion.}  The bar-cobar quasi-isomorphism
$\Omega^{\mathrm{ch}}(\bar{B}^{\mathrm{ch}}(\mathcal{A})) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathcal{A}$
requires a convergence condition.  Two regimes apply:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \emph{Conilpotent regime}: If the bar coalgebra
  $\bar{B}^{\mathrm{ch}}(\mathcal{A})$ is conilpotent (i.e., the iterated
  coproduct $\Delta^{(N)}$ vanishes on each element for sufficiently large
  $N$), then all sums in the cobar differential are finite and the
  quasi-isomorphism holds without completion.  The Heisenberg algebra and
  affine Kac--Moody at non-critical level are conilpotent.

\item \emph{Completed regime}: When the bar coalgebra is not conilpotent
  (e.g., the Virasoro algebra), one must work with the $I$-adic completion
  $\hat{\mathcal{A}} = \varprojlim_n \mathcal{A}/I^n$ where $I$ is the
  augmentation ideal.  See \S\ref{sec:i-adic-completion} for the
  precise formulation.
\end{enumerate}

Every bar-cobar theorem in this monograph explicitly states which regime
applies.

\subsection{Coordinate Independence}
\label{subsec:coordinate-independence}

The geometric bar complex is defined using logarithmic forms
$\eta_{ij} = d\log(z_i - z_j)$ and residues along boundary divisors of
$\overline{C}_n(X)$.  These expressions use local coordinates, but the
construction is intrinsic:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item The forms $\eta_{ij}$ are sections of $\Omega^1(\log D)$ where
  $D$ is the boundary divisor of the Fulton--MacPherson compactification.
  This sheaf is coordinate-independent.

\item The residue maps $\mathrm{Res}_{D_{ij}}$ are the Poincar\'e residue
  morphisms in the log de Rham complex, which are intrinsically defined.

\item The bar differential $d = d_{\mathrm{int}} + d_{\mathrm{dR}} + d_{\mathrm{res}}$
  is therefore a morphism of sheaves on $\overline{C}_n(X)$, independent of
  coordinate choices.
\end{enumerate}

\label{rem:conventions-summary}

assumes these conventions unless explicitly stated otherwise.  When a
chapter deviates (e.g., specific examples in Part~II that are known to be
conilpotent), this is stated at the point of use.
\end{remark}
