\chapter{Chiral Hochschild cohomology and deformation theory}

\index{Hochschild cohomology!classical}
\index{cyclic homology!chiral}

The chiral Hochschild cohomology $\ChirHoch^*(\cA)$ is the
cohomology of the convolution $L_\infty$-algebra in which the MC
element $\Theta_\cA$ lives.  It is the tangent complex to the MC
moduli at~$\Theta_\cA$, governing deformations, obstructions, and
bulk-field couplings.

\emph{Strictness as shadow}: the classical Gerstenhaber bracket on
Hochschild cochains is the strict shadow of a full $L_\infty$
bracket on the chiral Hochschild complex.  In the strict model,
$\mathrm{HH}^2$ classifies first-order deformations and
$\mathrm{HH}^3$ contains obstructions; in the $L_\infty$ extension,
higher brackets record coherent homotopies that govern the
convergence of formal deformations and the transfer of Koszul
properties across quasi-isomorphisms.
\emph{Modularity at chain level}: the cyclic structure (Connes'
operator~$B$, the mixed complex, the SBI sequence) is not merely
a homological decoration; it is the algebraic shadow of the modular
operad's gluing maps.  The periodicity phenomena discovered later
(Virasoro period~$2$, $\mathcal{W}_N$ period~$N$) are consequences
of the modular structure of the cyclic complex, and the shadow
Postnikov tower of Chapter~\ref{sec:koszul-across-genera} projects
onto the cyclic deformation complex $\mathrm{Def}_{\mathrm{cyc}}^{\mathrm{mod}}(\cA)$
defined here.
\emph{Examples as proving grounds}: every family in the standard
landscape receives its Hochschild computation.  The Heisenberg
complex is acyclic in positive degree (reflecting Gaussian
termination); the Virasoro complex exhibits $2$-periodic cohomology
(Theorem~\ref{thm:virasoro-hochschild}); the W-algebras inherit
$N$-periodicity from their free-field resolutions.  These
computations are the deformation-theoretic counterparts of the
shadow archetypes computed in Part~II.
\emph{Close the loop}: the chiral Hochschild complex is the
deformation-theoretic input to Theorem~H (polynomiality and Koszul
functoriality, Theorem~\ref{thm:hochschild-polynomial-growth}) and to
the modular cyclic deformation complex
(Definition~\ref{def:modular-cyclic-deformation-complex}) that houses
the shadow Postnikov tower.

The fiberwise curvature $\dfib^{\,2} = \kappa \cdot \omega_1$ at
genus~$1$ (Chapter~\ref{ch:heisenberg-frame}) shows that deformations
of the bar complex are controlled by a cohomological invariant.
Hochschild cohomology is the algebraic home of this invariant: it
organizes all infinitesimal deformations and obstructions of a chiral
algebra into a single graded object equipped with a Gerstenhaber
bracket.  The chiral enhancement incorporates OPE data, the geometry
of the curve, and the $A_\infty$ structure of the bar complex.

\section{Classical to chiral}

\subsection{Review of classical Hochschild}

For an associative algebra $A$ over $\mathbb{C}$ and an $A$-bimodule~$M$, the Hochschild cohomology $HH^*(A, M) = \mathrm{Ext}^*_{A^e}(A, M)$ classifies the deformation theory of~$A$: $HH^0$ is the center, $HH^1$ classifies outer derivations, $HH^2$ classifies first-order deformations, and $HH^3$ contains the obstructions to extending them.  The full structure is computed by the bar resolution $\cdots \to A^{\otimes(n+2)} \xrightarrow{b} A^{\otimes(n+1)} \to \cdots$ with differential $b(a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_{n+1}) = \sum_{i=0}^n (-1)^i a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_ia_{i+1} \otimes \cdots \otimes a_{n+1}$, and the resulting cohomology carries a Gerstenhaber bracket governing the Maurer--Cartan equation for formal deformations (Gerstenhaber~\cite{Ger63}).

\subsection{Chiral enhancement}

For chiral algebras, each ingredient of the classical theory acquires a geometric upgrade: the bimodule structure is replaced by the factorization structure on~$\operatorname{Ran}(X)$, the bar resolution becomes the geometric bar complex on Fulton--MacPherson configuration spaces, and the Gerstenhaber bracket lifts to a chiral bracket governed by the OPE.  The result is a chiral Hochschild complex whose cohomology computes chiral deformations, obstructions, and the center of the chiral algebra simultaneously.

\begin{definition}[Chiral Hochschild complex]
For a chiral algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on $X$, the chiral Hochschild complex is:
\[\ChirHoch^*(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{M}) = \text{RHom}_{\mathcal{D}_X}(\barBgeom(\mathcal{A}), \mathcal{M})\]
where $\mathcal{M}$ is a chiral $\mathcal{A}$-module.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Comparison with classical theory {\cite{BD04}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]\label{thm:hochschild-classical-comparison}
There is a spectral sequence:
\[E_2^{p,q} = HH^p(\mathcal{A}_0, H^q(\Omega^*_X)) \Rightarrow \ChirHoch^{p+q}(\mathcal{A})\]
where $\mathcal{A}_0$ is the fiber at a point.
\end{theorem}

\section{Periodicity phenomena}

\subsection{Virasoro periodicity}

\begin{theorem}[Virasoro Hochschild cohomology; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]\label{thm:virasoro-hochschild}
For the Virasoro algebra at generic central charge $c$ (avoiding
$c_{p,q} = 1 - 6(p-q)^2/(pq)$ and $c = 1$):
\[\ChirHoch^{n+2}(\mathrm{Vir}_c) \cong \ChirHoch^n(\mathrm{Vir}_c)
\quad \text{for all } n \geq 0\]
The period is~$2$.  More precisely:
$\ChirHoch^{2k}(\mathrm{Vir}_c) = \mathbb{C}$ and
$\ChirHoch^{2k+1}(\mathrm{Vir}_c) = 0$ for all $k \geq 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is the $r = 1$ case of the W-algebra cohomology theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:w-algebra-hochschild}).  Alternatively, it is an
immediate consequence of Theorem~\ref{thm:virasoro-periodicity}:
that theorem establishes the Weil--Petersson periodicity class
$\Theta \in CH^2(\mathrm{Vir}_c)$ and proves that
$\cup\,\Theta\colon CH^n(\mathrm{Vir}_c) \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} CH^{n+2}(\mathrm{Vir}_c)$
is an isomorphism for all $n \geq 0$ at generic~$c$.  The proof
proceeds in three steps: (1)~the Gel'fand--Fuchs $2$-cocycle
$\Theta(f\,\partial, g\,\partial) = \mathrm{Res}_0(f'''g\,dt)$
defines the periodicity class; (2)~the spectral sequence
$E_2^{p,q} \Rightarrow CH^{p+q}(\mathrm{Vir}_c)$ degenerates at~$E_2$
for generic~$c$ (by the Kac determinant: no null vectors at generic~$c$
implies $\mathrm{Ext}$-vanishing, hence $E_2$~collapse); (3)~the
Gel'fand--Fuchs theorem \cite{FF84} gives
$H^*_{\mathrm{cont}}(L_1;\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}[\Theta]$,
so $\ChirHoch^*(\mathrm{Vir}_c) = \mathbb{C}[\Theta]$ with the stated
periodicity.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Affine Kac--Moody periodicity}

\begin{theorem}[Critical level cohomology; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]\label{thm:critical-level-cohomology}
For $\widehat{\mathfrak{g}}_k$ at critical level $k = \critLevel$, the chiral Hochschild cohomology is the exterior-polynomial algebra
\[\ChirHoch^*(\widehat{\mathfrak{g}}_{\critLevel}) \;\cong\; \Lambda^*(P_1,\ldots,P_r) \otimes \mathbb{C}[\Theta_1,\ldots,\Theta_r]\]
with $\deg P_i = 2m_i + 1$ and $\deg \Theta_i = 2(m_i+1)$, where $m_1,\ldots,m_r$ are the exponents of~$\mathfrak{g}$ (Fuks--Feigin--Tsygan~\cite{FT87}, via BD comparison~\cite{BD04}).  For rank~$1$ ($\mathfrak{sl}_2$): $\ChirHoch^{n+4} \cong \ChirHoch^n$ (strict periodicity).  For rank $r > 1$: polynomial growth $O(n^{r-1})$ without strict periodicity (Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-periodicity-critical}).
\end{theorem}

The exterior-polynomial structure arises from the Coxeter element $w_{\mathrm{Cox}} \in W$ acting on the center at critical level (Feigin--Frenkel~\cite{Feigin-Frenkel}; the exterior-polynomial decomposition follows from Feigin--Frenkel's description of the center $\mathfrak{z}(\widehat{\mathfrak{g}})$ at critical level~\cite{Feigin-Frenkel}).  For simply-laced~$\mathfrak{g}$, $\mathsf{h} = h^\vee$; for non-simply-laced~$\mathfrak{g}$, $\mathsf{h} \neq h^\vee$ in general.

\subsection{W-algebra periodicity}

For W-algebras, the periodicity depends on the conformal weights of the generating fields:

\begin{theorem}[W-algebra cohomology; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]\label{thm:w-algebra-hochschild}
For the principal W-algebra $\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g}) = \Walg^k(\mathfrak{g}, f_{\mathrm{prin}})$ at generic level $k$
(away from admissible and critical levels), the chiral Hochschild cohomology
is a polynomial ring:
\[\ChirHoch^*(\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g}))
\;=\; \mathbb{C}[\Theta_1, \ldots, \Theta_r],
\qquad \Theta_i \in \ChirHoch^{h_i}\]
where $h_i = m_i + 1$ are the conformal weights of the $\Walg$-algebra
generators and $m_1, \ldots, m_r$ are the exponents of $\mathfrak{g}$.
Cup product with each generator $\Theta_i$ gives an injection $\ChirHoch^n \hookrightarrow \ChirHoch^{n+h_i}$, and for rank $r = 1$ (the Virasoro case) this is an isomorphism, yielding periodicity of period $h_1 = 2$.

For example: $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ gives the Virasoro with period~$2$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:virasoro-periodicity}).  For $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ ($\Walg_3$,
generators of weights $2$ and $3$), the dimensions $\dim \ChirHoch^n$
grow polynomially (not periodically): the ring $\mathbb{C}[\Theta_1, \Theta_2]$
has Hilbert function $\#\{(a,b) : 2a + 3b = n\}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We extend the argument of Theorem~\ref{thm:virasoro-periodicity} from the
Virasoro ($\mathfrak{sl}_2$) to general $\mathfrak{g}$.

\emph{Step 1} (Spectral sequence degeneration).
The bar filtration on $\ChirHoch^*(\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g}))$ induces a spectral
sequence
$E_2^{p,q} \Rightarrow \ChirHoch^{p+q}(\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g}))$
with $E_2^{p,q} = 0$ for $q > 0$ at generic $k$.  The vanishing of the
internal factor follows from:
\begin{itemize}
\item At generic $k$, $\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g})$ is \emph{simple}
      as a vertex algebra (Arakawa \cite{Ara07}, Theorem~4.1).
\item At generic~$k$, the Shapovalov form on Verma modules is
      non-degenerate (no singular vectors), so every
      finitely generated $\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g})$-module in category~$\mathcal{O}$
      is a direct sum of irreducible highest-weight modules.
      (This is a property of generic level, not of simplicity per se:
      simplicity of the vertex algebra does not by itself imply
      semisimplicity of the module category.)
\item Consequently $\mathrm{Ext}^q(M, N) = 0$ for $q > 0$ and
      $M, N \in \mathcal{O}_k$, giving $E_2^{p,q} = 0$ for $q > 0$.
\end{itemize}
The spectral sequence degenerates at $E_2$:
$\ChirHoch^n(\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g})) = E_2^{n,0}$.

\emph{Step 2} (Generalized Gel'fand--Fuchs computation).
The $E_2^{n,0}$ page computes the continuous relative Lie algebra cohomology
of the W-algebra Lie algebra.  The principal Drinfeld--Sokolov reduction
produces $r = \mathrm{rank}(\mathfrak{g})$ generators
$W^{(h_i)}$ of conformal weights $h_i = m_i + 1$.  The associated Lie
algebra $\mathrm{Lie}(\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g}))$ is a topological Lie algebra
with $r$ generating currents.

By the generalization of the Gel'fand--Fuchs theorem to Lie algebras of
formal differential operators with $r$ generators (Fuchs 1986, Ch.~2;
Feigin--Fuchs 1984 for the $r = 1$ case), the continuous relative
cohomology is a polynomial ring:
\[H^*_{\mathrm{cont}}(\mathrm{Lie}(\Walg), \mathrm{Lie}(\Walg)_0;\,
\mathbb{C}) \;=\; \mathbb{C}[\Theta_1, \ldots, \Theta_r]\]
where $\Theta_i \in H^{h_i}$ is the Gel'fand--Fuchs cocycle associated
to the $i$-th generator $W^{(h_i)}$.  Each $\Theta_i$ represents the
universal deformation of $W^{(h_i)}$ in the chiral Hochschild complex.

\emph{Step 3} (Cup product structure).
Combining Steps~1 and~2:
\[\ChirHoch^*(\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g}))
= \mathbb{C}[\Theta_1, \ldots, \Theta_r].\]
The cup product maps
$\cup\,\Theta_i\colon \ChirHoch^n \to \ChirHoch^{n+h_i}$
are injective (multiplication in a polynomial ring) and jointly surjective
(every monomial $\Theta_1^{a_1}\cdots\Theta_r^{a_r}$ is reached).
For $r = 1$ this gives literal periodicity of period $h_1$.
For $r \geq 2$, the Hilbert function $H(n) = \dim \ChirHoch^n$
is a quasi-polynomial in~$n$ of period
\[d = \mathrm{lcm}(h_1, \ldots, h_r) = \mathrm{lcm}(m_1+1, \ldots, m_r+1),\]
meaning $H(n)$ depends polynomially on $n$ within each residue class
modulo~$d$.  In particular, $\dim \ChirHoch^n$ grows as $n^{r-1}$
for $r \geq 2$, so there is no literal periodicity; rather, the growth
rate and pattern repeat with period~$d$.

Concretely: for $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}_2$ ($r = 1$, $h_1 = 2$),
$d = 2$, recovering the Virasoro periodicity.  For
$\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}_3$ ($r = 2$, $h_1 = 2$, $h_2 = 3$),
$d = 6$.  For $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}_N$ ($h_i = i+1$, $i = 1,
\ldots, N-1$), $d = \mathrm{lcm}(2, 3, \ldots, N)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Scope]
The proof assembles: (i)~Arakawa's simplicity theorem for W-algebras at
generic level (\ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere, \cite{Ara07});
(ii)~the Beilinson--Drinfeld comparison (\ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere, \cite{BD04});
(iii)~the generalized Gel'fand--Fuchs computation for Lie algebras with
multiple generators (\ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere, \cite{Fuks86}).  For
non-principal nilpotent orbits (Remark below), the simplicity hypothesis
requires case-by-case verification.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Elementary model presentation;
Convention~\ref{conv:proof-architecture}]
\label{rem:theorem-h-model}
\index{chiral Hochschild cohomology!model presentation}
\emph{Step~A} (H-level):
$\ChirHoch^*(\cA)$ is the endomorphism algebra of the
identity functor on factorization $\cA$-modules: it classifies
$A_\infty$-deformations of the chiral algebra~$\cA$.
The polynomial structure
$\ChirHoch^*(\Walg^k(\fg)) \cong \bC[\Theta_1,\ldots,\Theta_r]$
asserts that the formal moduli space of deformations is a smooth
affine space of dimension~$r = \operatorname{rank}(\fg)$.
Each generator~$\Theta_i$ is the universal deformation class
of the $i$-th $\Walg$-generator $W^{(h_i)}$; the polynomial
ring structure says all deformations are unobstructed.

\emph{Step~B} (M-level):
The proof is a chain-level computation: the bar filtration
induces a spectral sequence
$E_2^{p,q} \Rightarrow \ChirHoch^{p+q}(\Walg^k(\fg))$
that collapses at~$E_2$ by generic Ext-vanishing (Arakawa
simplicity~\cite{Ara07}), and the surviving $E_2^{n,0}$ page
computes the continuous Gel'fand--Fuchs cohomology of the
$\Walg$-Lie algebra with~$r$ generating currents
(Fuchs~\cite{Fuks86}).

\emph{Reduction B$\to$A} (M-level $\to$ H-level):
The M-level bar complex
$\bar{B}_{\mathrm{HH}}(\cA) = (\cA \otimes T^c(s^{-1}\bar\cA)
\otimes \cA,\, d)$ is a dg model for the derived endomorphism
object $R\!\operatorname{End}_{\cA\text{-}\cA}(\cA)$.
By Proposition~\ref{prop:model-independence}, the H-level
endomorphism algebra is recovered from the M-level bar complex
up to contractible ambiguity, so the M-level spectral sequence
collapse determines the H-level polynomial structure.

\emph{Step~C} (S-level):
The Hilbert function
$\dim \ChirHoch^n(\Walg^k(\fg))
= \#\{(a_1,\ldots,a_r) : \sum a_i h_i = n\}$
grows as $n^{r-1}/(h_1 \cdots h_r)$ and is quasi-polynomial of
period $d = \operatorname{lcm}(h_1,\ldots,h_r)$.
For $\fg = \mathfrak{sl}_2$: literal period~$2$.
For $\fg = \mathfrak{sl}_3$: $1,0,1,1,1,1,2,1,2,2,\ldots$
with quasi-period~$6$.

\emph{Reduction B$\to$C} (M-level $\to$ S-level):
The S-level Hilbert function is extracted from the M-level
spectral sequence by taking the Euler characteristic of
$E_2^{n,0}$: the collapse at~$E_2$ ensures
$\dim \ChirHoch^n = \dim E_2^{n,0}$, and the Gel'fand--Fuchs
computation identifies~$E_2^{n,0}$ with the partition function
of~$r$ generators of weights~$h_1,\ldots,h_r$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Non-principal nilpotent orbits]
For general $\Walg^k(\mathfrak{g}, f)$ with $f$ not necessarily principal, the conformal weights of the generators are determined by the Kazhdan grading associated to the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triple $(e, h, f)$.  The periodicity period is then $d = \mathrm{lcm}(h_1, \ldots, h_s)$ where $h_1, \ldots, h_s$ are the resulting conformal weights.  When the generic-level non-coboundary condition can be verified for a given orbit, the same argument applies; for special orbits this remains an open problem.
\end{remark}

\section{Deformation theory}

\subsection{Infinitesimal deformations}

\begin{theorem}[Deformation classification {\cite{Ger63,Loday98}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]\label{thm:deformation-classification}
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\ChirHoch^1(\mathcal{A})$ classifies \emph{outer derivations} $\mathrm{Der}(\mathcal{A})/\mathrm{Inn}(\mathcal{A})$ (not infinitesimal automorphisms, which are all derivations).
\item $\ChirHoch^2(\mathcal{A})$ classifies first-order deformations.
\item $\ChirHoch^3(\mathcal{A})$ contains the primary obstructions to extending deformations.
\item Unobstructed deformations correspond to marginal operators in CFT.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{example}[Marginal deformations of beta-gamma]
For the $\beta\gamma$ system:
\[\ChirHoch^2(\beta\gamma) = \mathbb{C} \cdot [\beta\gamma]\]
The class $[\beta\gamma]$ corresponds to the exactly marginal operator changing the conformal weights.
\end{example}

\subsection{Formal deformation theory}

The formal deformation space is controlled by the differential graded Lie algebra
$\mathfrak{g}_{\mathcal{A}} = \ChirHoch^*(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A})[1]$
with bracket induced by the cup product.

\begin{theorem}[Maurer--Cartan equation {\cite{Kon03,KontsevichSoibelman}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]\label{thm:maurer-cartan-deformations}
Formal deformations correspond to solutions of:
\[d\alpha + \frac{1}{2}[\alpha, \alpha] = 0, \quad \alpha \in \mathfrak{g}_{\mathcal{A}}^1\]
\end{theorem}

\section{Physical applications}

\subsection{Marginal operators and RG flow}

In CFT, marginal operators have dimension $(1,1)$ and correspond to
$\ChirHoch^2_{\text{marginal}}(\mathcal{A}) = \{\omega \in \ChirHoch^2 : h(\omega) = 1\}$.
The beta function vanishes if and only if the obstruction in $\ChirHoch^3$ vanishes.

\subsection{String field theory}

The conjectural identification of the bar complex with BRST cohomology (Conjecture~\ref{conj:master-bv-brst}) takes the form:
\[H^*_{\text{BRST}}(\text{String}[\mathcal{A}]) \cong \ChirHoch^*(\mathcal{A})\]

String vertices are encoded in the $A_\infty$ structure: $m_2$ gives the three-string vertex, $m_3$ the four-string contact term, and higher $m_k$ the multi-string interactions.

\section{Hochschild-cyclic spectral sequence for chiral algebras}
\label{sec:hochschild-cyclic-spectral}

\begin{remark}[Physical origin]
In 2d~CFT on a genus~$g$ surface $\Sigma_g$, the partition function
$Z[\Sigma_g] = \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathcal{H}}(q^{L_0-c/24})$ is computed by the
Hochschild-cyclic spectral sequence: the $E_1$ page gives the tree-level (genus~0)
contribution, $E_2$ incorporates one-loop corrections, and higher pages capture
multi-loop effects.  The spectral sequence degenerates at $E_2$ precisely when the
theory is modular invariant.
\end{remark}

\begin{construction}[Geometric realization via loop spaces]
The Hochschild complex of a chiral algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on a curve $X$ is:
\[\text{HC}_n^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}) = \Gamma(C_n(X), 
   \mathcal{A}^{\boxtimes n} \otimes \det(\Omega^1_{C_n(X)/X}))\]

This has a natural $S^1$-action (cyclic rotation of points), giving:
\[\text{HC}_n^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}) = 
   [\text{Maps}(S^1, LX) \otimes_{\mathcal{A}} \mathcal{A}^{\otimes n}]^{S^1}\]
where $LX = \text{Maps}(S^1, X)$ is the loop space.

The cyclic spectral sequence computes $H_*(LX, \mathcal{A})$ by first computing 
$H_*(\text{Maps}(S^1, X), \mathcal{A})$ and then taking $S^1$-invariants.

Hochschild homology computes the homology of the free loop space, and cyclic homology computes the $S^1$-equivariant loop space homology.
This identification is classical for associative algebras \cite{Loday98}; the chiral extension follows from the factorization structure of the loop space \cite[Chapter~5]{CG17}.
\end{construction}

\begin{computation}[Explicit second-page computation; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
We compute the $E_2$ page explicitly for standard examples:

\emph{Heisenberg algebra.}
\[\text{HH}_*^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{H}) = \mathbb{C}[c] \otimes \Lambda(\sigma)\]
where $|c| = 2$ (central charge) and $|\sigma| = 1$ (desuspension of $S^1$).

The Connes operator $B: \text{HH}_n \to \text{HH}_{n+1}$ (degree $+1$) satisfies $B(c) = 0$,
$B(\sigma) = c$, giving:
\[\text{HC}_*^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{H}) = \mathbb{C}[c]\]
(polynomials in the central charge).

\emph{Free fermion $\beta\gamma$ system.}
\[\text{HH}_*^{\text{ch}}(\beta\gamma) = \Lambda(\beta_0, \gamma_0) \otimes \mathbb{C}[c]\]
where $\beta_0, \gamma_0$ are zero modes. Cyclic homology adds periodicity:
\[\text{HC}_*^{\text{ch}}(\beta\gamma) = \mathbb{C}[c] \otimes
   \mathbb{C}[\beta_0^{-1}, \gamma_0^{-1}]_{\text{formal}}\]
(the localization $\mathbb{C}[\beta_0, \gamma_0][\beta_0^{-1}, \gamma_0^{-1}]$ at the zero-mode variables, completed formally).

The zero-mode periodicity in $\text{HC}_*^{\text{ch}}(\beta\gamma)$ reflects the symplectic pairing of the $\beta\gamma$ system: the localized zero modes span the deformation space of the Lagrangian splitting.
\end{computation}

\begin{remark}[Functoriality]
The Hochschild-cyclic spectral sequence is functorial: a morphism
$f\colon \cA \to \mathcal{B}$ induces $f_*\colon E_r(\cA) \to E_r(\mathcal{B})$,
tensor products satisfy $E_r(\cA \otimes \mathcal{B}) \cong E_r(\cA) \otimes E_r(\mathcal{B})$
up to higher structure, and Koszul duality gives $E_r(\cA^!) \cong E_r(\cA)^\vee$.
The Hochschild complex represents the functor
$\operatorname{Bimod}_{\cA} \ni M \mapsto \operatorname{Hom}_{\operatorname{Bimod}}(\operatorname{HH}(\cA), M)$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Hochschild complex for chiral algebras}

\begin{definition}[Chiral Hochschild complex]
\label{def:chiral-hochschild}
\index{Hochschild cohomology!chiral|textbf}
For a chiral algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on a smooth curve $X$, the \emph{chiral
Hochschild complex} is:
\[\text{CH}_n(\mathcal{A}) = \Gamma\left(\overline{C}_{n+1}(X), 
   \mathcal{A}^{\boxtimes(n+1)} \otimes \det\left(\Omega^1_{\overline{C}_{n+1}(X)/X}\right)\right)\]

with differential:
\[d_{\text{HH}}(\omega) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} (-1)^i \left[\text{omit } i\text{-th factor}\right]\]

More explicitly, using the chiral product $\mu_{ij}$:
\begin{align*}
d_{\text{HH}}(a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n) 
&= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} (-1)^i (a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes \mu(a_i, a_{i+1}) 
   \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n) \\
&\quad + (-1)^n (\mu(a_n, a_0) \otimes a_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_{n-1})
\end{align*}

The last term implements the \emph{cyclic structure}: $a_n$ wraps around to multiply $a_0$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Hochschild complex is a chain complex; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{thm:hochschild-chain-complex}
The identity $d_{\text{HH}}^2 = 0$ is the Hochschild analogue of the Arnold-relation mechanism that ensured $d^2 = 0$ for the bar differential (\S\ref{sec:frame-bar-deg2}): adjacent face maps cancel in pairs.  In the chiral setting, the same cancellation acquires OPE corrections from the higher poles.
The differential $d_{\text{HH}}$ satisfies $d_{\text{HH}}^2 = 0$, making
$(\text{CH}_*(\mathcal{A}), d_{\text{HH}})$ a chain complex.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We must show that for any $\omega \in \text{CH}_n(\mathcal{A})$:
\[d_{\text{HH}}^2(\omega) = 0\]

\emph{Step 1: Expand $d_{\text{HH}}^2$}

Applying $d_{\text{HH}}$ twice gives:
\begin{align*}
d_{\text{HH}}^2(a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n) 
&= d_{\text{HH}}\left(\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} (-1)^i (\cdots \otimes \mu(a_i, a_{i+1}) 
   \otimes \cdots)\right. \\
&\quad\left. + (-1)^n (\mu(a_n, a_0) \otimes a_1 \otimes \cdots)\right)
\end{align*}

\emph{Step 2: Identify canceling pairs}

After expanding, terms come in two types:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Type A}: Apply $\mu$ at positions $(i,i+1)$ then $(j,j+1)$ with $j \neq i, i+1$
\item \emph{Type B}: Apply $\mu$ twice at adjacent triples $(i,i+1,i+2)$
\end{enumerate}

\emph{Type A terms cancel} because applying $\mu$ at position~$i$ first reduces the chain length by~1, so the second application at position~$j$ (with $j > i+1$) picks up index $j-1$:
\[(-1)^i (-1)^{j-1} (\ldots) +
   (-1)^j (-1)^{i} (\ldots) = (-1)^{i+j-1} + (-1)^{i+j} = 0\]
(the index shift from the first contraction produces the sign difference)

\emph{Type B terms cancel} by the Borcherds identity (the chiral analog of associativity).
For three elements $a_i, a_{i+1}, a_{i+2}$ at pairwise distinct points $z_i, z_{i+1}, z_{i+2}$,
the Type~B contributions are:
\begin{align*}
&(-1)^i(-1)^{i+1}(\cdots \otimes \mu_{z_i, z_{i+2}}(\mu_{z_i, z_{i+1}}(a_i,a_{i+1}),a_{i+2}) \otimes \cdots) \\
&+ (-1)^{i+1}(-1)^i (\cdots \otimes \mu_{z_i, z_{i+2}}(a_i,\mu_{z_{i+1}, z_{i+2}}(a_{i+1},a_{i+2})) \otimes \cdots)
\end{align*}

The signs give $(-1)^{2i+1} = -1$ for the first and $(-1)^{2i+1} = -1$ for the second,
so the total contribution is:
\[-\bigl(\mu(\mu(a_i,a_{i+1}),a_{i+2}) - \mu(a_i,\mu(a_{i+1},a_{i+2}))\bigr)\]

By the Borcherds identity \cite[Section~3.4.7]{BD04}
(equivalently: the factorization algebra structure
encodes \emph{homotopy} associativity via the codimension-1 boundary strata
of $\overline{C}_3$), the iterated products
$\mu(\mu(a_i,a_{i+1}),a_{i+2})$ and $\mu(a_i,\mu(a_{i+1},a_{i+2}))$
agree up to the contribution of the boundary stratum $\partial \overline{C}_3$,
which is accounted for by the Hochschild complex structure
(the face maps encode precisely these boundary identifications).
Thus the two terms cancel in the Hochschild complex, giving $d_{\text{HH}}^2 = 0$.

\emph{Note}: Unlike the classical case, the chiral product is \emph{not} strictly associative;
the Borcherds identity provides associativity only up to total derivatives
(equivalently, up to homotopy). The cancellation above relies on the
factorization algebra axioms rather than strict associativity.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Chiral Hochschild homology]
\label{def:chiral-HH}
The \emph{chiral Hochschild homology} of $\mathcal{A}$ is:
\[\text{HH}_n^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}) = H_n(\text{CH}_*(\mathcal{A}), d_{\text{HH}})\]
\end{definition}

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Cyclic structure and $S^1$-action}{Cyclic structure and S 1-action}}

\begin{definition}[Cyclic operator]
\label{def:cyclic-operator}
The \emph{cyclic operator} $t: \text{CH}_n(\mathcal{A}) \to \text{CH}_n(\mathcal{A})$ 
is defined by:
\[t(a_0 \otimes a_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n) = (-1)^n (a_n \otimes a_0 \otimes a_1 
   \otimes \cdots \otimes a_{n-1})\]

This generates a $\mathbb{Z}/(n+1)$-action on $\text{CH}_n(\mathcal{A})$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Cyclic operator commutes with Hochschild differential; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{lem:cyclic-commutes}
The cyclic operator commutes with the Hochschild differential:
\[t \circ d_{\text{HH}} = d_{\text{HH}} \circ t\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Direct computation using the definition of $d_{\text{HH}}$:
\begin{align*}
t(d_{\text{HH}}(a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n))
&= t\left(\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} (-1)^i (\cdots \otimes \mu(a_i,a_{i+1}) \otimes \cdots) 
   + (-1)^n (\mu(a_n,a_0) \otimes \cdots)\right)
\end{align*}

After applying $t$ (cyclic permutation), each term shifts indices: $i \to i+1 \pmod{n+1}$.

Similarly:
\[d_{\text{HH}}(t(a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n)) = 
   d_{\text{HH}}((-1)^n(a_n \otimes a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_{n-1}))\]

After accounting for Koszul signs from moving $a_n$ past $a_0, \ldots, a_{n-1}$, 
the two expressions are identical.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Connes operator B]
\label{def:connes-B}
\index{cyclic homology!Connes operator|textbf}
The \emph{Connes operator} $B: \mathrm{CH}_n(\mathcal{A}) \to \mathrm{CH}_{n+1}(\mathcal{A})$ is defined as
\[B = (1-t_{n+1}) \circ s \circ N_n\]
where $N_n = \sum_{i=0}^{n} t^i$ is the norm operator and $s: \mathrm{CH}_n \to \mathrm{CH}_{n+1}$ is the extra degeneracy $s(a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n) = 1 \otimes a_0 \otimes \cdots \otimes a_n$. The identities $B^2 = 0$ and $Bd + dB = 0$ hold (Theorem~\ref{thm:connes-exact-sequence}).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Connes mixed-complex structure {\cite{Connes85,Loday98}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{thm:connes-exact-sequence}
The pair $(d_{\mathrm{HH}}, B)$ equips $\mathrm{CH}_*(\mathcal{A})$ with the
structure of a \emph{mixed complex}: $d^2 = B^2 = dB + Bd = 0$.
This gives rise to a short exact sequence of complexes:
\[0 \to \mathrm{CC}_*(\mathcal{A}) \to \mathrm{CC}_*^{\mathrm{per}}(\mathcal{A})
\to \mathrm{CC}_*^{\mathrm{neg}}(\mathcal{A})[2] \to 0\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathrm{CC}_* = \mathrm{CH}_* \otimes k[u]/(d + uB)$ is the cyclic complex
  ($|u| = -2$)
\item $\mathrm{CC}_*^{\mathrm{per}} = \mathrm{CH}_* \otimes k((u))/(d+uB)$ is the
  periodic cyclic complex
\item $\mathrm{CC}_*^{\mathrm{neg}}$ is the negative cyclic complex
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Provenance and citation]
This theorem is imported and treated as \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere. Standard
presentations of Connes' mixed-complex formalism and the associated exact
sequence are in \cite{Connes85,Loday98}.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Connes SBI exact sequence {\cite{Connes85,Loday98}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{cor:connes-SBI}
There is a long exact sequence relating Hochschild and cyclic homology:
\[\cdots \to \mathrm{HH}_n(\mathcal{A}) \xrightarrow{I} \mathrm{HC}_n(\mathcal{A}) \xrightarrow{S} \mathrm{HC}_{n-2}(\mathcal{A}) \xrightarrow{B} \mathrm{HH}_{n-1}(\mathcal{A}) \to \cdots\]
where $S$ is the periodicity operator, $B$ is Connes' operator, and $I$ is the natural inclusion. In particular, $S$ is an isomorphism in degrees where $\mathrm{HH}_*$ vanishes, yielding $2$-periodicity of periodic cyclic homology $\mathrm{HP}_* = \varprojlim_S \mathrm{HC}_{*+2k}$.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{The Hochschild-cyclic spectral sequence}

\begin{theorem}[Hochschild-cyclic spectral sequence {\cite{Connes85,Loday98}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{thm:HC-spectral-sequence}
\index{spectral sequence!Hochschild-cyclic}
The cyclic structure induces a spectral sequence:
\[E_1^{p,q} = \text{HH}_{q}^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A}) \otimes \Lambda^p(\mathbb{C} \cdot \sigma)\]
converging to:
\[E_\infty^{p,q} \Rightarrow \text{HC}_{p+q}^{\text{ch}}(\mathcal{A})\]

The $E_1$ differential is:
\[d_1: E_1^{p,q} \to E_1^{p-1,q}\]
\[d_1(\omega \otimes \sigma^p) = B(\omega) \otimes \sigma^{p-1}\]
where $B$ is Connes' operator.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Provenance and citation]
This theorem is imported and treated as \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere. The
spectral sequence attached to the mixed complex $(\mathrm{CH}_*,d_{\mathrm{HH}},B)$
is standard in the cyclic-homology literature; see \cite{Connes85,Loday98}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$E_2$ page: explicit computation}{E2 page: explicit computation}}

\begin{theorem}[Second-page formula {\cite{Loday98}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{thm:E2-page-formula}
The $E_2$ page of the Hochschild-cyclic spectral sequence is:
\[E_2^{p,q} = \begin{cases}
\text{HH}_q(\mathcal{A})^{S^1} & \text{if } p = 0 \\
\text{HH}_{q-1}(\mathcal{A})_{S^1} & \text{if } p = 1 \\
0 & \text{if } p \geq 2
\end{cases}\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $(-)^{S^1}$ denotes $S^1$-invariants (fixed points)
\item $(-)_{S^1}$ denotes $S^1$-coinvariants ($S^1$-orbits)
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Provenance and citation]
This theorem is imported and treated as \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere. The $E_2$
identification in terms of invariants/coinvariants of the circle action is the
standard computation for the cyclic spectral sequence (see \cite{Loday98}).
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Second page for the Heisenberg algebra]
\label{ex:E2-heisenberg}
For the Heisenberg chiral algebra $\mathcal{H}$ at level $k$:

\emph{Step 1: Compute Hochschild homology}
\[\text{HH}_*(\mathcal{H}) = \mathbb{C}[a_{-n}]_{n \geq 1} \otimes \mathbb{C} \cdot \mathbf{1}\]
where $a_{-n}$ are negative modes of the Heisenberg field $a(z) = \sum_n a_n z^{-n-1}$.

In more invariant terms:
\[\text{HH}_*(\mathcal{H}) = \text{Sym}^*(H_1(S^1, \mathbb{C})) \otimes \Lambda(H_0(S^1, \mathbb{C})) = \mathbb{C}[c] \otimes \Lambda(\sigma)\]
where $|c| = 2$ (from the $S^1$ homology) and $|\sigma| = 1$ (from the constant functions), consistent with the Serre duality computation.

\emph{Step 2: $S^1$-action}

The $S^1$-action on $\text{HH}_*(\mathcal{H})$ is trivial on the center, so:
\[\text{HH}_*(\mathcal{H})^{S^1} = \mathbb{C}[c]\]
\[\text{HH}_*(\mathcal{H})_{S^1} = \mathbb{C}[c]\]

\emph{Step 3: $E_2$ page}
\[E_2^{0,q} = \mathbb{C}[c] \cap \{|*| = q\} = \begin{cases}
\mathbb{C} & \text{if } q = 0, 2, 4, \ldots \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}\]

\[E_2^{1,q} = \mathbb{C}[c] \cap \{|*| = q-1\} = \begin{cases}
\mathbb{C} & \text{if } q = 1, 3, 5, \ldots \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}\]

The $E_2$ page is therefore:
\[E_2 = \begin{array}{c|cc}
q \backslash p & 0 & 1 \\
\hline
0 & \mathbb{C} & 0 \\
1 & 0 & \mathbb{C} \\
2 & \mathbb{C} & 0 \\
3 & 0 & \mathbb{C} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots
\end{array}\]

The spectral sequence \emph{degenerates at $E_2$} (all higher differentials are zero)
because the only possible higher differentials $d_r: E_r^{1,q} \to E_r^{0,q+r}$ would require $r \geq 2$, but $E_r^{p,q} = 0$ for $p \geq 2$, so all higher differentials vanish.

\emph{Comparison with Hochschild cohomology.}
The polynomial $E_2$ page above should not be confused with the ordinary Hochschild cohomology $HH^*(\mathcal{H})$, which is computed in Example~\ref{ex:HH-heisenberg-complete}.  The latter uses the Koszul resolution (length~$2$) and gives $HH^n(\mathcal{H}) = 0$ for $n \geq 3$, i.e., $HH^*(\mathcal{H}) \cong \mathbb{C}[\kappa]/(\kappa^2)$.  The difference is that the $E_2$ page here is computed from Hochschild \emph{homology} $HH_*(\mathcal{H}) = \mathbb{C}[c] \otimes \Lambda(\sigma)$, which is polynomial and does not vanish in high degrees.  Hochschild cohomology (the $\mathrm{Ext}$-computation) and Hochschild homology (the $\mathrm{Tor}$-computation) are distinct invariants; the former detects deformations, while the latter feeds the cyclic spectral sequence.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Second page for affine sl3]
\label{ex:E2-sl3}
\index{$\mathfrak{sl}_3$!Hochschild-cyclic spectral sequence}
For $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_3$ at generic level~$k$:

\emph{Step~1: Hochschild homology.}
By the continuous cohomology of current algebras, $\mathrm{HH}_*^{\mathrm{ch}}(\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_3)$ is determined by the Lie algebra cohomology $H^*(\mathfrak{sl}_3; \mathbb{C}) = \Lambda(x_3, x_5)$ (exterior algebra on generators of degrees $3$ and $5$, corresponding to the exponents $m_1 = 1$, $m_2 = 2$ of $\mathfrak{sl}_3$) tensored with the polynomial algebra from the loop direction.  This gives:
\[\mathrm{HH}_*^{\mathrm{ch}}(\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_3)
= \mathbb{C}[\Theta_2, \Theta_3] \otimes \Lambda(\sigma_3, \sigma_5)\]
where $|\Theta_j| = j$ (from the Casimir operators of degrees $2$ and $3$, corresponding to the exponents $m_1 = 1$, $m_2 = 2$) and $|\sigma_{2i+1}| = 2i+1$ (from $H^*(\mathfrak{sl}_3)$).

\emph{Step~2: $S^1$-action and $E_2$ page.}
The $S^1$-action on the polynomial part is trivial (Casimirs are invariant).
The exterior generators $\sigma_3, \sigma_5$ lie in odd degrees and are
$S^1$-coinvariants:
\[E_2^{0,*} = \mathbb{C}[\Theta_2, \Theta_3], \qquad
E_2^{1,*} = \mathbb{C}[\Theta_2, \Theta_3].\]

\emph{Step~3: Degeneration.}
At generic~$k$, $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_3$ is simple (Ext-vanishing in
category~$\mathcal{O}$), so the spectral sequence degenerates at~$E_2$ by
the same argument as for $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_2$.  The Hilbert function
of $\mathrm{HC}_n = E_2^{0,n} \oplus E_2^{1,n-1}$ gives:
\[\dim \mathrm{HC}_n^{\mathrm{ch}}(\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_3)
= \#\{(a,b) : 2a + 3b = n\} + \#\{(a,b) : 2a + 3b = n-1\}\]
which grows linearly in~$n$ (rank~$2$), in contrast to the constant
$\dim \mathrm{HC}_n = 1$ for $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}}_2$ (rank~$1$).
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Second page for lattice VOAs]
\label{ex:E2-lattice}

For a lattice VOA $V_\Lambda$ of rank~$d$, the underlying Heisenberg

\subsection{Cyclic homology exchange under Koszul duality}

\begin{corollary}[Cyclic homology duality; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]\label{cor:cyclic-homology-duality}
\index{cyclic homology!Koszul duality}
For a Koszul pair $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A}^!)$, the Connes periodicity operator\index{Connes periodicity operator} $S$ intertwines the Koszul duality on Hochschild homology with a $[-2]$-shifted duality on cyclic homology:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cyclic-duality}
\mathrm{HC}_n(\mathcal{A}) \;\cong\; \mathrm{HC}_{-n}(\mathcal{A}^!)^{\vee}
\end{equation}
for all $n$, where $\mathrm{HC}_*$ denotes periodic cyclic homology.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The SBI exact sequence (Corollary~\ref{cor:connes-SBI}) relates $\mathrm{HH}_*$ and $\mathrm{HC}_*$ via the periodicity operator $S$.  The Koszul duality for Hochschild cohomology (Theorem~\ref{thm:main-koszul-hoch}) gives $\mathrm{HH}^n(\mathcal{A}) \cong \mathrm{HH}^{2-n}(\mathcal{A}^!)^\vee \otimes \omega_X$.  Inserting this into the SBI sequence for $\mathcal{A}$ and comparing with the SBI sequence for $\mathcal{A}^!$: the periodicity operator $S$ shifts degree by $-2$ on both sides, so the duality on $\mathrm{HH}_*$ descends to periodic cyclic homology as stated.  The shift by~$2$ in the Hochschild duality is absorbed by the periodicity $S$, yielding the unshifted duality~\eqref{eq:cyclic-duality} on $\mathrm{HP}_* = \varprojlim_S \mathrm{HC}_{*+2k}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:cyclic-koszul-virasoro}
For the Virasoro algebra $\mathrm{Vir}_c$ and its same-family partner
$\mathrm{Vir}_{26-c}$: both have $\mathrm{CH}^* \cong \mathbb{C}[\Theta]$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:virasoro-hochschild}), so the periodic cyclic
homology is $2$-periodic on both sides.  The duality~\eqref{eq:cyclic-duality}
exchanges the periodicity generators $\Theta \leftrightarrow \Theta'$,
consistent with the Verdier anti-commutativity of the Kodaira--Spencer
class (Theorem~\ref{thm:kodaira-spencer-chiral-complete}).  Here
$\mathrm{Vir}_{26-c}$ is the proved M/S-level shadow partner used in the
current Hochschild calculation; the stronger H-level infinite-generator
realization is delegated to the exact $W_\infty$ MC4 package of
filtered H-level targets, residue identities, and finite detection on
$\mathcal{I}_N$, whose first live higher-spin stages are already
reduced on the theorem surface to the stage-$3$ fifteen-coefficient
packet and the stage-$4$ six-coefficient packet organized as three
local OPE blocks.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Hochschild cup product exchange; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{cor:hochschild-cup-exchange}
\index{Hochschild cohomology!cup product exchange}
For a Koszul pair $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{A}^!)$, let $\Theta \in CH^2(\mathcal{A})$ be a periodicity class \textup{(}as in Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:virasoro-periodicity})}.  Under the Koszul duality isomorphism $CH^n(\mathcal{A}) \cong CH^{2-n}(\mathcal{A}^!)^\vee \otimes \omega_X$ \textup{(}Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:main-koszul-hoch})}, the cup product with $\Theta$ corresponds to the cup product with the dual class $\Theta' \in CH^2(\mathcal{A}^!)$ up to sign:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cup-product-exchange}
\Theta \;\longleftrightarrow\; -\Theta'
\end{equation}
under the Verdier anti-commutativity of the Kodaira--Spencer class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The Kodaira--Spencer class $\mathrm{KS}_g(\mathcal{A}) \in H^1(\overline{\mathcal{M}}_g, Z(\mathcal{A}))$ satisfies $\mathrm{KS}_g(\mathcal{A}) = -\mathrm{KS}_g(\mathcal{A}^!)$ under the Verdier involution (Theorem~\ref{thm:kodaira-spencer-chiral-complete}, equation~\eqref{eq:verdier-ks-anticommute}).  The periodicity class $\Theta$ arises from the $g = 0$ contribution to the Kodaira--Spencer class via the bar complex on $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{0,4} \cong \mathbb{P}^1$ (proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:virasoro-periodicity}, Step~1).  The Verdier anti-commutativity descends to the periodicity generators: $\Theta$ on the $\mathcal{A}$ side corresponds to $-\Theta'$ on the $\mathcal{A}^!$ side.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Virasoro verification]\label{rem:cup-exchange-virasoro}
For $\mathrm{Vir}_c$ with $\ChirHoch^* = \mathbb{C}[\Theta]$ and its

applications of the anti-commutativity $\Theta \leftrightarrow

(Corollary~\ref{cor:cyclic-homology-duality}), where the sign is
absorbed by the Connes periodicity operator $S$.
\end{remark}

\section{Derived center and Hochschild cochains (M4)}
\label{sec:derived-center-hochschild-cochains}

\index{derived center|textbf}
\index{Morita equivalence!Hochschild cohomology}
\index{compact generator|textbf}

The derived center of a dg-category is the derived endomorphisms of
the identity functor.  For a category with a compact generator, this
reduces, via the Morita equivalence, to the Hochschild cochain
complex of the endomorphism algebra.  The argument is categorical in
nature and depends on three classical inputs: Morita theory for
dg-categories, the identification of endofunctors with bimodules, and
the definition of Hochschild cochains as bimodule self-Ext.

\subsection{Compact generators and the Morita equivalence}

\begin{definition}[Compact generator]\label{def:compact-generator}
Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a $k$-linear dg-category admitting all cones,
shifts, and retracts (i.e.\ a pretriangulated dg-category).  An
object $b \in \mathcal{C}$ is a \emph{compact generator} if:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \emph{Compactness}: the functor
  $\RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b, -)\colon \mathcal{C} \to \Ch(k)$
  preserves arbitrary coproducts (equivalently, filtered colimits);
\item \emph{Generation}: if $\RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b, c) \simeq 0$
  for an object $c \in \mathcal{C}$, then $c \simeq 0$.
\end{enumerate}
Write $A := \RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b, b)$ for the endomorphism
dg-algebra.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Morita equivalence {\cite{Keller06,Toen07}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{prop:morita-equivalence-compact-gen}
\index{Morita equivalence|textbf}
Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a pretriangulated dg-category and
$b \in \mathcal{C}$ a compact generator with
$A = \RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b, b)$.  Define the functor
\[
\Psi\colon \mathcal{C} \;\longrightarrow\;
\mathrm{Perf}(A), \qquad
\Psi(c) := \RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b, c),
\]
where $\mathrm{Perf}(A)$ denotes the dg-category of perfect
\textup{(}compact\textup{)} right $A$-modules.  Then $\Psi$ is a
quasi-equivalence of dg-categories.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The argument has two steps.

\emph{Fully faithful.}
For any $c, c' \in \mathcal{C}$, the map
\[
\Psi_{c,c'}\colon \RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(c, c')
\;\longrightarrow\;
\RHom_A(\RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b,c),\, \RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b,c'))
\]
is the composition map.  When $c = b$, the right-hand side is
$\RHom_A(A, \Psi(c')) \simeq \Psi(c')$, which equals the left-hand
side $\RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b, c')$ by definition.  The full
subcategory of objects $c$ for which $\Psi_{c,-}$ is an isomorphism
contains~$b$ and is closed under cones, shifts, and retracts (since
$\RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(-, c')$ and $\Psi$ both preserve these
operations).  Since $b$ generates~$\mathcal{C}$, the subcategory
is all of~$\mathcal{C}$.

\emph{Essentially surjective.}
The image of $\Psi$ contains the free module
$A = \Psi(b)$ and is closed under cones, shifts, and retracts (since
$\Psi$ is an exact functor of pretriangulated categories).  Every
perfect $A$-module is built from $A$ by finitely many cones, shifts,
and retracts, so $\Psi$ is essentially surjective onto
$\mathrm{Perf}(A)$.

The result is standard: see Keller~\cite{Keller06},
Schwede--Shipley~\cite{SS03},
and To\"en~\cite{Toen07} for the $\infty$-categorical refinement.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The derived center}

\begin{definition}[Derived center of a dg-category]
\label{def:derived-center}
\index{derived center|textbf}
For a $k$-linear dg-category $\mathcal{C}$, the \emph{derived center}
is the dg-algebra of derived endomorphisms of the identity functor:
\[
Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C})
:= \RHom_{\mathrm{Fun}(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{C})}
   (\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}},\, \mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}}).
\]
A degree-$n$ cocycle in $Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C})$ is a
natural transformation $\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}} \Rightarrow
\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}}[n]$; its cohomology class is a derived
natural transformation.  The product on $Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C})$
is composition of natural transformations; in particular,
$H^0(Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C}))$ is the ordinary center of the
homotopy category $[\mathcal{C}]$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Endofunctors as bimodules}

The bridge between the derived center and Hochschild cochains is the
identification of endofunctors with bimodules.

\begin{proposition}[Endofunctor--bimodule equivalence {\cite{Toen07,BZFN10}};
\ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{prop:endofunctor-bimodule}
\index{bimodule!endofunctor equivalence}
For a dg-algebra~$A$, there is a quasi-equivalence of dg-categories
\[
\mathrm{Fun}_{\mathrm{cocont}}(\mathrm{Perf}(A),\,
\mathrm{Perf}(A))
\;\simeq\; A\text{-}\mathrm{bimod}^{\mathrm{perf}},
\]
where the left-hand side consists of cocontinuous \textup{(}colimit-preserving\textup{)} dg-functors
$\mathrm{Perf}(A) \to \mathrm{Perf}(A)$, and the right-hand side
is the dg-category of perfect $A$-$A$-bimodules
\textup{(}equivalently, perfect $A^e$-modules, where
$A^e = A \otimes A^{\mathrm{op}}$\textup{)}.  The equivalence sends
a bimodule $M$ to the tensor functor $- \otimes_A^{\mathbf{L}} M$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The Eilenberg--Watts theorem for dg-categories: every cocontinuous
functor $F\colon \mathrm{Perf}(A) \to \mathrm{Perf}(A)$ is
represented by the bimodule $M_F := F(A)$, where $A$ carries its
canonical left module structure and $M_F$ inherits a right
$A$-structure from the right action of $A = \End(A)$ on the free
module.  The assignment $F \mapsto F(A)$ is quasi-inverse to
$M \mapsto (- \otimes_A^{\mathbf{L}} M)$.  See
To\"en~\cite{Toen07} and Ben-Zvi--Francis--Nadler~\cite{BZFN10}
for the $\infty$-categorical statement.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Identity functor $=$ diagonal bimodule]
\label{cor:identity-diagonal}
Under the equivalence of
Proposition~\textup{\ref{prop:endofunctor-bimodule}}, the identity
functor $\mathrm{Id}\colon \mathrm{Perf}(A) \to \mathrm{Perf}(A)$
corresponds to the diagonal bimodule ${}_A A_A$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The identity functor sends the free module~$A$ to itself:
$\mathrm{Id}(A) = A$.  The corresponding bimodule is therefore~$A$,
with left $A$-action by left multiplication and right $A$-action by
right multiplication.  This is the diagonal bimodule.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The main identification}

\begin{theorem}[Derived center $=$ Hochschild cochains;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{thm:derived-center-hochschild}
\index{derived center!Hochschild identification}
\index{Hochschild cohomology!derived center}
Let\/ $\mathcal{C}$ be a pretriangulated dg-category with compact
generator $b$, and set $A = \RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b, b)$.  There is a
natural quasi-isomorphism of dg-algebras
\begin{equation}\label{eq:derived-center-HH}
Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C})
\;\simeq\;
\mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A, A)
:= \RHom_{A^e}(A, A).
\end{equation}
In particular, the derived center depends on $\mathcal{C}$ only up to
Morita equivalence.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof assembles the three preceding results.

\emph{Step~1} (Morita reduction).
By Proposition~\ref{prop:morita-equivalence-compact-gen}, $b$ being a
compact generator gives a quasi-equivalence
$\Psi\colon \mathcal{C} \xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} \mathrm{Perf}(A)$.
The derived center is manifestly invariant under quasi-equivalence of
dg-categories (a quasi-equivalence $\Psi$ induces a quasi-isomorphism
$\Psi_*\colon Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C})
\xrightarrow{\sim} Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathrm{Perf}(A))$
by conjugation: $\alpha \mapsto \Psi \circ \alpha \circ \Psi^{-1}$),
so
\[
Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C})
\;\simeq\;
Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathrm{Perf}(A))
= \RHom_{\mathrm{Fun}(\mathrm{Perf}(A), \mathrm{Perf}(A))}
  (\mathrm{Id},\, \mathrm{Id}).
\]

\emph{Step~2} (Endofunctors $=$ bimodules).
By Proposition~\ref{prop:endofunctor-bimodule}, cocontinuous
endofunctors of $\mathrm{Perf}(A)$ are identified with
$A$-bimodules.  Applying $\RHom$ in the functor category translates
to $\RHom$ in the bimodule category:
\[
\RHom_{\mathrm{Fun}}(\mathrm{Id},\, \mathrm{Id})
\;\simeq\;
\RHom_{A\text{-}\mathrm{bimod}}
  (M_{\mathrm{Id}},\, M_{\mathrm{Id}}).
\]

\emph{Step~3} (Identity $\mapsto$ diagonal).
By Corollary~\ref{cor:identity-diagonal}, $M_{\mathrm{Id}} = {}_A A_A$
is the diagonal bimodule.  Therefore:
\[
Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathrm{Perf}(A))
\;\simeq\;
\RHom_{A^e}(A, A)
= \mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A, A).
\]

\emph{Naturality.}  Each step is natural: the Morita equivalence is
natural in the generator~$b$, the endofunctor--bimodule equivalence is
natural in~$A$, and $\RHom$ is a bifunctor.  The composite
isomorphism~\eqref{eq:derived-center-HH} is therefore natural
in~$\mathcal{C}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Provenance]
\label{rem:derived-center-provenance}
The identification $Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C}) \simeq
\mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A)$ is implicit in Keller~\cite{Keller06} and
made explicit in the framework of $(\infty,1)$-categories by
Ben-Zvi--Francis--Nadler~\cite{BZFN10} and
To\"en~\cite{Toen07}.  The argument presented here distills the
essential chain of identifications without passing through the full
$(\infty,2)$-categorical machinery.  For the factorization-algebra
refinement relevant to chiral algebras, see
Costello--Gwilliam~\cite[Chapter~5]{CG17}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Independence of generator: Morita invariance of
Hochschild cohomology}

\begin{theorem}[Morita invariance of $\mathrm{HH}^\bullet$;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{thm:morita-invariance-HH}
\index{Hochschild cohomology!Morita invariance|textbf}
\index{Morita equivalence!Hochschild invariance}
Let $A$ and $A'$ be dg-algebras that are Morita equivalent, meaning
there exists a dg-category $\mathcal{C}$ with compact generators
$b, b'$ such that $A \simeq \RHom(b,b)$ and
$A' \simeq \RHom(b',b')$.  Then there is a natural quasi-isomorphism
\[
\mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A, A) \;\simeq\; \mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A', A').
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:derived-center-hochschild}, both sides are
quasi-isomorphic to $Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C})$, which depends
only on the dg-category~$\mathcal{C}$ and not on the choice of
generator.  Concretely, the chain of quasi-isomorphisms is:
\[
\mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A)
\xleftarrow{\;\sim\;}
Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathcal{C})
\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}
\mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A').
\]
\end{proof}

For the explicit bimodule construction of the Morita transfer map, we
record the following refinement.

\begin{proposition}[Explicit Morita transfer; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{prop:explicit-morita-transfer}
\index{Morita equivalence!explicit transfer map}
Let $P = \RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b, b')$ and
$P^\vee = \RHom_{\mathcal{C}}(b', b)$ be the bimodules implementing
the Morita equivalence, so that
$P \otimes_{A'}^{\mathbf{L}} P^\vee \simeq A$ and
$P^\vee \otimes_A^{\mathbf{L}} P \simeq A'$.  The Morita transfer
map
\begin{equation}\label{eq:morita-transfer-map}
\Phi\colon \mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A)
\;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A'),
\qquad
f \;\longmapsto\;
P^\vee \otimes_A^{\mathbf{L}} f \otimes_A^{\mathbf{L}} P,
\end{equation}
is a quasi-isomorphism of dg-algebras.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We verify $\Phi$ is well-defined, multiplicative, and a
quasi-isomorphism.

\emph{Well-definedness.}
An element $f \in \mathrm{HH}^n(A) = \RHom_{A^e}(A, A)^n$
is an $A$-bimodule map $A \to A[n]$.  The conjugation
$\Phi(f) := P^\vee \otimes_A f \otimes_A P$ is the composition
\[
A' \simeq P^\vee \otimes_A P
\xrightarrow{\mathrm{id} \otimes f \otimes \mathrm{id}}
P^\vee \otimes_A A[n] \otimes_A P
\simeq P^\vee \otimes_A P[n]
\simeq A'[n],
\]
which uses $A \otimes_A P \simeq P$ and
$P^\vee \otimes_A A \simeq P^\vee$ (left/right unit).  The
composite is an $A'$-bimodule map $A' \to A'[n]$, hence an element
of $\mathrm{HH}^n(A')$.

\emph{Multiplicativity.}
For $f, g \in \mathrm{HH}^\bullet(A)$, the composition
$\Phi(f) \circ \Phi(g)$ is
$P^\vee \otimes_A f \otimes_A P \circ P^\vee \otimes_A g \otimes_A P$.
Using $P \otimes_{A'} P^\vee \simeq A$ to contract the inner
$P$-$P^\vee$ pair:
\[
\Phi(f) \circ \Phi(g)
= P^\vee \otimes_A f \otimes_A \underbrace{(P \otimes_{A'} P^\vee)}_{\simeq A}
  \otimes_A g \otimes_A P
\simeq P^\vee \otimes_A (f \circ g) \otimes_A P
= \Phi(f \circ g).
\]

\emph{Quasi-isomorphism.}
The inverse map $\Phi^{-1}(g) = P \otimes_{A'} g \otimes_{A'} P^\vee$
is constructed symmetrically.  Since
$P \otimes_{A'} P^\vee \simeq A$ and
$P^\vee \otimes_A P \simeq A'$, we have
$\Phi^{-1} \circ \Phi \simeq \mathrm{id}$ and
$\Phi \circ \Phi^{-1} \simeq \mathrm{id}$, using the same
contraction.  Hence $\Phi$ is a quasi-isomorphism.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Chiral application]\label{rem:derived-center-chiral}
\index{derived center!chiral application}
For a chiral algebra $\cA$ on a curve~$X$, the module category
$\mathrm{Mod}_\cA$ (Definition~\ref{def:module-categories}) is a
pretriangulated dg-category.  When $\mathrm{Mod}_\cA$ is compactly
generated with compact generator $\mathrm{Free}_\cA(k)$,
Theorem~\ref{thm:derived-center-hochschild} gives
\[
Z_{\mathrm{der}}(\mathrm{Mod}_\cA)
\;\simeq\;
\ChirHoch^\bullet(\cA, \cA).
\]
This identification controls the deformations of the
\emph{category} $\mathrm{Mod}_\cA$, not merely the algebra~$\cA$
itself: an element of $\ChirHoch^2(\cA)$ deforms the identity functor
on modules, hence the entire module theory.

\emph{Compact generation for the standard families.}
The hypothesis is verified in the following cases:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)}]
\item \emph{Heisenberg $\mathcal{H}_k$} ($k \neq 0$):
  the module category is equivalent to $\mathrm{Mod}_{\mathrm{Weyl}}$
  (Weyl algebra modules), which is compactly generated by the
  vacuum module~$F_0$.  (The Weyl algebra is Morita-simple.)
\item \emph{Universal affine $V_k(\fg)$} at generic~$k$:
  the category $\mathrm{KL}_k(\fg)$ (Kazhdan--Lusztig category) is
  compactly generated by generalized Verma modules~\cite{KL93}.
  At non-generic (e.g., admissible)
  levels, compact generation holds by the Arakawa--Kawasetsu
  theorem~\cite{AK18} ($C_2$-cofiniteness for the simple quotient
  $L_k(\fg)$).
\item \emph{Virasoro $\mathrm{Vir}_c$} at generic~$c$:
  the free-field resolution (Feigin--Fuchs) provides a
  compact generator.  At rational~$c$, compact generation
  follows from $C_2$-cofiniteness of the minimal model
  $L(c_{p,q}, 0)$~\cite{Miyamoto04}.
\item \emph{Principal $\mathcal{W}$-algebras}: compact generation
  follows from the quantum Drinfeld--Sokolov reduction, which
  preserves compact generation when the BRST complex is
  well-defined~\cite{Arakawa15}.
\end{enumerate}
For the universal algebras at generic level (the primary setting
of this monograph), compact generation is unconditional.
The hypothesis fails only for pathological algebras with
uncountably-generated module categories, which do not arise in
the Lie-theoretic landscape.
\end{remark}

\section{Factorization homology on the annulus: Hochschild chains (M7)}
\label{sec:annulus-hochschild-chains}

\index{factorization homology!annulus|textbf}
\index{Hochschild homology!excision|textbf}
\index{excision!factorization homology|textbf}

The complementary side of Hochschild theory is homological: while
Hochschild cochains are the derived center (deformations of the
identity functor), Hochschild chains are the derived trace
(factorization homology over the circle).  The excision axiom for
factorization homology reduces the circle computation to a tensor
product over an interval, yielding the identification of factorization
homology on $S^1$ with Hochschild chains.

\subsection{Factorization homology: recollections}

\begin{definition}[Factorization homology over a manifold]
\label{def:factorization-homology-manifold}
\index{factorization homology|textbf}
For a locally constant factorization algebra $\mathcal{F}$ on a
manifold~$M$ with values in a symmetric monoidal
$\infty$-category~$\mathcal{V}$, the \emph{factorization homology}
of $\mathcal{F}$ over~$M$ is the global sections:
\[
\int_M \mathcal{F}
:= \mathcal{F}(M)
\;\in\; \mathcal{V}.
\]
When $\mathcal{F}$ is constructible rather than locally constant,
this is the homotopy colimit of $\mathcal{F}$ over the
Weiss topology on~$M$
\textup{(}Ayala--Francis~\cite{AF15},
Costello--Gwilliam~\cite{CG17}\textup{)}.
\end{definition}

The factorization homology functor satisfies two fundamental
properties that make it computable: \emph{local constancy} (the
value on a disk is determined by the $\En$-algebra structure) and
\emph{excision} (the value on a glued manifold is computed by a
derived tensor product).

\begin{theorem}[Excision; {\cite[Theorem~3.18]{AF15}}; \ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{ax:excision}
\index{excision!for factorization homology}
Let $M = M_+ \cup_N M_-$ be a collar-gluing decomposition: $N$ is a
codimension-$1$ manifold with a collar neighborhood in~$M$, and
$M_\pm$ are the two halves.  Then there is a natural equivalence
\[
\int_M \mathcal{F}
\;\simeq\;
\int_{M_+} \mathcal{F}
\;\otimes^{\mathbf{L}}_{\int_N \mathcal{F}}\;
\int_{M_-} \mathcal{F}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\subsection{The circle computation by excision}

\begin{theorem}[Factorization homology on $S^1$ $=$ Hochschild chains;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{thm:circle-fh-hochschild}
\index{factorization homology!circle}
\index{Hochschild homology!factorization homology}
Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a locally constant factorization algebra on
$S^1$ with values in $\Ch(k)$, and let $\mathcal{C}$ be the
associated $\Eone$-category \textup{(}the dg-category of modules
over the $\Eone$-algebra $A = \mathcal{F}(I)$ for any interval
$I \subset S^1$\textup{)}.  Then there is a natural equivalence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:circle-fh-HH}
\int_{S^1} \mathcal{F}
\;\simeq\;
\mathrm{HH}_\bullet(\mathcal{C})
:= A \otimes^{\mathbf{L}}_{A^e} A,
\end{equation}
where $A^e = A \otimes A^{\mathrm{op}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof proceeds by cutting $S^1$ into overlapping arcs and
applying excision.

\emph{Step~1} (Collar-gluing decomposition of $S^1$).
Choose a decomposition $S^1 = U_+ \cup U_-$ where $U_+$ and $U_-$
are open arcs (each diffeomorphic to an interval) whose intersection
consists of two disjoint intervals:
\[
U_+ \cap U_- = J_1 \sqcup J_2.
\]
Concretely, parametrize $S^1 = \R / \Z$ and take
$U_+ = (0, 3/4)$, $U_- = (1/2, 5/4)$, so that
$J_1 = (1/2, 3/4)$ and $J_2 = (0, 1/4)$ (the latter wrapping around
$0 \equiv 1$).  The collar structure along the boundary is given by
the intervals $J_1$ and $J_2$ themselves.

\emph{Step~2} (Local constancy on arcs).
Since $\mathcal{F}$ is locally constant, its value on any interval is
equivalent to the $\Eone$-algebra $A$:
\[
\mathcal{F}(U_+) \simeq A, \quad
\mathcal{F}(U_-) \simeq A, \quad
\mathcal{F}(J_1) \simeq A, \quad
\mathcal{F}(J_2) \simeq A.
\]
More precisely, for each interval $J \subset S^1$, the restriction
$\mathcal{F}|_J$ is equivalent to the factorization algebra associated
to a single $\Eone$-algebra~$A$, and the value $\mathcal{F}(J) \simeq A$
is given by the $\Eone$-structure.

\emph{Step~3} (Excision).
By the excision axiom (Axiom~\ref{ax:excision}) applied to the
collar-gluing $S^1 = U_+ \cup_{J_1 \sqcup J_2} U_-$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:excision-circle}
\int_{S^1} \mathcal{F}
\;\simeq\;
\mathcal{F}(U_+)
\;\otimes^{\mathbf{L}}_{\mathcal{F}(J_1) \otimes \mathcal{F}(J_2)}\;
\mathcal{F}(U_-).
\end{equation}
Substituting the local constancy identifications from Step~2:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:excision-substituted}
\int_{S^1} \mathcal{F}
\;\simeq\;
A \;\otimes^{\mathbf{L}}_{A^e}\; A,
\qquad A^e := A \otimes A^{\mathrm{op}}.
\end{equation}

\emph{Step~4} (Bimodule structure and identification with HH).
The key is to identify the $A \otimes A$-module structures on the two
copies of~$A$ in~\eqref{eq:excision-substituted}.  The inclusions
$J_1 \hookrightarrow U_+$ and $J_2 \hookrightarrow U_+$ give the
left and right $A$-actions on $\mathcal{F}(U_+) \simeq A$: the
inclusion of $J_1$ at one end of the arc~$U_+$ provides the left
action, and the inclusion of $J_2$ at the other end provides the
right action.  That these are the \emph{regular} left and right
actions (i.e., $a \cdot_L x = ax$ and $x \cdot_R b = xb$) follows
from local constancy: the factorization algebra~$\mathcal{F}$ on the
arc~$U_+$ is equivalent to the $\Eone$-algebra~$A$ (Step~2), and
the collar inclusions $J_i \hookrightarrow U_+$ restrict to the
identity on~$A$ (since all intervals carry the same algebra by local
constancy).  The bimodule structure is therefore
$(a_1, a_2) \cdot x = a_1 \cdot x \cdot a_2$, which is precisely the
diagonal bimodule ${}_A A_A$.  Similarly for $\mathcal{F}(U_-)$, where
the \emph{opposite} orientation of $U_-$ relative to $U_+$ introduces
the $A^{\mathrm{op}}$ factor: the left action from $J_1$ on $U_-$
is the right action on $U_+$, and vice versa.  This is the source
of the $A^{\mathrm{op}}$ in
$A^e = A \otimes A^{\mathrm{op}}$.

The tensor product over $A \otimes A$ of two copies of the diagonal
bimodule is, by definition, the Hochschild chain complex:
\[
A \otimes^{\mathbf{L}}_{A \otimes A^{\mathrm{op}}} A
= A \otimes^{\mathbf{L}}_{A^e} A
=: \mathrm{HH}_\bullet(A).
\]
Here $A \otimes A$ acts on the left copy of~$A$
via $(a_1, a_2) \cdot x = a_1 \cdot x \cdot a_2$
(using the bimodule structure from $J_1$ and $J_2$), and on the
right copy of~$A$ symmetrically; the tensor product over this action
contracts the two bimodules into the cyclic bar complex
$\cdots \to A^{\otimes 3} \to A^{\otimes 2} \to A$ computing
$\mathrm{HH}_\bullet(A)$.

\emph{Step~5} (Passage to the categorical statement).
If $\mathcal{C} = \mathrm{Perf}(A)$ is the dg-category of perfect
$A$-modules, then $\mathrm{HH}_\bullet(\mathcal{C})$ is defined as
$\mathrm{HH}_\bullet(A)$ via the Morita-invariant cyclic bar
construction.  Combining with Step~4 gives the stated equivalence
$\int_{S^1} \mathcal{F} \simeq \mathrm{HH}_\bullet(\mathcal{C})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Provenance]
\label{rem:annulus-provenance}
The identification of factorization homology on $S^1$ with Hochschild
homology is due (in the topological setting) to
Ben-Zvi--Francis--Nadler~\cite{BZFN10} and
Ayala--Francis~\cite{AF15}; in the vertex algebra setting, the
parallel result appears in Costello--Gwilliam~\cite[Chapter~5]{CG17}.
The excision argument above follows the treatment in~\cite{AF15}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The monodromy subtlety: from chiral to locally constant}
\label{subsec:monodromy-subtlety}

\index{monodromy!factorization homology}

The excision argument of Theorem~\ref{thm:circle-fh-hochschild}
requires $\mathcal{F}$ to be a \emph{locally constant} factorization
algebra on $S^1$.  In the chiral algebraic setting, the situation is
more delicate: a chiral algebra~$\cA$ on a curve~$X$ is a
factorization algebra on $X$ that is locally constant on small disks
(the $\Eone$-structure on intervals), but the passage from an interval
to the full circle involves identifying the endpoints.  This
identification may carry \emph{monodromy}, a nontrivial automorphism
of the $\Eone$-algebra $A = \cA(I)$ arising from parallel transport
around the puncture.

\begin{definition}[Monodromy automorphism]\label{def:monodromy-aut}
\index{monodromy!automorphism|textbf}
Let $\cA$ be a chiral algebra on a punctured disk $D^\times = D
\setminus \{0\}$, and let $I \subset D^\times$ be an interval.
The fundamental group $\pi_1(D^\times) \cong \Z$ acts on the
fiber $A = \cA(I)$ by monodromy.  The \emph{monodromy
automorphism} is the generator:
\[
\mathfrak{m}\colon A \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; A,
\]
obtained by analytically continuing sections of $\cA$ around the
puncture once counterclockwise.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Monodromy for standard families;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{prop:monodromy-standard}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)}]
\item \emph{Heisenberg $\mathcal{H}_k$.}  The monodromy
  automorphism $\mathfrak{m}$ acts on the mode algebra generated
  by $\{J_n\}_{n \in \Z}$ via $\mathfrak{m}(J_n) = J_n$ for all
  $n$.  The monodromy is \emph{trivial} on the algebra.  However,
  on Fock modules $F_\lambda$, the monodromy acts by the scalar
  $e^{2\pi i \lambda^2 / (2k)}$
  \textup{(}from the conformal weight $h_\lambda = \lambda^2/(2k)$
  contribution to $q^{L_0}$\textup{)}.  For the vacuum module
  $F_0$, this is trivial.
\item \emph{Affine $\widehat{\fg}_k$.}  The monodromy is trivial on
  the algebra \textup{(}the current modes $J^a_n$ are
  single-valued\textup{)}.  On highest-weight modules $V_\Lambda$ at
  integer level, the monodromy acts by
  $e^{2\pi i h_\Lambda}$ where $h_\Lambda = (\Lambda, \Lambda +
  2\rho) / 2(k + h^\vee)$ is the conformal weight.
\item \emph{Lattice VOA $V_\Lambda$.}  The vertex operators
  $e^\alpha$ for $\alpha \in \Lambda$ have monodromy
  $\mathfrak{m}(e^\alpha) = e^{2\pi i (\alpha, \alpha)/2}\, e^\alpha$.
  For an even lattice, $(\alpha, \alpha) \in 2\Z$, so the monodromy
  is trivial.  For an odd lattice, the monodromy is a sign
  $(-1)^{(\alpha,\alpha)}$ on each sector (note: odd lattices
  define \emph{super} vertex algebras; the sign is the
  fermion parity).
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In each case, the monodromy is computed from the $L_0$-eigenvalue.
For a primary field $\phi$ of conformal weight~$h$, the operator
product expansion $\phi(e^{2\pi i} z) = e^{2\pi i h} \phi(z)$ gives
the monodromy phase.

(a)~For $\mathcal{H}_k$: the current $J(z) = \sum_n J_n z^{-n-1}$
has conformal weight~$1$, so $J(e^{2\pi i}z) = e^{2\pi i} \cdot
J(z) \cdot e^{-2\pi i} = J(z)$ (the $z^{-n-1}$ factor absorbs the
phase).  More precisely, $\mathfrak{m}(J_n) = J_n$ because the modes
are defined as contour integrals $J_n = \oint z^n J(z)\,dz$ over
cycles that are invariant under the monodromy deck transformation.
On $F_\lambda$, the highest-weight vector $|\lambda\rangle$ has
$L_0$-eigenvalue $h_\lambda = \lambda^2/(2k)$, giving the stated
phase.

(b)~Identical argument for $\widehat{\fg}_k$, with $h_\Lambda$
replacing $h_\lambda$.

(c)~For $V_\Lambda$, $e^\alpha$ has conformal weight
$(\alpha,\alpha)/2$, so $\mathfrak{m}(e^\alpha) = e^{2\pi i
(\alpha,\alpha)/2}\, e^\alpha$.  For an even lattice,
$(\alpha,\alpha)/2 \in \Z$, so the phase is~$1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Twisted Hochschild homology]
\label{rem:twisted-hochschild}
\index{Hochschild homology!twisted}
When the monodromy $\mathfrak{m}\colon A \to A$ is nontrivial, the
factorization homology on $S^1$ computes the
\emph{$\mathfrak{m}$-twisted Hochschild homology}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:twisted-HH}
\int_{S^1} \mathcal{F}
\;\simeq\;
\mathrm{HH}_\bullet(A;\, {}_{\mathfrak{m}}A)
:= A \otimes^{\mathbf{L}}_{A^e} {}_{\mathfrak{m}}A,
\end{equation}
where ${}_{\mathfrak{m}}A$ is $A$ as a right $A$-module but with
left action twisted by $\mathfrak{m}$: $a \cdot_{\mathfrak{m}} x :=
\mathfrak{m}(a) \cdot x$.  The excision argument of
Theorem~\ref{thm:circle-fh-hochschild} goes through verbatim with
$U_-$ contributing the twisted bimodule structure: the analytic
continuation around the full circle inserts $\mathfrak{m}$ into one
of the two collar inclusions.

For the standard families of
Proposition~\ref{prop:monodromy-standard}, the monodromy is trivial
on the algebra itself (the algebra modes are single-valued), so the
untwisted identification~\eqref{eq:circle-fh-HH} holds.  The twist
becomes visible only at the module level: the torus partition function
$Z_{T^2}(\cA) = \mathrm{Tr}_{\mathcal{H}}(q^{L_0 - c/24})$ traces
over the Hilbert space~$\mathcal{H}$ with the insertion of
$q^{L_0}$, which is precisely the twist by the monodromy of the
cylinder.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The $(g{=}1, k{=}0)$ modular MC component]
\label{rem:mc-hh-identification}
\index{Maurer--Cartan!genus-1 and Hochschild}
The $(g=1, n=0)$ component of the modular MC equation
(Chapter~\ref{ch:en-koszul-duality}) is:
\[
d\Theta_{\cA}^{(1,0)} + \tfrac{1}{2}
  [\Theta_{\cA}^{(0)}, \Theta_{\cA}^{(0)}]^{(1,0)}
  + \Delta_{\mathrm{sew}}\Theta_{\cA}^{(0,2)} = 0.
\]
The sewing operator $\Delta_{\mathrm{sew}}$ contracts two external
legs of $\Theta^{(0,2)}_{\cA}$ using the propagator on the torus.
This is precisely the genus-$1$ Hochschild differential: the annulus
amplitude $\Theta^{(0,2)}_{\cA}$ evaluated on two inputs and sewn
into a torus is the trace $\mathrm{Tr}(m_2(-,-))$, the standard
generator of $\mathrm{HH}_0(A)$.  The identification of
$\int_{S^1}\cA$ with $\mathrm{HH}_\bullet(A)$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:circle-fh-hochschild})
is therefore the $n=0$ case of the general MC$\to$sewing
correspondence.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Heisenberg verification}

\begin{example}[Hochschild chains of the Heisenberg algebra;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{ex:heisenberg-HH-chains}
\index{Heisenberg algebra!Hochschild chains}
We verify the identification
$\int_{S^1} \mathcal{H}_k \simeq \mathrm{HH}_\bullet(\mathcal{H}_k)$
against the known genus-$1$ partition function.

\emph{Step~1} (The algebra).
The Heisenberg vertex algebra $\mathcal{H}_k$ at level $k \neq 0$
is generated by a single current $J(z) = \sum_{n \in \Z} J_n
z^{-n-1}$ with OPE $J(z) J(w) \sim k/(z-w)^2$.  The mode
algebra is the Weyl algebra on infinitely many generators:
$[J_m, J_n] = k\,m\,\delta_{m+n,0}$.  The zero mode $J_0$
acts on the vacuum Fock module $\mathcal{H}_k = F_0$
(with $J_0$-eigenvalue~$0$); the full module category
contains Fock modules $F_\lambda$ for $\lambda \in k^{-1}\Z$.

\emph{Step~2} (Hochschild homology computation).
By the computation in Example~\ref{ex:E2-heisenberg}:
\[
\mathrm{HH}_\bullet^{\mathrm{ch}}(\mathcal{H}_k)
= \C[c] \otimes \Lambda(\sigma),
\qquad |c| = 2,\; |\sigma| = 1.
\]
The class $c \in \mathrm{HH}_2$ is the Chern class of the level
(the universal deformation class), and $\sigma \in \mathrm{HH}_1$
is the fundamental class of $S^1$ in the cyclic direction.

\emph{Step~3} (Genus-1 partition function).
The factorization homology $\int_{S^1} \mathcal{H}_k
\simeq \mathrm{HH}_\bullet(\mathcal{H}_k)$ computes
Hochschild chains.  The \emph{torus partition function} is
the Euler characteristic of $\int_{T^2}\mathcal{H}_k$,
which by the sewing formula reduces to
$\mathrm{Tr}_{F_0}(q^{L_0 - c/24})$ where $F_0$ is the
vacuum module (the trace inserts the $q$-grading operator
on the space of states):
\[
Z_{T^2}(\mathcal{H}_k)
= \mathrm{Tr}_{F_0}(q^{L_0 - 1/24})
= q^{-1/24} \prod_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{1 - q^n}
= \frac{1}{\eta(\tau)},
\]
where $\eta(\tau) = q^{1/24} \prod_{n \geq 1}(1-q^n)$ is the
Dedekind eta function.

\emph{Step~4} (Consistency check: $\mathrm{HH}_0$).
The degree-$0$ Hochschild homology $\mathrm{HH}_0(\mathcal{H}_k) =
A / [A, A]$ is the abelianization of the mode algebra.  For the Weyl
algebra generated by $\{J_n\}$, all commutators $[J_m, J_n] = km\,
\delta_{m+n,0}$ are scalar, so $A / [A, A]$ is the symmetric algebra
on the generators modulo the scalars: $\mathrm{HH}_0 \cong \C$
(one-dimensional, generated by the vacuum class).  This is consistent
with the partition function $Z_{T^2}$ being a single function
(not a vector) -- the space of conformal blocks on the torus is
one-dimensional for the Heisenberg algebra at any level.

\emph{Step~5} (Comparison with Theorem~\ref{thm:partition-cyclic}).
The cyclic homology $\mathrm{HC}_0(\mathcal{H}_k) = \C$ matches the
one-dimensional space of genus-$1$ conformal blocks.  The full
Hochschild chain complex $\mathrm{HH}_\bullet(\mathcal{H}_k) = \C[c]
\otimes \Lambda(\sigma)$ encodes the deformation theory of the torus
partition function: $c$ controls the variation of $Z_{T^2}$ with
respect to the level~$k$, and $\sigma$ represents the circle
direction in the torus.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Annulus vs torus]
\label{rem:annulus-vs-torus}
\index{annulus!vs torus}
The factorization homology $\int_{S^1} \mathcal{F}$ over the circle
gives the Hochschild chains $\mathrm{HH}_\bullet(A)$.  The partition
function on the torus $T^2 = S^1 \times S^1$ involves a further
circle integral, which gives the cyclic homology
$\mathrm{HC}_\bullet(A)$ (the $S^1$-equivariant version of
$\mathrm{HH}_\bullet$).  Schematically:
\[
\int_{S^1} \mathcal{F} = \mathrm{HH}_\bullet(A),
\qquad
\int_{T^2} \mathcal{F}
= \mathrm{HC}_\bullet(A)
= \mathrm{HH}_\bullet(A)_{hS^1}.
\]
The first circle is the spatial circle (excision gives HH); the second
is the thermal circle (the $S^1$-action on HH gives HC).
For the Heisenberg:
$\mathrm{HC}_0(\mathcal{H}_k) = \C$, and the character
$\mathrm{Tr}_{F_0}(q^{L_0-c/24}) = 1/\eta(\tau)$ is the
generating function for the graded dimensions of the cyclic complex.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Relation to Theorem~C \textup{(}complementarity\textup{)}]
\label{rem:annulus-complementarity}
\index{complementarity!annulus}
The Hochschild chain identification provides the genus-$1$ input to
the complementarity theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:quantum-complementarity-main}).  At genus~$1$, the
complementarity formula reads
$Q_1(\cA) \oplus Q_1(\cA^!) \cong H^*(\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{1,1},
\mathcal{Z}(\cA))$.
The left-hand side is computed by Hochschild chains of $\cA$ and
$\cA^!$ respectively (via the annulus identification); the right-hand
side is the cohomology of the moduli space with coefficients in the
center sheaf.  The complementarity is the statement that the two
Hochschild chain complexes assemble into a complete basis for the
modular functor at genus~$1$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Summary and future directions}

The theoretical arc of Part~I is now complete.  Algebraic foundations (\S\ref{ch:algebraic-foundations}) supplied the operadic language; configuration spaces (\S\ref{chap:config-spaces}) gave it geometric content; the bar-cobar construction realized the adjunction as Verdier duality on the Ran space; and the higher-genus theory assembled the proved scalar package~$\kappa$, the proved spectral package~$(\Delta_{\cA},\Pi_{\cA})$, and the theorematic simple-Lie-symmetry Maurer--Cartan completion~$\Theta_{\cA}$.  The three main theorems (adjunction (A), inversion on the Koszul locus (B), and Lagrangian complementarity (C)) are shadows of a single structure: the modular Koszul object associated to a factorization algebra on curves.  Chiral Hochschild cohomology, developed in this chapter, is the infinitesimal invariant of that structure: it detects periodicities, controls deformations, and exchanges under Koszul duality via the Connes operator.  Part~II turns from the abstract theory to its concrete realizations, beginning with the lattice construction that provides a uniform source for all major families of chiral algebras.
