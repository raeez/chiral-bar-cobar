\label{ch:all-tier-generating-function}

\section*{Orientation}

The Virasoro shadow tower has a single strong generator.  The scalar
Riccati recurrence therefore has one transport channel, and the
Laurent expansion at $c=\infty$
\[
\Phi_r(c) := c^{r-2}S_r(\Vir_c)
 = A_r + \frac{B_r}{c} + \frac{\Gamma_r}{c^2}
 + \frac{\Delta_r}{c^3}+\cdots
\]
is controlled by a one-variable Catalan convolution.  The first four
tiers of Chapter~\ref{ch:shadow-tower-higher-coefficients} are the
initial shadows of the following closed form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:all-tier-raw-intro}
\frac{\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}K(r)}{A_r}
 \;=\; (-1)^{K+1}
 \sum_{m = 0}^{K-1}
 \binom{K-1}{m}\,
 \frac{\tau^{K-1-m}\,(r-4)_{2m}}{9^m \, m! \, (m+1)!},
\end{equation}
for every $K \ge 1$ and every integer $r \ge 4$.  Here
$\tau=22/5$, $A_r=8(-6)^{r-4}/r$, and
$(r-4)_{2m}=(r-4)(r-5)\cdots(r-4-(2m-1))$.

The bivariate package is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bivariate-F-intro}
F(r, u) \;:=\; \frac{\Phi_r(1/u)}{A_r}
 \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + \tau u} \,
 {}_2F_1\!\left(\frac{4-r}{2},\frac{5-r}{2};\; 2;\;
\frac{-4u}{9(1 + \tau u)}\right),
\end{equation}
which is rational in $u$ because the Gauss factor terminates at
degree $\lfloor (r-4)/2\rfloor$.  The base cases are
$F(4,u)=F(5,u)=(1+\tau u)^{-1}$.  For $r\ge5$ the rescaled master
recurrence is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bilinear-master-intro}
\frac{r\, F(r, u)\, A_r}{u}
 \;=\; -\sum_{\substack{j + k = r + 2 \\ 3 \le j \le k < r}}
f(j, k)\, j k\, A_j A_k\, F(j, u)\, F(k, u),
\end{equation}
with $F(3,u)=1/u$, $A_3=2$, $f(j,k)=1$ for $j<k$, and
$f(j,j)=1/2$.  Splitting off the $(3,r-1)$ channel gives the
ordinary Catalan recurrence in the shifted arity $n=r-4$; solving it
is the proof of \eqref{eq:bivariate-F-intro}.

This is a scalar diagonal theorem.  It applies to the Virasoro family,
equivalently the principal $\mathcal{W}_2$ line, where
$\kappa(\Vir_c)=c/2$, $\Vir_c^!=\Vir_{26-c}$, and
$\kappa+\kappa^!=13$.  For principal $\mathcal{W}_N$ one has the
diagonal scalar coefficient
$\kappa(\mathcal{W}_N)=c(H_N-1)$ and
$\kappa+\kappa^!=(c+c')(H_N-1)$, with
$c+c'=2(N-1)(2N^2+2N+1)$; at $N=3$ this is $250/3$.  For
$N\ge3$ the full genus expansion has cross-channel corrections from
mixed generator pairs.  The hypergeometric formula above is not a
multi-weight $\mathcal{W}_N$ formula.

\section{Bivariate generating function and the hypergeometric form}

\subsection{Definitions and normalisation}

Fix $\tau := 22/5$ throughout.  The Zamolodchikov quasi-primary
$\Lambda={:}TT{:}-(3/10)\partial^2T$ has
$\langle \Lambda | \Lambda \rangle = c(5c + 22)/10$, and this is the
source of the factor $22/5$.  Fix also the rescaled shadow sequence
\begin{equation}
\Phi_r(c) \;:=\; c^{r-2}\, S_r(\Vir_c), \qquad r \ge 2,
\end{equation}
so that $\Phi_r$ is a rational function of $c$ with $\Phi_3 = 2c$ of
negative Laurent order at $c = \infty$ and $\Phi_r$ of order zero for
$r \ge 4$. The leading Laurent coefficient at $c = \infty$ is
$A_r = 8 (-6)^{r-4}/r$ for $r \ge 4$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:shadow-tower-asymptotic-closed-form}).

\begin{definition}[Bivariate generating function; \ClaimStatusDefinitional]
\label{def:bivariate-F}
For every integer $r \ge 4$, set
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:F-def}
F(r, u) \;:=\; \Phi_r(c = 1/u) / A_r \;\in\; \mathbb{Q}(u).
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $F(r, u) = 1 + \sum_{K \ge 1} \mathrm{Tier}\text{-}(K+1)(r)
/ A_r \cdot u^K$ where $\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}K(r)$ is the $c^{-(K-1)}$
Laurent coefficient of $\Phi_r(c)$ at $c = \infty$. By convention the
constant term of $F(r, u)$ is $\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}1(r)/A_r = 1$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{The hypergeometric closed form}

\begin{theorem}[\label{thm:all-tier-bivariate-generating-function}%
Bivariate hypergeometric generating function for the Virasoro shadow
tower]
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
For every integer $r \ge 4$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:F-hyper}
F(r, u) \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + \tau u}\,
 {}_2F_1\!\left(\frac{4-r}{2},\, \frac{5-r}{2};\; 2;\;
 \frac{-4 u}{9 (1 + \tau u)}\right).
\end{equation}
The Gauss hypergeometric factor terminates at polynomial degree
$\lfloor (r-4)/2 \rfloor$ in its argument, hence $F(r, u) \in
\mathbb{Q}(u)$ is a rational function whose denominator divides
$(1 + \tau u)^{\lfloor (r-4)/2 \rfloor + 1}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The base cases are direct:
\begin{align*}
\Phi_4(c) &= c^{2} \cdot \frac{10}{c(5c + 22)}
 \;=\; \frac{10 c}{5 c + 22}
 \;=\; \frac{2}{1 + 22/(5c)}, \\
F(4, u) &= \Phi_4(1/u) / A_4 \;=\; \frac{2}{1 + \tau u} \cdot \frac{1}{2}
 \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + \tau u}.
\end{align*}
For $r = 5$, $\Phi_5(c) = -48 c / (5 c + 22)$ and $A_5 = -48/5$ gives
$F(5, u) = 1 / (1 + \tau u)$ identically. These are also the two
specialisations of \eqref{eq:F-hyper}: at $r=4$ one upper parameter is
$0$, and at $r=5$ the second upper parameter is $0$.

For $r \ge 5$, the Virasoro weighted recurrence of
Theorem~\ref{thm:virasoro-shadow-recurrence}, equivalently the
rescaled recurrence used in
Theorem~\ref{thm:shadow-tower-subleading-closed-form}, gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:master-bilinear-proof}
r\, c\, \Phi_r \;=\; -\sum_{\substack{j + k = r + 2 \\ 3 \le j \le k < r}}
f(j, k)\, j k\, \Phi_j \Phi_k
\end{equation}
with $\Phi_3=2c$.  Substituting $c = 1/u$ and dividing by $A_r$ gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:master-in-u}
\frac{r\, F(r, u)\, A_r}{u}
 \;=\; -\sum_{\substack{j + k = r + 2 \\ 3 \le j \le k < r}}
 f(j, k)\, j k\, A_j A_k\, F(j, u)\, F(k, u),
\end{equation}
where we use $F(3, u) := \Phi_3(1/u) / A_3 = (2/u) / 2 = 1/u$ with the
formal setting $A_3 = 2$ (the leading-$c$ coefficient of $\Phi_3 = 2 c$).

Split the $(3,r-1)$ transport term.  Since
$rA_r=-6(r-1)A_{r-1}$ for $r\ge5$, this term contributes exactly
$rA_rF(r-1,u)/u$ to the right hand side of the displayed recurrence.
For the remaining terms $j,k\ge4$ the identity
\[
\frac{jkA_jA_k}{rA_r}
 = \frac{8(-6)^{j-4}\,8(-6)^{k-4}}{8(-6)^{r-4}}
 = \frac{2}{9},
\qquad j+k=r+2,
\]
is independent of $(j,k)$.  Thus
\[
F(r,u)=F(r-1,u)
-\frac{2u}{9}
\sum_{\substack{j+k=r+2\\4\le j\le k\le r-2}}
f(j,k)F(j,u)F(k,u).
\]
Set $H_n(u)=F(n+4,u)$ and $a(u)=(1+\tau u)^{-1}$.  Then
$H_0=H_1=a$, and for $n\ge2$
\[
H_n=H_{n-1}
-\frac{u}{9}\sum_{p+q=n-2}H_pH_q,
\]
where the unordered sum with weights $f$ has become one half of the
ordered convolution.

Let $R(t,u)=\sum_{n\ge0}H_n(u)t^n$.  Multiplying the recurrence by
$t^n$ and summing over $n\ge1$ gives the quadratic identity
\[
(1-t)R(t,u)-a(u)=-\frac{u}{9}\,t^2R(t,u)^2.
\]
The solution with $R(0,u)=a(u)$ is
\[
R(t,u)=\frac{a(u)}{1-t}\,
C\!\left(-\,\frac{u\,a(u)t^2}{9(1-t)^2}\right),
\qquad
C(z)=\sum_{m\ge0}\frac{1}{m+1}\binom{2m}{m}z^m .
\]
Extracting the coefficient of $t^n$ yields
\[
H_n(u)
=a(u)\sum_{m\ge0}
\frac{(n)_{2m}}{9^m\,m!\,(m+1)!}
\bigl(-u\,a(u)\bigr)^m ,
\]
with automatic termination at $m=\lfloor n/2\rfloor$.
Substituting $n=r-4$ and $a(u)=(1+\tau u)^{-1}$ gives
\[
F(r,u)=\frac{1}{1+\tau u}
\sum_{m\ge0}
\frac{(r-4)_{2m}}{9^m\,m!\,(m+1)!}
\left(\frac{-u}{1+\tau u}\right)^m .
\]
Finally
\[
(r-4)_{2m}
=4^m\left(\frac{4-r}{2}\right)_m
\left(\frac{5-r}{2}\right)_m,
\qquad
(2)_m=(m+1)!,
\]
turns this finite Catalan sum into the Gauss expression
\eqref{eq:F-hyper}.  One of the two upper parameters is a
non-positive integer for every integer $r\ge4$, so the Gauss series
terminates at degree $\lfloor(r-4)/2\rfloor$; hence $F(r,u)$ is
rational with denominator dividing
$(1+\tau u)^{\lfloor(r-4)/2\rfloor+1}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[\label{rem:F-reduction-structure}%
Reduction to polynomial + denominator power]
The formula \eqref{eq:F-hyper} makes the rational structure of $\Phi_r/A_r$
transparent. Clearing the denominator:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:F-clear-denominator}
(1 + \tau u)^{\lfloor (r-4)/2 \rfloor + 1}\, F(r, u) \;=\; P_r(u),
\end{equation}
where $P_r \in \mathbb{Q}[u]$ is a polynomial of degree
$\lfloor (r-4)/2 \rfloor$ obtained as the ${}_2F_1$ polynomial scaled by
the extra denominator factors. The denominator pattern
$(5 c + 22)^{\lfloor (r-2)/2 \rfloor}$ of $S_r(\Vir_c)$ corresponds
(via $c = 1/u$ and $5 c + 22 = (5 + 22 u)/u = 5(1 + \tau u)/u$) to
$(1 + \tau u)^{\lfloor (r-2)/2 \rfloor}$ in $F(r, u)$.  Since
\[
\lfloor(r-2)/2\rfloor=\lfloor(r-4)/2\rfloor+1
\qquad (r\ge4),
\]
this is exactly \eqref{eq:F-clear-denominator}; $u=0$ is the regular
expansion point.
\end{remark}

\section{All-tier closed form}

\begin{theorem}[\label{thm:all-tier-laurent-stratification}%
All-tier Laurent stratification of the Virasoro shadow tower at $c =
\infty$]
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
For every $K \ge 1$ and every integer $r \ge 4$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tier-K-closed-form}
\frac{\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}K(r)}{A_r}
 \;=\; (-1)^{K+1}
 \sum_{m = 0}^{K-1}
 \binom{K-1}{m}\,
 \frac{\tau^{K-1-m}\,(r-4)_{2m}}{9^m \, m! \, (m+1)!},
\end{equation}
where $\tau = 22/5$, $(r-4)_{2m} = (r-4)(r-5) \cdots (r-4-(2m-1))$ is
the falling factorial of length $2m$ starting at $r - 4$, and $A_r =
8 (-6)^{r-4} / r$ is the leading asymptotic coefficient.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:all-tier-bivariate-generating-function},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:F-sum-over-m}
F(r, u) \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + \tau u}
 \sum_{m \ge 0} \frac{(r-4)_{2m}}{9^m\, m!\, (m+1)!}\,
 \left( \frac{-u}{1 + \tau u} \right)^{m},
\end{equation}
where the ${}_2F_1$ series has been written in the form $\sum_m
(a)_m^{\uparrow} (b)_m^{\uparrow} (2)_m^{-1} x^m / m!$ and the identity
\begin{equation}
(r - 4)_{2m}^{\downarrow}
 \;=\; 4^m \bigl(\tfrac{4-r}{2}\bigr)_m^{\uparrow}
 \bigl(\tfrac{5-r}{2}\bigr)_m^{\uparrow}
\end{equation}
(Gauss's multiplication theorem for the Pochhammer symbol) has been
used, together with $(2)_m^{\uparrow} = (m+1)!$ and $4^m / 9^m$
absorbed into $(4/9)^m$, cancelling the $(4/9)^m$ from the argument
$-4 u / (9 (1 + \tau u))$ to leave $(-u/(1 + \tau u))^m$.

Using this expansion and the binomial series
$(1 + \tau u)^{-(m+1)} = \sum_{n \ge 0} \binom{m + n}{m} (-\tau u)^n$:
\begin{equation}
F(r, u) \;=\; \sum_{m, n \ge 0}
 \frac{(r-4)_{2m}}{9^m\, m!\, (m+1)!}\,
 (-u)^m\, \binom{m + n}{m}\, (-\tau u)^n.
\end{equation}
Collect powers of $u$: set $K - 1 = m + n$, so $n = K - 1 - m$ with
$m \in \{0, 1, \ldots, K - 1\}$. Then
\begin{equation}
[u^{K-1}]\, F(r, u)
 \;=\; \sum_{m=0}^{K-1}
 \frac{(r-4)_{2m}}{9^m\, m!\, (m+1)!}\,
 (-1)^{K-1}\, \binom{K - 1}{m}\, \tau^{K-1-m}.
\end{equation}
The sign $(-1)^m (-1)^{n} = (-1)^{m+n} = (-1)^{K-1} = -(-1)^{K}$
simplifies to $(-1)^{K-1} = (-1)^{K+1}$.

By Definition~\ref{def:bivariate-F},
$F(r, u) = 1 + \sum_{K \ge 1} (\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}(K+1)(r) / A_r) u^K$,
i.e.\ $[u^{K-1}] F(r, u) = \mathrm{Tier}\text{-}K(r) / A_r$ (with
Tier-1 being the constant term, equal to 1, which is
$(-1)^{1+1} \binom{0}{0} \tau^0 \cdot 1 / (9^0 \cdot 0! \cdot 1!) = 1$
on the right-hand side of \eqref{eq:tier-K-closed-form}, as required).

This is \eqref{eq:tier-K-closed-form}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[\label{cor:tier-k-leading-coefficient}%
Leading (highest-degree) coefficient of Tier-$K$]
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
For every $K \ge 1$ and every $r \ge 4$ with $r - 4 \ge 2(K-1)$, the
coefficient $c^{(K)}_{K-1}$ of the highest-degree term
$(r-4)_{2(K-1)}$ in $\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}K(r)/A_r$ is
\begin{equation}
c^{(K)}_{K-1} \;=\;
\frac{(-1)^{K+1}}{9^{K-1}\,(K-1)!\,K!}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the leading denominator pattern of Tier-$K$ is
$D_K = 9^{K-1}\,(K-1)!\,K!$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Specialize $m = K - 1$ in \eqref{eq:tier-K-closed-form}, giving
coefficient
$(-1)^{K+1}\binom{K-1}{K-1}\tau^0/(9^{K-1}(K-1)!K!)$.
Its denominator is $D_K$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[\label{rem:four-tier-recovery}%
Four-tier recovery]
The four explicitly inscribed Tiers of
Chapter~\ref{ch:shadow-tower-higher-coefficients} are recovered from
\eqref{eq:tier-K-closed-form} at $K = 1, 2, 3, 4$:
\begin{align*}
K &= 1: \quad \tau^0 / (9^0 \cdot 1) \;=\; 1 \;=\; A_r/A_r. \\
K &= 2: \quad -[\tau + (r-4)(r-5)/18] \;=\; B_r/A_r. \\
K &= 3: \quad +[\tau^2 + 2\tau (r-4)(r-5)/18 + (r-4)_4/972]
 \;=\; \tau^2 + \tfrac{22}{45}(r-4)(r-5) + (r-4)_4/972, \\
 &\qquad = \Gamma_r/A_r, \\
K &= 4: \quad -[\tau^3 + 3\tau^2(r-4)(r-5)/18 + 3 \tau (r-4)_4/972
 + (r-4)_6/104976] \\
 &\qquad = -(22/5)^3 - \tfrac{242}{75}(r-4)(r-5)
 - \tfrac{11}{810}(r-4)_4 - (r-4)_6/104976
 \;=\; \Delta_r/A_r.
\end{align*}
All four agree with the closed forms of
Theorems~\ref{thm:shadow-tower-asymptotic-closed-form},
\ref{thm:shadow-tower-subleading-closed-form},
\ref{thm:shadow-tower-sub-subleading-closed-form},
\ref{thm:shadow-tower-tier-4-closed-form}.
\end{remark}

\section{Tier-5 closed form}

\begin{theorem}[\label{thm:tier-5-closed-form}%
Tier-5 closed form]
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
Let $\mathrm{Epsilon}_r := \mathrm{Tier}\text{-}5(r)$ denote the
$c^{-4}$ Laurent coefficient of $\Phi_r(c) = c^{r-2} S_r(\Vir_c)$ at
$c = \infty$. Then for every $r \ge 4$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tier-5-explicit}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\mathrm{Epsilon}_r}{A_r}
 &= \left(\frac{22}{5}\right)^{4}
 \;+\; \frac{21296}{1125}\,(r-4)(r-5)
 \;+\; \frac{242}{2025}\,(r-4)(r-5)(r-6)(r-7) \\
 &\quad\;+\; \frac{11}{65610}\,(r-4)(r-5)(r-6)(r-7)(r-8)(r-9) \\
 &\quad\;+\; \frac{1}{18895680}\,(r-4)(r-5)(r-6)(r-7)(r-8)(r-9)(r-10)(r-11).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The five coefficients specialize the all-tier formula
\eqref{eq:tier-K-closed-form} at $K = 5$:
$c^{(5)}_m = \binom{4}{m} \tau^{4-m} / (9^m\, m!\, (m+1)!)$ for
$m = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Specialize \eqref{eq:tier-K-closed-form} at $K = 5$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\mathrm{Epsilon}_r}{A_r}
 \;=\; (-1)^6 \sum_{m=0}^{4} \binom{4}{m} \frac{\tau^{4-m} (r-4)_{2m}}
 {9^m \, m! \, (m+1)!}.
\end{equation}
Compute each coefficient:
\begin{align*}
m = 0: &\quad \binom{4}{0} \tau^4 / (9^0 \cdot 0! \cdot 1!)
 \;=\; \tau^4 \;=\; (22/5)^4 \;=\; 234256/625, \\
m = 1: &\quad \binom{4}{1} \tau^3 / (9 \cdot 1! \cdot 2!)
 \;=\; 4 \cdot (22/5)^3 / 18 \;=\; 4 \cdot 10648 / (125 \cdot 18)
 \;=\; 21296/1125, \\
m = 2: &\quad \binom{4}{2} \tau^2 / (81 \cdot 2! \cdot 3!)
 \;=\; 6 \cdot (484/25) / 972 \;=\; 2904 / 24300
 \;=\; 242/2025, \\
m = 3: &\quad \binom{4}{3} \tau / (729 \cdot 3! \cdot 4!)
 \;=\; 4 \cdot (22/5) / 104976 \;=\; 88/524880
 \;=\; 11/65610, \\
m = 4: &\quad \binom{4}{4} \tau^0 / (6561 \cdot 4! \cdot 5!)
 \;=\; 1 / 18895680.
\end{align*}
The sign $(-1)^{K+1} = (-1)^{6} = +1$, giving the stated formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[\label{rem:tier-5-numerical-signature}%
Tier-5 signature at characteristic $r$]
Evaluating \eqref{eq:tier-5-explicit} at representative $r$:
\begin{align*}
r = 4: &\quad \mathrm{Epsilon}_4/A_4 \;=\; (22/5)^4 \;=\; 234256/625. \\
r = 8: &\quad \mathrm{Epsilon}_8/A_8 \;=\; \tfrac{234256}{625}
 + \tfrac{21296 \cdot 12}{1125} + \tfrac{242 \cdot 24}{2025}
 \;=\; 10206592/16875. \\
r = 12: &\quad \mathrm{Epsilon}_{12}/A_{12} \;=\; 6721045886/4100625.
\end{align*}
The Tier-5 denominator pattern $D_5 = 9^4 \cdot 4! \cdot 5! = 6561
\cdot 24 \cdot 120 = 18895680 = 2^6 \cdot 3^{10} \cdot 5$ introduces
the prime $5$ to the leading denominator sequence for the first
time; all prior Tier leading denominators $D_1, D_2, D_3, D_4$ were
$\{2, 3\}$-smooth.
\end{remark}

\section{Arithmetic consequences}

\begin{corollary}[\label{cor:tier-leading-denominator-pattern}%
Tier leading denominator pattern]
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
The leading (highest-polynomial-degree) denominator of
$\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}K(r)/A_r$ is
\begin{equation}
D_K \;=\; 9^{K-1} \, (K-1)! \, K!.
\end{equation}
The first five values are
$D_1 = 1$, $D_2 = 18$, $D_3 = 972$, $D_4 = 104976$, $D_5 = 18895680$.
Explicit factorisations:
\begin{align*}
D_2 &= 2 \cdot 3^2 = 18, \\
D_3 &= 2^2 \cdot 3^5 = 972, \\
D_4 &= 2^4 \cdot 3^8 = 104976, \\
D_5 &= 2^6 \cdot 3^{10} \cdot 5 = 18895680, \\
D_6 &= 2^7 \cdot 3^{13} \cdot 5^2 = 5101833600, \\
D_7 &= 2^8 \cdot 3^{16} \cdot 5^2 \cdot 7 = 1928493100800.
\end{align*}
The first prime $\ge 5$ appearing in the $D_K$ sequence is $p = 5$, at
$K = 5$ (via $K! = 120 = 2^3 \cdot 3 \cdot 5$). The first prime $\ge 7$
appears at $K = 7$. More generally, for every prime $p \ge 5$, $p$
first appears in $D_K$ at $K = p$, a direct consequence of
$v_p(K!) \ge 1 \Leftrightarrow K \ge p$ (the primes $p \in \{2, 3\}$
are already present in $D_2 = 18 = 2 \cdot 3^2$).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The denominator pattern follows from
Corollary~\ref{cor:tier-k-leading-coefficient}.  The displayed
factorisations are the prime decompositions of
$9^{K-1}(K-1)!K!$ for $2\le K\le7$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[\label{cor:tier-K-kummer-arithmetic}%
Denominator support and characteristic numerator primes]
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
For every fixed integer $r\ge4$ and every $K\ge1$,
\[
M_K\cdot \frac{\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}K(r)}{A_r}\in\mathbb{Z},
\qquad
M_K:=5^{K-1}\,9^{K-1}\,(K-1)!\,K!.
\]
Thus every denominator prime of
$\mathrm{Tier}\text{-}K(r)/A_r$ is a prime divisor of $M_K$.  There is
no analogous support restriction on the numerator: new characteristic
primes can be created by the integer sum over $m$.

The four-tier range already shows this numerator phenomenon:
\[
\frac{\Gamma_8}{A_8}
=\frac{484}{25}+\frac{88}{15}+\frac{2}{81}
=\frac{39204+11880+50}{2025}
=\frac{2\cdot37\cdot691}{3^4\cdot5^2}.
\]
The prime $691$ does not divide the falling-factorial window
$(8-4)_4=24$; it is produced by the numerator sum.  At Tier~5 and
$r=12$ one obtains
\[
\frac{\mathrm{Epsilon}_{12}}{A_{12}}
=\frac{6721045886}{4100625},
\qquad
4100625=3^8\cdot5^4,
\]
so the denominator contains no factor $13$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Multiplying the $m$th summand of \eqref{eq:tier-K-closed-form} by
$M_K$ gives
\[
(-1)^{K+1}\binom{K-1}{m}
22^{K-1-m}5^m9^{K-1-m}
\frac{(K-1)!}{m!}\frac{K!}{(m+1)!}(r-4)_{2m},
\]
an integer.  Summing over $m$ proves the denominator assertion.  The
displayed Tier~3 and Tier~5 values follow by substitution in
\eqref{eq:tier-K-closed-form}; they show why numerator primes require
separate arithmetic analysis.
\end{proof}

\section{Fuchsian ODE satisfied by the generating function}

The rationality of $F(r, u)$ established in
Theorem~\ref{thm:all-tier-bivariate-generating-function} is refined here
to a \emph{Fuchsian} second-order linear ordinary differential equation
in $u$, with polynomial coefficients in $(r, u)$ and four regular
singular points on $\mathbb{P}^1_u$.  The operator records the same
finite Catalan solution as a Frobenius solution of a pulled-back Gauss
equation; its fourth singularity is apparent for the rational solution
selected by $F(r,0)=1$.

\begin{theorem}[\label{thm:all-tier-fuchsian-ode}%
Fuchsian ODE in $u$ for the bivariate generating function]
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
For every integer $r \ge 4$, the bivariate generating function
$F(r, u) = \Phi_r(1/u)/A_r$ of
Definition~\ref{def:bivariate-F} satisfies the second-order linear ODE
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fuchsian-ode}
L_r[F](r, u) \;=\; 0,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fuchsian-ode-expanded}
\begin{aligned}
L_r \;=\;
 & -u\,(22u + 5)^{2}\,(218u + 45)\,\partial_u^{2} \\
 & + 2\,(22u + 5)\,\bigl[50\, r\, u - 9592\, u^{2} - 3245\, u - 225\bigr]\,
 \partial_u \\
 & - \bigl[125\, r^{2} - 2200\, r\, u - 1125\, r + 211024\, u^{2} + 99220\, u + 12400\bigr].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The ODE is Fuchsian on $\mathbb{P}^{1}_{u}$ with regular singular points
at the four loci
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fuchsian-sing-loci}
u \;\in\; \Bigl\{\, 0,\; -\tfrac{5}{22},\; -\tfrac{45}{218},\; \infty \,\Bigr\}
\end{equation}
for every $r \ge 4$.  After the gauge transformation
$G=(1+\tau u)F$ and the M\"obius coordinate
$x = -4u/(9(1 + \tau u))$, the equation reduces to the Gauss
hypergeometric equation with parameters
$a = (4-r)/2$, $b = (5-r)/2$, $c_{\mathrm{G}} = 2$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:all-tier-bivariate-generating-function},
$F(r, u) = G(r, u)/(1 + \tau u)$ with $G(r, u) = {}_2F_1(a, b; 2; x)$,
$a = (4-r)/2$, $b = (5-r)/2$, $x = -4u/(9(1 + \tau u))$. The function $G$
satisfies the Gauss hypergeometric equation in $x$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gauss-ode-x}
x(1 - x)\,\partial_x^{2} G + \bigl[2 - (a + b + 1) x\bigr]\,\partial_x G
 - a b\, G \;=\; 0.
\end{equation}
Here
\[
a+b+1=\frac{11-2r}{2},\qquad
ab=\frac{(r-4)(r-5)}{4}.
\]
The change of variables has
\begin{equation}
\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}u}
 \;=\; \frac{-4(1 + \tau u) + 4u \tau}{9(1 + \tau u)^{2}}
 \;=\; \frac{-4}{9(1 + \tau u)^{2}},
\end{equation}
and note that $1 - x = (9 + 9\tau u + 4u)/(9(1 + \tau u)) = (45 + 218u)/
(45(1 + \tau u))$ using $9\tau = 198/5$, so $45 + 218u = 9\cdot 5 +
(9\cdot 22 + 20) u = 45 + 198 u + 20 u$. The factor $218u + 45$
is therefore precisely $45(1 - x)(1 + \tau u)$ cleared of its $u$-pole
at $u = -5/22$.

Direct substitution of $G = F (1 + \tau u)$,
$\partial_u G = F'(1 + \tau u) + F \tau$,
$\partial_u^{2} G = F''(1 + \tau u) + 2 F' \tau$ into the displayed
Gauss equation and multiplication by $9 (1 + \tau u)^{3} / [(1 + \tau u) \cdot
(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}u)^{2}]$ gives exactly
\eqref{eq:fuchsian-ode-expanded}.  This is an identity in
\(\mathbb{Q}[r,u,\partial_u]\), not a finite check at selected $r$.

For the Fuchsian assertion, write the operator as
$P_2(u)\partial_u^2+P_1(u)\partial_u+P_0(u)$ with
$P_2=-u(22u+5)^2(218u+45)$.  At $u=0$, $u=-45/218$, and
$u=-5/22$, the quotients $P_1/P_2$ and $P_0/P_2$ have poles of order
at most $1$ and $2$, respectively.  At $u=\infty$,
$P_1/P_2=O(u^{-1})$ and $P_0/P_2=O(u^{-2})$.  These are precisely
Fuchs's regular-singularity conditions on $\mathbb{P}^1_u$.

The points $u=0$, $u=-45/218$, and $u=-5/22=-1/\tau$ are the
preimages of the Gauss points $x=0$, $x=1$, and $x=\infty$.
The point $u=\infty$ maps to the ordinary Gauss point
$x=-4/(9\tau)=-10/99$ and is an apparent regular singularity of the
gauged equation for $F$; in the coordinate $v=1/u$ its indicial
exponents are $1$ and $2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[\label{rem:fuchsian-heun-comparison}%
Comparison with the Heun equation]
An ODE with four regular singular points is classically a Heun equation.
The operator \eqref{eq:fuchsian-ode-expanded} is a pullback-and-gauge
of Gauss, not a generic Heun operator.  The three Gauss singularities
have preimages $u=0$, $u=-45/218$, and $u=-5/22$; the remaining point
$u=\infty$ is apparent for the gauged equation.  For the selected
terminating solution $F(r,u)$, $u=-45/218$ and $u=\infty$ are regular
points of the rational function, while $u=-5/22$ is the
Zamolodchikov pole.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[\label{cor:three-disjoint-hz-iv-chains}%
Three derivations of the scalar formula]
\ClaimStatusProvedHere
Theorem~\ref{thm:all-tier-laurent-stratification} has three compatible
derivations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(A)] \textbf{Master Riccati.} The scalar bilinear recurrence
displayed in the proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:all-tier-bivariate-generating-function}, after the

\item[(B)] \textbf{Hypergeometric Laurent extraction.} The explicit

$(r-4)_{2m}^{\downarrow} = 4^m (a)_m^{\uparrow} (b)_m^{\uparrow}$ and the

\item[(C)] \textbf{Fuchsian ODE.} The second-order linear ODE

\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}

\end{proof}

\section{Scalar boundary and analytic radius}

\begin{remark}[\label{rem:borel-analytic-continuation}%
Taylor radius at the Zamolodchikov pole]
The bivariate generating function $F(r, u)$ of
Theorem~\ref{thm:all-tier-bivariate-generating-function} is \emph{rational}
in $u$ for every integer $r$, but its Taylor expansion at $u=0$ does
not have infinite radius.  The pole at
$u=-1/\tau=-5/22$ is the $c=-22/5$ Zamolodchikov pole, so the fixed-$r$
Laurent series in $1/c$ has radius $5/22$.  Clearing the factor
$(1+\tau u)^{\lfloor(r-4)/2\rfloor+1}$ produces a polynomial
$P_r(u)$; it does not change the analytic radius of $F(r,u)$ itself.
Thus no fixed-$r$ Borel summation problem is present.  The large-$r$
asymptotic regime is a different question governed by the
shadow-exponential base $C_{\Vir}=6$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[\label{rem:hypergeometric-contiguous-relation}%
Contiguous relations and the Catalan recurrence]
Gauss contiguous relations for the shift $r\mapsto r+1$ are another
language for the same scalar recurrence.  In the shifted index
$n=r-4$, the recurrence is
\[
H_n=H_{n-1}-\frac{u}{9}\sum_{p+q=n-2}H_pH_q,
\qquad H_0=H_1=(1+\tau u)^{-1}.
\]
The ordinary generating function
\[
R(t,u)=\frac{(1+\tau u)^{-1}}{1-t}\,
C\!\left(-\frac{u t^2}{9(1+\tau u)(1-t)^2}\right)
\]
is the exact contiguous object; expanding the Catalan series gives
the Gauss polynomial in \eqref{eq:F-hyper}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[\label{rem:bivariate-generalisation-W-N}%
Class-$M$ boundary: Virasoro versus multi-weight $\mathcal{W}_N$]
The class label $\mathsf{M}$ records infinite shadow depth, not the
existence of a one-channel hypergeometric formula.  The Virasoro
family is the unique principal $\mathcal{W}_N$ family with $N=2$ and a
single strong generator; its cross-channel correction is identically
zero.  For $N\ge3$, principal $\mathcal{W}_N$ has generators of
weights $2,3,\ldots,N$.  Its scalar diagonal genus term is
\[
F_g^{\mathrm{diag}}(\mathcal{W}_N)
=c(H_N-1)\lambda_g^{\mathrm{FP}},
\]
and its complementarity constant is
\[
\kappa+\kappa^!
=2(N-1)(2N^2+2N+1)(H_N-1).
\]
This gives $13$ at $N=2$ and $250/3$ at $N=3$.  For interacting
multi-weight families the full genus expansion is
$F_g^{\mathrm{diag}}+\delta F_g^{\mathrm{cross}}$ for $g\ge2$; the
mixed $T$--$W$ and higher-spin channels are outside the scalar
Virasoro theorem proved here.
\end{remark}
